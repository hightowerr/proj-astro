# Reasoning File 1: Incentives

This document applies the mental model "Incentives" to the conflict between the Product and Engineering managers regarding development philosophy.

## Analysis using Thinking Steps

### 1. Identify the Desired Outcome

The desired outcome is for the Engineering Manager (EM) to willingly allocate engineering resources to iterative A/B testing, viewing it as a valuable activity rather than a distraction from the "real work."

### 2. Analyze the Current Incentives

The core of the problem lies in a direct conflict between the incentives of the two parties:

*   **The Engineering Manager's Incentives:** You've stated she is measured on **"hitting large project deadlines"** and **"completing previously started work."** Her world is optimized for predictability, stability, and closing out committed work (like the "Quick checkout project"). From this perspective, A/B tests are a threat: they are *new* work, they are *unpredictable* (they might fail), and they consume resources that could be used to ensure her primary, committed projects are delivered on time.

*   **Your (the PM's) Incentives:** Your ambition is to be **"OKR driven,"** meaning you are incentivized by achieving *outcomes* (e.g., increase conversion). You are also pressured by the need to be **"perceived to be delivering"** to leadership. A/B tests are your primary tool for both learning how to hit the OKR and demonstrating iterative progress.

**Conclusion:** Under the current structure, you are in a zero-sum game. For you to get resources for your goal, she has to risk failing at hers. Her pushback is a completely rational response to the incentives governing her role.

### 3. Design Aligned Incentives

Since you cannot change the EM's formal incentives, you must reframe the work so that it aligns with them. The goal is to turn the zero-sum game into a positive-sum partnership. The most effective way to do this is to make the resource allocation explicit and predictable.

**Proposed Solution: The 70/30 Resource Budget**

Propose a formal agreement with the EM and leadership to explicitly budget the team's capacity. For example:

*   **70% of Capacity (The "Commitment" Budget):** This portion is dedicated entirely to the EM's primary goal: delivering the committed roadmap of large projects like "Quick checkout." This protects her and guarantees progress on her key incentives.
*   **30% of Capacity (The "Discovery" Budget):** This portion is dedicated entirely to your primary goal: running A/B tests and other iterative experiments to find ways to move the needle on your OKRs.

This simple allocation changes the dynamic entirely. You are no longer "stealing" resources from her projects; you are simply spending your own pre-approved budget. Her incentive is no longer to block you, but to partner with you to ensure both budgets are managed effectively.

### 4. Stress-Test for Unintended Consequences

*   **Risk:** The 30% "Discovery" budget could be seen as "fluff" and be the first thing cut when a large project runs into trouble.
*   **Mitigation:** Both you and the EM must present this 70/30 plan to leadership as a unified strategy. The 30% budget must have its own clear, lightweight roadmap of hypotheses to test, and you must be accountable for reporting on the learnings (both successes and failures) generated from it. This frames the 30% as equally important and accountable as the 70%.

By making the incentives and trade-offs explicit, you can move from a relationship of conflict to one of co-ownership over a shared portfolio of work.

# Analysis Report: Aligning Product and Engineering on a Shared Development Philosophy

## Executive Summary

The current conflict with your Engineering Manager (EM) is not a personal disagreement but a systemic issue caused by misaligned incentives and a flawed team strategy. Your EM is incentivized to deliver large, committed projects on time, making your desire for iterative A/B testing feel like a direct threat to her success. This has placed the team in an unwinnable **"Two-Front War"**—fighting to deliver committed projects while simultaneously trying to do discovery work—leading to resource contention and unclear priorities.

The path forward is not to "win" the argument, but to reframe the problem and propose a new, cooperative system. **The core recommendation is to formally propose a 70/30 split of the team's capacity.** 70% of resources will be dedicated to the EM's goal of delivering the committed roadmap, while 30% will be explicitly budgeted for your goal of OKR-driven discovery and A/B testing.

This solution changes the game from a zero-sum competition for resources into a positive-sum partnership. It provides the EM with the predictability she needs, gives you the dedicated capacity for learning you need, and provides a clear, rational framework for making strategic **trade-offs**.

## Problem Statement

The Product Manager and Engineering Manager are misaligned on the team's development philosophy. The PM wants to increase velocity with more A/B tests and frequent releases to drive OKRs, while the EM is pushing back, concerned this prioritizes speed over delivering value on large, committed projects. This is causing friction, resource contention, and is blocking the team's ability to be both predictable and innovative.

## Individual Model Analysis

### Model 1: Incentives

*   **Rationale for Selection:** This model was chosen to diagnose the root cause of the EM's behavior.
*   **Analysis & Findings:** The EM's pushback is a rational response to her incentives, which reward delivering large, committed projects on time. A/B tests, being uncertain and new work, directly threaten her ability to meet these goals. The conflict is a natural outcome of a system where both leaders have opposing, unaligned incentives.

### Model 2: Two-Front War

*   **Rationale for Selection:** This model provides a powerful, neutral metaphor to describe the team's current, painful state.
*   **Analysis & Findings:** The team is being asked to fight on two fronts: delivering committed projects and doing discovery work. This explains the "issues of resource and unclear priorities." It proves that the current system is unsustainable and inefficient, creating a shared motivation to find a new strategy.

### Model 3: Trade-offs & Opportunity Cost

*   **Rationale for Selection:** This model provides the practical language for negotiating a solution.
*   **Analysis & Findings:** By framing the discussion around explicit trade-offs, the debate becomes economic rather than emotional. The "opportunity cost" of not doing A/B tests is the high risk of building the wrong thing in the future. The "opportunity cost" of a 70/30 split is a predictable, acceptable slowdown in current large projects. This makes the trade-off clear and justifiable.

## Synthesis & Integrated Insights

The three models provide a clear, unified narrative and action plan:

1.  **Incentives** explains *why* the conflict is happening. It's not personal; it's systemic. The EM is not being difficult; she is acting rationally based on her goals. This insight allows you to approach the conversation with empathy instead of frustration.
2.  **Two-Front War** gives you a shared, non-confrontational language to describe *why the current situation is failing for both of you*. It turns you from adversaries into allies facing a common enemy: an inefficient system.
3.  **Trade-offs & Opportunity Cost** provides the specific mechanism for *how to solve the problem*. It allows you to propose the 70/30 split not as a demand, but as the most rational portfolio allocation for the business, with clear costs and benefits.

The integrated insight is that you must stop arguing about the merits of A/B testing and instead propose a new system that realigns your incentives with your EM's. The goal is to give her the predictability she needs to succeed, in exchange for the dedicated capacity you need to succeed.

## Actionable Options & Recommendations

### Recommendation: Propose the 70/30 "Portfolio" Model

Schedule a meeting with your Engineering Manager (and potentially your shared manager) with the explicit goal of "defining a clearer resource allocation framework for the team."

**The Pitch:**

1.  **Acknowledge the Shared Problem (Two-Front War):** "I've been thinking about the tension around the 'Quick checkout project' and our A/B tests. I feel like we're asking the team to fight a war on two fronts, and it's causing unclear priorities and stretching everyone thin. It's not working for either of us."

2.  **Validate Her Incentive (Incentives):** "I know your primary goal is to deliver our committed projects on time, and my requests for testing resources have put that at risk. That's a failure in our current process."

3.  **Propose the New Framework (Trade-offs):** "I'd like to propose a new model where we treat our team's capacity like a portfolio. We could formally allocate 70% of our capacity to the committed roadmap, which gives you total predictability for those projects. The other 30% would be a dedicated, separate budget for discovery work and A/B tests to make sure we're hitting our OKRs. The trade-off is that the committed projects will move slightly slower, but the opportunity cost of *not* doing this is that we risk building the wrong things in the future. This feels like a trade-off worth making for the health of the business and the team."

4.  **Define the Partnership:** "My responsibility would be to manage the 30% budget and be accountable for the learnings we generate. Your responsibility would be to manage the 70% budget and deliver the roadmap. Together, we would be co-owners of this portfolio, reporting on both parts to leadership."

This approach changes the conversation from a conflict over resources to a partnership in strategic planning.

# Reasoning File 2: Two-Front War

This document applies the mental model "Two-Front War" to the team's conflicting priorities.

## Analysis using Thinking Steps

### 1. Identify Your Fronts

The team is currently fighting a classic, unwinnable two-front war:

*   **Front 1: The War of Commitment.** This is the battle to deliver large, committed projects with fixed deadlines, such as the "Quick checkout project." The enemy is time and complexity. Success is measured by shipping the feature on schedule. This is the front the Engineering Manager is forced to prioritize due to her incentives.

*   **Front 2: The War of Discovery.** This is the battle to achieve outcome-based goals (OKRs), such as increasing conversion. The enemy is uncertainty. Success is measured by moving a metric, which requires experimentation, learning, and iteration (A/B tests). This is the front you, the Product Manager, are championing.

### 2. Assess the Resource Drain

Your description of "issues of resource and unclear priorities" is the textbook symptom of a two-front war. When a single army (your engineering team) is asked to fight on two different fronts at once, it is guaranteed to be less effective on both. 

*   **Cognitive Overhead:** Engineers are forced to context-switch between deep, planned work on the large project and quick, iterative work on experiments. This kills productivity.
*   **Planning Failure:** It becomes impossible for the EM to create a predictable project plan because she cannot know when her resources will be pulled away to fight on the "Discovery" front. This directly threatens her primary incentive.
*   **Morale:** The team is caught in the middle of a strategic conflict, leading to frustration and a sense of being pulled in two directions at once.

### 3. Seek to Close a Front

In this business context, you cannot simply abandon one front. The business needs both the delivery of committed projects and the achievement of its strategic OKRs. 

The solution is not to close a front, but to **stop fighting them with the same army at the same time.** The current strategy is to have 100% of the team context-switching between both wars, which is inefficient.

### 4. The Strategic Truce: A Deliberate Allocation

The 70/30 resource split proposed in the "Incentives" analysis is the strategic answer to the two-front war. It is a deliberate decision to divide your forces and assign them to a single front.

*   **The "Commitment" Army (70% of the team):** This force is now fighting on only one front. They have a clear objective: deliver the "Quick checkout project." They can plan effectively, focus deeply, and their success is measured by hitting the deadline. The EM's primary goal is now secure.

*   **The "Discovery" Army (30% of the team):** This force is also fighting on only one front. Their objective is clear: run experiments to move the OKR metric. Their success is measured by learning and iteration. Your primary goal is now resourced.

By framing the problem in this way, you are not arguing that your front is more important than hers. You are making a strategic argument that dividing the army is the only way for the team to win on *both* fronts.

# Mental Model Selection Report

## 1. Selection Funnel

From an initial list of over 50 potential models, a candidate list of 5 was created based on the problem diagnosis of strategic misalignment. After a deep-dive review, a final selection of the 3 most impactful models was made.

## 2. Final Ranked Selection

1.  **Incentives**
2.  **Two-Front War**
3.  **Trade-offs & Opportunity Cost**

## 3. Rationale for Selection

### 1. Incentives

*   **Reason for Selection:** This is the most critical model for diagnosing the root cause of the conflict. The Engineering Manager's pushback is a rational response to her incentives, which are to "hit large project deadlines" and "complete previously started work." Your desire to run A/B tests (new, uncertain work) is a direct threat to her ability to meet her goals. The problem cannot be solved without addressing this fundamental misalignment.
*   **Reference from Model:** The selection is guided by the core principle: "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." The EM's behavior is a predictable outcome of her current incentive structure.

### 2. Two-Front War

*   **Reason for Selection:** This model provides a powerful, non-confrontational metaphor for the team's current state. They are being forced to fight a war on two fronts: delivering large, committed projects (the "Quick checkout project") and iterating on OKRs via A/B tests. This explains the "issues of resource and unclear priorities." Framing the problem this way elevates the conversation from a personal disagreement to a strategic discussion about unsustainable resource allocation.
*   **Reference from Model:** The selection is based on the model's `Thinking Steps`, particularly `Step 1: Identify Your Fronts` and `Step 2: Assess the Resource Drain`. This provides a clear language for articulating the current pain.

### 3. Trade-offs & Opportunity Cost

*   **Reason for Selection:** Once the problem is framed as a "Two-Front War" driven by misaligned incentives, this model provides the practical tool for resolution. It moves the debate from "who is right" to "what is the explicit trade-off we are making?" It allows you to have a rational discussion about resource allocation, for example: "What is the opportunity cost of *not* running A/B tests for a quarter? We ship Project X, but we learn nothing about conversion and risk Project Y being a failure."
*   **Reference from Model:** This choice is guided by the model's `Keywords for Situations` (prioritization, strategic planning) and its core concept of making the value of the path *not taken* explicit.

# Reasoning File 3: Trade-offs & Opportunity Cost

This document applies the mental model "Trade-offs & Opportunity Cost" to create a framework for the specific conversation with the Engineering Manager.

## Analysis using Thinking Steps

### 1. Identify the Core Decision

The core decision is not "Should we do more A/B tests?" The real decision is: **"How should we allocate our team's finite engineering capacity between delivering committed projects and discovering what moves our OKRs?"**

Framing it this way moves the conversation from a tactical disagreement to a strategic portfolio discussion.

### 2. List All Viable Options

Using this framing, you can present the situation as a clear choice between three options:

*   **Option A (The "Project-Driven" Model):** Allocate 100% of capacity to committed projects like "Quick checkout."
*   **Option B (The Current "Two-Front War" Model):** Allocate resources fluidly, leading to context switching, unclear priorities, and conflict.
*   **Option C (The "Hybrid/Portfolio" Model):** Formally budget capacity between the two goals (e.g., a 70/30 split).

### 3. & 4. Define Gains and Identify Opportunity Costs

This is the most critical step. By making the costs explicit, you can have a rational debate.

*   **Analysis of Option A (100% Projects):**
    *   **Gains:** Maximizes predictability and the likelihood of hitting deadlines for committed projects. This directly serves the EM's primary incentive.
    *   **Opportunity Cost:** The team learns nothing about what actually drives the OKR. The **opportunity cost is future value and risk reduction.** You accept a high risk that the *next* big project will be a failure because it wasn't informed by iterative learning.

*   **Analysis of Option B (Fluid Mix - The Status Quo):**
    *   **Gains:** Some work gets done on both fronts, but inefficiently.
    *   **Opportunity Cost:** The cost is **team velocity and morale.** The constant context-switching, friction between leaders, and lack of clear priorities makes this the most expensive option. You get the worst of both worlds: the large projects are delayed by distractions, and the experiments are not run with enough focus to be effective.

*   **Analysis of Option C (70/30 Split):**
    *   **Gains:** Predictable progress on committed projects (serving the EM's incentive). A dedicated, predictable budget for discovery and learning (serving your incentive). Team clarity and focus.
    *   **Opportunity Cost:** The committed projects will be completed more slowly than in Option A. This is the explicit, upfront trade-off. You are trading some delivery speed on the current large project for a massive increase in learning and a reduction in future risk.

### 5. & 6. Align with Priorities and Make a Deliberate Choice

This framework allows you to present the choice to the EM and leadership in a neutral, economic way. The conversation is no longer about your desire for A/B tests vs. her desire for stability. It is about which portfolio allocation strategy is best for the business.

The argument becomes:

> "We can continue with our current fluid approach (Option B), but we agree it's causing friction and inefficiency. We could go to 100% project focus (Option A), which feels safe but carries the hidden risk of building the wrong things in the future. I propose we choose Option C. We accept a calculated 30% slowdown on the 'Quick checkout' project as a trade-off for the enormous benefit of de-risking our future roadmap and ensuring we are building what actually moves our metrics. This seems like the most rational investment for the business."

This approach acknowledges her priorities, validates her concerns, and presents your proposal not as a threat, but as the most logical solution to the shared pain of the "Two-Front War."

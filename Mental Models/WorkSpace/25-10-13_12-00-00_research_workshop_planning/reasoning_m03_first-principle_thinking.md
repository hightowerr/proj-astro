# First-Principle Thinking Analysis for Research Workshop Planning

This document applies the First-Principle Thinking model to deconstruct the purpose of the research workshop and build a more effective plan from the ground up.

## 1. The Goal

To design a workshop that generates the most critical research questions for the basket redesign.

## 2. Deconstruction of the Problem

- **What is a “basket” in e-commerce?** A temporary holding place for items a user is considering buying.
- **What is its fundamental purpose?** To convert a user's *intent* to purchase into a *completed* purchase with minimal friction.
- **Why do we need a “workshop”?** To create a shared understanding and alignment between different teams (Trading, Product, Design) who have different perspectives and knowledge.
- **What is a “research question”?** A specific inquiry designed to reduce uncertainty and risk in the design process.

## 3. Challenging Assumptions

- **Assumption:** The basket *must* have recommendations and insurance offers.
  - **Challenge:** Does it? What is the fundamental trade-off between helping the business (cross-selling) and helping the user (reducing friction)? This is the core of the “clutter” debate.
- **Assumption:** The workshop output must be a list of questions.
  - **Challenge:** Is a list of questions the most valuable output, or is it a *shared, prioritized understanding of the problems*? The questions are a tool to achieve that understanding.
- **Assumption:** We are starting from “no data.”
  - **Challenge:** This is false. We have the existing basket itself, access to analytics, and the collective experience and biases of everyone in the room. The workshop's job is to structure and surface this implicit data.

## 4. Reconstructing the Workshop from First Principles

The workshop's primary purpose is not just to generate a list of questions, but to build a **shared understanding of what currently helps or hinders the basket's core job of converting intent into a completed purchase.**

Therefore, the workshop should be designed around answering one fundamental question: **“What are the biggest sources of friction and confidence in our current basket experience?”**

## 5. Optimized Workshop Design

This leads to a more effective workshop structure:

1.  **Activity 1: Agree on the Basket's Core Job.** Start the workshop by getting all participants to agree on a first-principles definition of the basket's purpose. (e.g., “To help users confidently and easily complete their intended purchase.”)
2.  **Activity 2: “Friction & Confidence” Mapping.** Each participant silently brainstorms on sticky notes all the moments in the current basket journey that create **friction** (e.g., “I can’t see the delivery cost,” “I’m being shown things I don’t want”) and moments that build **confidence** (e.g., “It’s easy to change the quantity,” “The return policy is clear”).
3.  **Activity 3: Cluster and Theme.** As a group, cluster the sticky notes into common themes. This will visually represent the collective understanding of the basket's current strengths and weaknesses. (e.g., “Clarity of Costs,” “Ease of Editing,” “Trust & Security,” “Unwanted Distractions”).
4.  **Activity 4: Frame Themes as “How Might We…” Questions.** Convert the most critical themes (especially the friction clusters) into actionable “How Might We…” (HMW) questions. For example, the “Unwanted Distractions” cluster becomes, “How might we surface relevant offers without creating a sense of clutter or pressure?”
5.  **Activity 5: Prioritize the HMW Questions.** Use a simple dot-voting exercise or a 2x2 matrix (e.g., User Impact vs. Business Impact) to prioritize the HMWs. The top 1-3 HMWs become the core focus for the subsequent research plan.

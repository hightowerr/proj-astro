# Inversion Analysis for Research Workshop Planning

This document applies the Inversion mental model to identify potential failure points in the planned research question–gathering workshop and create a set of avoidance strategies.

## 1. The Goal

To run a successful research question–gathering workshop that produces a clear, prioritized list of actionable research questions for the Basket Redesign project, with full stakeholder alignment.

## 2. Inverted Problem: How to Guarantee Failure?

- The workshop produces ambiguous, un-prioritized, or unactionable research questions.
- Key stakeholders leave the workshop misaligned and more confused than when they started.
- The session is a waste of valuable pre-peak time and delays the entire redesign project.
- The research questions we generate are fundamentally wrong, leading the project in a direction that doesn’t solve real user problems or meet business goals.

## 3. Brainstorming Failure Points

- **Wrong People:** The room is missing key decision-makers from Trading, Product, or Design, or is crowded with people who have only a tangential stake, leading to scope creep and irrelevant discussions.
- **No Structure:** The workshop lacks a clear agenda, facilitated exercises, or time management. It becomes a chaotic and circular conversation.
- **Lack of Focus:** The discussion gets derailed by topics outside the basket redesign (e.g., checkout issues, general company strategy).
- **Groupthink:** Participants anchor on the first few ideas or are hesitant to challenge the loudest voice in the room, leading to a superficial consensus on unimportant questions.
- **Ignoring the Elephant in the Room:** The known disagreement about the basket being “too cluttered” with recommendations and insurance offers is avoided, leading to a failure to address the core tension.
- **Analysis Paralysis:** The group gets bogged down in semantic debates over question wording and fails to move on to prioritization.
- **No Clear Outcome:** The workshop ends without a tangible, agreed-upon list of prioritized questions and clear next steps, causing the generated momentum to dissipate.

## 4. Avoidance List & Mitigation Strategies

To prevent these failures, we will implement the following rules:

1.  **Curate the Attendee List:** The invitation list will be strictly limited to the core participants from Trading, Product, and Design. We will schedule separate, brief pre-interviews with any other relevant stakeholders to gather their input beforehand.
2.  **Structure the Agenda Rigorously:** The UX research team will design a detailed agenda with specific time-boxed activities (e.g., silent brainstorming, dot voting, impact/effort mapping) and will actively facilitate the session to keep it on track.
3.  **Frame the Problem & Use a “Parking Lot”:** The workshop will begin by explicitly stating the goal and scope. A “parking lot” will be used to table any off-topic discussions for a later time.
4.  **Mandate Individual Contribution First:** We will use techniques like silent brainstorming on sticky notes before any group discussion. This ensures all ideas are captured before being influenced by the group.
5.  **Address the “Clutter” Issue Head-On:** We will dedicate a specific agenda item to deconstruct the “clutter” issue. An exercise like “Pros, Cons, and Interesting Questions” regarding the current cross-sells can help structure this sensitive conversation.
6.  **Force Prioritization:** The final activity will be a structured prioritization exercise (e.g., a 2x2 matrix of “Impact vs. Feasibility to Answer”) to force a ranked order on the generated questions.
7.  **Define and Assign Next Steps:** The workshop will conclude with a summary of the prioritized questions and a clear assignment of who is responsible for turning them into a formal research plan and by when.

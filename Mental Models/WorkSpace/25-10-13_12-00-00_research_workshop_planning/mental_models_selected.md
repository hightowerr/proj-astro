# Mental Models Selected for Workshop Planning

This document outlines the mental models selected to structure the planning of the research question–gathering workshop.

## Selection Funnel

From an initial list of over 90 candidate models, a deeper evaluation was performed on a shortlist of 8 models related to problem-solving, stakeholder management, and avoiding bias. From this, the final 3 were selected for their complementary strengths in tackling this specific challenge.

## Final Selection & Rationale

### 1. Inversion (m07)

*   **Rationale:** The primary goal is to identify pain points. By inverting the problem and asking, “What would make this the worst possible workshop?” or “How could we guarantee we get the research questions wrong?”, we can proactively identify risks, hidden assumptions, and potential points of failure. This is especially useful given the known disagreement about basket “clutter.”

### 2. First-Principle Thinking (m03)

*   **Rationale:** The problem states we are starting from a blank slate with no existing data. First-Principle Thinking is the ideal tool for this scenario. It will force us to break down the basket to its absolute fundamentals: What is a basket for? What must it do? This approach will help us build up a set of core research questions that are not biased by the current implementation or pre-existing opinions.

### 3. Bias from Incentives (m77)

*   **Rationale:** The questionnaire revealed a key conflict: the perception of clutter versus the business need for recommendations and attachments. This model is crucial for analyzing the underlying incentives of each stakeholder group (Trading, Product, Design). By understanding what drives each team, we can facilitate a more productive conversation and frame research questions that seek to find a balance between user experience and business objectives, rather than treating them as mutually exclusive.

# Analysis Report: Planning the Research Question–Gathering Workshop

## Executive Summary

This report provides a strategic plan for the upcoming Basket Redesign research workshop. By applying a latticework of three mental models—Inversion, First-Principle Thinking, and Bias from Incentives—we have developed a robust framework for a one-day workshop. The primary goal is to move beyond the “clutter” debate and produce a prioritized set of actionable research questions that align stakeholder incentives and de-risk the redesign project. The recommended approach is to structure the workshop around deconstructing the basket’s core purpose, mapping friction points, and reframing competing stakeholder narratives into a shared, data-driven objective.

## Problem Statement

The core challenge is to design and execute a highly effective, one-day workshop to define the critical research questions for the Basket Redesign project. This must be done efficiently before the peak retail season, with a diverse group of time-poor stakeholders (Trading, Product, Design), and without any prior formal research. The workshop must address the known disagreement regarding basket “clutter” versus cross-selling opportunities.

## Individual Model Analysis

### Model 1: Inversion

- **Rationale for Selection:** Chosen to proactively identify and mitigate risks that could cause the workshop to fail.
- **Analysis & Findings:** By inverting the problem and asking “How could we guarantee failure?”, we identified several key risks: a lack of structure, groupthink, stakeholder misalignment, and a failure to address the core “clutter” debate. This led to the creation of an “avoidance list,” which includes strategies like curating the attendee list, mandating individual brainstorming before group discussion, and using a neutral facilitator to address contentious topics head-on.

### Model 2: First-Principle Thinking

- **Rationale for Selection:** Chosen because we are starting with a “blank slate” and need to challenge ingrained assumptions about what a basket should be.
- **Analysis & Findings:** Deconstructing the basket to its fundamental purpose revealed its core job is to **convert a user's intent into a completed purchase with minimal friction.** This shifts the workshop’s primary goal from just “generating questions” to “building a shared understanding of what helps or hinders the basket’s core job.” This insight led to a proposed workshop structure based on mapping “friction and confidence” points and then converting those into “How Might We…” questions.

### Model 3: Bias from Incentives

- **Rationale for Selection:** Chosen to understand and manage the conflicting perspectives of the different stakeholder groups (Trading, Product, Design).
- **Analysis & Findings:** The analysis revealed predictable narratives: Trading is incentivized to maximize transaction value (supporting cross-sells), while Design is incentivized to create a frictionless user experience (opposing clutter). The Product team is caught in the middle. This model shows that the workshop cannot be about deciding who is “right.” Instead, it must be designed to **reframe the debate** from “clutter vs. clean” to “value vs. distraction” and to define research questions that can provide objective data on the trade-offs.

## Synthesis & Integrated Insights

The three models converge on a powerful, unified strategy:

1.  **Inversion** tells us *what to avoid*: a chaotic, biased, and inconclusive meeting. It gives us the guardrails for the workshop.
2.  **First-Principle Thinking** tells us *what to focus on*: the basket’s core job of converting intent. It gives us the fundamental structure for the workshop’s activities.
3.  **Bias from Incentives** tells us *how to manage the people*: by acknowledging their different goals and reframing the debate around objective, user-centric data. It gives us the facilitation strategy.

Together, they transform the workshop from a simple brainstorming session into a structured, three-act process: first, **de-risk** the session (Inversion); second, **focus** on the fundamentals (First Principles); and third, **align** the stakeholders (Incentives).

## Actionable Options & Recommendations

Based on this analysis, the following workshop agenda and principles are recommended:

**Recommended Workshop Agenda (1-Day):**

- **Morning Session 1 (Focus: First Principles):**
    - **(15 min) Align on the Core Job:** Start with a group exercise to agree on a first-principles definition of the basket's purpose.
    - **(60 min) Friction & Confidence Mapping:** Silent, individual brainstorming on the current basket journey, followed by group clustering and theming.
- **Morning Session 2 (Focus: Incentives & Reframing):**
    - **(45 min) Acknowledge the Incentives:** Openly discuss the different goals of each team (Trading, Product, Design) and frame the shared challenge.
    - **(45 min) Reframe “Clutter” to “Value vs. Distraction”:** Review the clustered friction points and reframe them through this new lens.
- **Afternoon Session (Focus: Inversion & Prioritization):**
    - **(60 min) “How Might We…” Generation:** Convert the top friction themes into actionable HMW questions.
    - **(30 min) Prioritization:** Use a 2x2 matrix (e.g., User Impact vs. Business Impact) to prioritize the HMWs. The top 3-5 become the core research questions.
    - **(15 min) Define Next Steps:** Assign clear ownership for turning the prioritized questions into a formal research plan.

**Key Recommendation:** The UX research team should act as a neutral facilitator, guiding the participants through these exercises and ensuring that the conversation remains productive and focused on the shared goal of understanding the user.

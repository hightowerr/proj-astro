# Bias from Incentives Analysis for Research Workshop Planning

This document applies the Bias from Incentives model to understand the perspectives and motivations of the workshop participants, which is key to navigating the “clutter” debate and ensuring a productive session.

## 1. The Key Players

Based on the questionnaire, the essential participants are from three core teams:

- **Trading**
- **Product**
- **Design**

## 2. Following the Incentives

- **Trading Team:**
  - **Incentive:** Primarily driven by revenue, margin, and achieving sales targets. Their goal is to maximize the value of every transaction.
  - **Predicted Narrative:** They will likely advocate for features that increase Average Order Value (AOV), such as recommendations, cross-sells (like insurance), and complex promotions. They may view a “clean” or minimalist basket as a wasted opportunity to sell.

- **Product Team:**
  - **Incentive:** Driven by a balance of user satisfaction (measured by conversion rate, retention, CSAT) and achieving business goals. They act as the bridge between user needs and business objectives.
  - **Predicted Narrative:** They will seek a “win-win” solution. They will be most interested in research questions that help quantify the trade-offs, such as, “What is the conversion impact of removing this module?”

- **Design Team:**
  - **Incentive:** Primarily driven by user-centricity, usability, and creating a frictionless, intuitive, and positive user experience. They are the primary advocates for the user.
  - **Predicted Narrative:** They will likely advocate for a cleaner, more streamlined basket, and will be skeptical of any element that adds visual “clutter” or cognitive load without providing clear user value.

## 3. Questioning the Narrative

The existing feedback that the basket is “too cluttered” and “forces” recommendations is a narrative driven by a user-centric perspective (Design). The counter-narrative, likely held by the Trading team, is that these modules are “helpful suggestions” and “valuable services” that drive business growth.

## 4. Inverting the Problem

- **What narrative would perfectly serve the Trading team’s incentives?** “The basket is a valuable piece of digital real estate that must be optimized to maximize revenue on every visit. Every empty space is a wasted opportunity.”
- **What narrative would perfectly serve the Design team’s incentives?** “The basket should be a sanctuary of calm and clarity, where the user can review their choices without distraction and proceed to payment with absolute confidence. Any element that does not directly contribute to this should be removed.”

## 5. Workshop Design Implications

Understanding these competing incentives is critical for designing a successful workshop. The goal is not to decide who is “right,” but to define research that will provide objective data to resolve the debate.

1.  **Acknowledge All Incentives:** The workshop facilitator should explicitly acknowledge the different (and equally valid) goals of each team. Frame the shared challenge as: “How can we maximize our business goals *while* creating a best-in-class user experience?”
2.  **Focus on Quantifiable Trade-offs:** The research questions generated must be framed to provide data on the trade-offs. Instead of asking, “*Is* the basket cluttered?” a better starting point is, “*At what point* do cross-sell recommendations start to negatively impact the conversion rate?”
3.  **Use a Neutral Facilitator:** The UX research team must act as a neutral party, ensuring all perspectives are heard and guiding the conversation back to the user’s point of view, which is the one perspective everyone is trying to understand.
4.  **Reframe the “Clutter” Debate:** The workshop should reframe the debate from “clutter vs. clean” to “value vs. distraction.” The research should aim to identify which elements users find valuable and which they find distracting or untrustworthy.

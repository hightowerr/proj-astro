# Reasoning via Occam's Razor

This document applies the Occam's Razor mental model to the Basket Redesign Dilemma. The goal is to "shave away" unnecessary complexity and find the simplest effective path forward.

## 1. The Phenomenon

You are stuck in "prioritization paralysis" and need a clear, actionable plan for the basket redesign.

## 2. Plausible, but Complex, Explanations/Solutions

One could approach this problem with a variety of complex, time-consuming solutions. These often feel productive but create drag in a low-engagement culture:

*   **The Heavyweight Scoring Model:** You could create a detailed spreadsheet with 10-15 weighted criteria (e.g., revenue impact, dev effort, strategic alignment, CSAT score), and then force every feature to be scored by multiple stakeholders. **(High number of assumptions: assumes you can get stakeholder time, that the weights are correct, and that the data for each score is accurate).**
*   **The Endless Workshop Tour:** You could try to schedule a series of multi-hour workshops with every conceivable stakeholder group (product, engineering, design, marketing, legal, finance) to get universal buy-in on the story map. **(High number of assumptions: assumes you can get everyone in a room, that they will all agree, and that this level of consensus is necessary).**
*   **The Perfect Data Quest:** You could halt all prioritization until you have statistically significant data from your analyst and the new UX researcher for every single idea on your story map. **(High number of assumptions: assumes the data can be gathered quickly and that it will provide a perfectly clear answer, removing the need for judgment).

## 3. Identifying the Simplest Explanation/Solution

Occam's Razor directs us to find the explanation with the fewest assumptions. What is the simplest possible process that could achieve your goal: a prioritized plan that is credible to your key stakeholders?

**The Simplest Process:**
1.  You, the product manager closest to the problem, make the first draft of the priorities using a simple 2x2 framework (Impact vs. Effort).
2.  You show this draft to the 2-3 people whose opinions are absolutely essential for credibility.
3.  You use their feedback to refine the draft into a proposed plan.

This process assumes only one thing: that a good-enough first draft, vetted by a small group of the right people, is sufficient to create a credible plan.

## 4. Testing the Simplest Explanation First

Instead of starting with the complex processes, we should start with the simplest one. The analysis from the **Leverage** model already points to this: using your existing 2x2 assessment and conducting a "15-minute tour" with your minimum viable stakeholders *is* the Occam's Razor approach in action.

It avoids the unnecessary complexity of large meetings and perfect data, focusing only on the essential components required to make a decision and move forward. This is the most direct path from your current state of anxiety to an actionable plan.

# Reasoning via Leverage

This document applies the Leverage mental model to the Basket Redesign Dilemma. The goal is to identify the highest-leverage activities to break the current paralysis.

## 1. Desired Outcome

The primary desired outcome is to **get customers to checkout as efficiently as possible.** The plan to achieve this must be clear, prioritized, and credible to key stakeholders.

## 2. Identifying the System's Fulcrum (The Point of Leverage)

In this system, there are two main points of leverage:

1.  **The User Story Map:** This is the tool you've already started. Its leverage comes from its ability to transform a cloud of ideas into a single, shared view of the work. Making this map useful is the fulcrum for the entire project.
2.  **The "Minimum Viable Stakeholders":** In a low-engagement culture, the attention of your manager (George), the Head of Product, and the Trading stakeholders is a scarce and powerful resource. A small amount of their time, applied correctly, provides the leverage needed for buy-in and credibility.

## 3. Brainstorming Levers (High-Leverage Actions)

Based on the fulcrum points, here are the highest-leverage actions you can take right now:

*   **Leverage Action 1: Focus the Story Map on the Goal.** Your story map is currently a collection of all ideas. The highest-leverage change is to restructure it. Instead of just listing features, **reframe every item on the map in the context of the primary goal.** For each item, ask: "How does this help get customers to checkout more efficiently?" If it doesn't, move it to a separate "Future Ideas" list. This single action will bring immediate clarity.

*   **Leverage Action 2: Use Your Preliminary 2x2 Analysis.** You have already done a rough cut of Effort vs. Impact. This is a high-leverage artifact. Formalize it slightly. Take your story map and physically (or digitally) tag each item as a "Quick Win," "Big Bet," "Maybe," or "Time Sink." This is the fastest way to make the map a decision-making tool.

*   **Leverage Action 3: A "15-Minute-Each" Stakeholder Tour.** Do not try to get all stakeholders in a room. Instead, use their time as a high-leverage tool. Schedule separate, 15-minute meetings with each of your three key stakeholders. The agenda is simple: "I have a draft of the priorities for the redesign, based on the goal of improving checkout efficiency. Can I quickly show you the 'Quick Wins' and 'Big Bets' I've identified and get your gut reaction?" This is a light-touch, high-impact way to get their input.

## 4. Assess the Upside and Downside

*   **Upside:** These actions will immediately break your paralysis. You will have a focused story map, a clear starting point for prioritization (Quick Wins), and initial buy-in from your key stakeholders. This creates momentum.
*   **Downside:** The initial prioritization will be based on your gut feel and limited data. It might not be perfect. However, the leverage here comes from making progress and getting feedback, not from being perfectly right the first time.

## 5. Apply with Measure

The recommendation is to **start with the "Quick Wins" you have identified.** These are your highest-leverage initial projects. They are low-effort, so the risk is low, but they are high-impact, so they will deliver visible results and build credibility for the larger "Big Bets."

By applying these high-leverage actions, you can transform your current state of anxiety into a clear, actionable plan in a very short amount of time.

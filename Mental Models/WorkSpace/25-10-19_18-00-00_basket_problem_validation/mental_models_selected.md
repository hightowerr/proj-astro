# Mental Models Selected for Basket Problem Validation

This document outlines the mental models selected to analyze the task of "Validate/refresh customer evidence for existing Basket problems."

## Selection Summary

From an initial list of potentially relevant models (including First-Principle Thinking, 5 Whys, Jobs to be Done, Confirmation Bias, Survivorship Bias), three have been selected to form the core of the analysis. The goal is to provide a robust framework for validating existing research and uncovering deep user insights.

---

## Final Ranked List & Rationale

### 1. Jobs to be Done (JTBD)

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The kickoff document highlights a need to solve "real user problems." JTBD is the ideal framework for this as it shifts the focus from the basket's features to what the customer is trying to *accomplish*. It helps answer the question: "What job did the user hire the basket to do?" This is foundational for validating whether the "existing problems" are actual problems or just symptoms of a deeper, unmet need.

### 2. Confirmation & Survivorship Bias

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The task is to "validate/refresh" existing evidence. This requires a critical, objective lens. These two biases are the most significant risks to this process. **Confirmation Bias** will challenge the team to look for evidence that *disproves* their workshop ideas, not just confirms them. **Survivorship Bias** will force an analysis of users who *abandoned* their baskets, not just those who successfully checked out, providing a more complete picture of the problems.

### 3. 5 Whys

*   **Rationale for Selection:** This is a practical tool for the analysis phase. Once a problem is identified and validated through the other models (e.g., "Users are not applying promo codes"), the 5 Whys provides a simple, systematic way to drill down to the root cause (e.g., Why? The input field is hard to find -> Why? It's below the fold -> Why? ...etc.). This ensures the redesign solves the fundamental issue, not just a surface-level symptom.

# Applying Jobs to be Done to Basket Problem Validation

This document applies the thinking steps from the Jobs to be Done (JTBD) framework to create a research plan for the task: "Validate/refresh customer evidence for existing Basket problems."

---

### Introduction: The Core Job of the Basket

Before validating specific problems, we must first define the core "Job" a customer hires a shopping basket to do. The job is not "to hold products"; it is the progress the user is trying to make. We can hypothesize a few core jobs to be validated by research:

*   **Main Job:** When I'm ready to buy, I want to complete my purchase quickly and confidently, so I can get on with my day.
*   **Ancillary Job (Planning):** When I'm browsing but not ready to buy, I want to use the basket as a temporary holding place, so I can easily compare items or come back later.
*   **Ancillary Job (Validation):** When I'm considering a purchase, I want to see the final, all-in cost including shipping and discounts, so I can make a final budget decision without surprises.

All "existing problems" should be evaluated against their ability to help or hinder these core jobs.

---

### JTBD Thinking Steps: A Research Plan

Here is a plan to validate evidence, structured around the JTBD thinking steps:

**1. Spot the Struggle**
*   **Goal:** Identify where users struggle to make progress on their Job.
*   **Research Actions:**
    *   Analyze customer support tickets where the topic is "basket" or "checkout." What are the verbatim complaints?
    *   Interview users who recently abandoned a cart. Ask them to narrate the story of what they were trying to do and what went wrong.
    *   Look for "compensatory behaviors" or hacks. Are users taking screenshots of their basket? Emailing themselves links? These are signs the product is failing to do its job.

**2. Map the Circumstances**
*   **Goal:** Understand the context in which the Job arises.
*   **Research Actions:**
    *   Segment analytics data: Does basket abandonment rate change for new vs. returning customers? Mobile vs. desktop users?
    *   In interviews, ask: "Where were you when you were trying to do this? What else was going on? Were you in a hurry?"

**3. Elicit Desired Progress (Validate Job Stories)**
*   **Goal:** Confirm the proposed Job Stories and uncover new ones.
*   **Research Actions:**
    *   In user interviews, ask forward-looking questions: "If the basket worked perfectly for you, how would that make your life better or easier?"
    *   Present the hypothesized Job Stories and ask users to rank them based on what they were trying to do during their last session.

**4. Analyze the Four Forces of Progress**
*   **Goal:** Understand the forces driving and blocking a switch from the current basket experience to a new, improved one.
*   **Research Actions:** Create a 2x2 map and fill it with insights from customer interviews:
    *   **Push of the Situation:** What specific problems with the current basket are pushing users to want something different?
    *   **Pull of the New Solution:** What promise would a new design have to make to pull them in (e.g., "one-click checkout")?
    *   **Anxieties about the Solution:** What are their fears? (e.g., "Will my payment be secure?", "Will I lose my promo code if I click back?")
    *   **Inertia/Habits of the Present:** What keeps them using the current basket even if it's flawed? (e.g., saved payment info, familiarity).

**5. Widen the Competitive Set**
*   **Goal:** Identify what your basket *really* competes with.
*   **Research Actions:**
    *   Ask users: "Before you put items in our basket, where else did you consider putting them?" and "When you abandoned our basket, what did you do next?"
    *   Recognize that the competition isn't just Amazon's basket. It's also taking a screenshot, writing a note, emailing a link, or giving up entirely.

**6. Model the System**
*   **Goal:** Identify all stakeholders involved in the purchase.
*   **Research Actions:**
    *   Ask: "Is the person adding items to the basket the same person who pays for them?" (e.g., a designer shopping for a client, a child adding to a parent's account).
    *   Determine if anyone else needs to approve the purchase and how that affects the Job.

**7. & 8. Validate and Connect to Redesign**
*   **Goal:** Use the JTBD insights to prioritize the redesign effort.
*   **Next Steps:**
    *   The insights from this research should be used to rank the "existing problems." Problems that directly cause a struggle with a core Job are the highest priority.
    *   The redesign effort should be framed as a mission to better serve these validated Jobs.
    *   New prototypes should be tested against their ability to reduce struggle, minimize anxiety, and deliver the desired progress.

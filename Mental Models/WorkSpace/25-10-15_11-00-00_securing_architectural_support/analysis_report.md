# Analysis Report: A Strategy for Securing Architectural Support

## Executive Summary

The project to migrate the basket's data source to the strategic DPMS platform is blocked. The root cause is not a lack of available architects, but a **mismatch in perceived importance**. The request is currently framed as a "low-level" task and is being ignored because it does not align with the architects' incentives to work on high-impact, strategic projects.

To unblock the project, the team must stop asking for a "sign-off" and instead **reframe the project as a strategic opportunity**. This report recommends a clear, three-step sequence of actions:

1.  **Frame:** Create a concise, professional one-page proposal that frames this work not as a bug fix, but as the **"DPMS Pilot Program"**—a strategic initiative that de-risks the basket and accelerates the adoption of a company-wide standard.
2.  **Align:** Submit this proposal through the company's **formal architecture review process**. This makes the request visible and aligns it with the architects' incentives by offering them a quick, strategic win.
3.  **Leverage:** If the formal process fails, use the ignored request as justification to engage a senior business stakeholder, using their organizational **leverage** to prioritize the issue.

Following this strategy will transform the request from an easily ignored chore into a compelling, mutually beneficial partnership.

## Problem Statement

The development team has a clear technical plan to migrate the basket's promotional data source to DPMS, but cannot proceed without architectural review and approval. They are caught in an "approval paradox": they cannot get an architect's attention because the work is perceived as "low-level," yet they feel they cannot proceed without approval. This has stalled a critical project needed to improve the basket's functionality and reliability.

## Individual Model Analysis

### Model 1: Framing

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The problem is rooted in perception. This model was chosen to change that perception.
*   **Analysis & Findings:** The current "bug fix" frame is ineffective. The analysis shows that by reframing the project as a "Strategic Enabler"—one that de-risks a critical journey and accelerates the company's DPMS strategy—it becomes compelling to a senior, strategic audience like the architecture team.

### Model 2: Incentives

*   **Rationale for Selection:** This model explains *why* the current approach is failing.
*   **Analysis & Findings:** Architects are incentivized to work on large-scale strategic problems. A request to "sign off on a fix" is misaligned with these incentives. By framing the project as a pilot for the DPMS rollout, we transform it into a strategic win for the architect, aligning their incentives with the team's goals. Helping the team now helps the architect achieve their own long-term objectives.

### Model 3: Leverage

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The team's current approach has no leverage. This model identifies tools to multiply their influence.
*   **Analysis & Findings:** The analysis identified three levers of increasing power: a professional proposal, using the formal review process, and stakeholder escalation. This provides a clear, sequential action plan that is far more effective than informal conversations.

## Synthesis & Integrated Insights

The three models combine to create a powerful and coherent strategy:

1.  **Framing** tells us *what* to say. We need to change the story from "we need help with a small task" to "we are offering you a strategic win."
2.  **Incentives** tells us *why* the new story will be effective. It works because it aligns with the architects' motivation to demonstrate strategic impact and drive their own initiatives forward.
3.  **Leverage** tells us *how* to deliver the story. We deliver it not through an informal chat, but through powerful tools like a professional one-pager, the official company process, and, if necessary, the influence of a senior business leader.

The integrated insight is that the team must stop acting like a low-level subordinate asking for a favor and start acting like a strategic partner offering a mutual benefit. The key is to do the upfront work to make it incredibly easy for an architect to see the strategic value and say "yes."

## Actionable Options & Recommendations

### Recommendation: The Three-Step Engagement Plan

This is a sequential plan. Do not skip steps.

**Step 1: Create the "Strategic Enabler" Proposal (Lever 1)**

*   **Action:** Draft a concise one-page document or a 3-slide deck.
*   **Title:** "De-Risking the Basket: A Pilot Program for the DPMS Strategy."
*   **Content:** Use the language from the "Framing" analysis. Focus on risk reduction, strategic acceleration, and unblocking business value.
*   **The Ask:** End with a clear, low-friction request: "We are requesting a 1-hour review session with the appropriate architect to validate our approach for this strategic pilot."

**Step 2: Utilize the Formal Process (Lever 2)**

*   **Action:** Find the official, formal process for requesting architectural review. This is a critical discovery task. Ask a project manager, a senior engineer, or look on the company's internal wiki.
*   **Execution:** Submit the proposal from Step 1 through this formal channel. This creates a visible, trackable record and makes the request official.

**Step 3: Escalate with Justification (Lever 3 - If Necessary)**

*   **Condition:** Only proceed with this step if the formal request in Step 2 is ignored for an unreasonable amount of time (e.g., more than a week or two, depending on company culture).
*   **Action:**
    1.  Go to your direct manager or product owner.
    2.  Present the situation clearly: "We are blocked on a project to improve basket conversion. We have a clear proposal and have followed the formal process for architectural review, but have received no response."
    3.  Ask them to engage their superior (e.g., the Head of Product) to raise the issue with the Head of Architecture.
*   **The Message (from one leader to another):** "My team is blocked from improving a revenue-critical journey. They have a clear plan and have followed the process. Can you help me understand what we need to do to get them the architectural guidance they need?"

This structured approach maximizes the chances of success at each stage while minimizing political risk.

# Reasoning File 3: Leverage

This document applies the mental model "Leverage" to identify the most effective ways to multiply the team's influence and unblock the project.

## Analysis using Thinking Steps

### 1. Identify the System's Fulcrum

The system is the organization's process for architectural review. The heavy object we want to move is the stalled DPMS migration project.

The team is currently using a low-leverage approach: informal chats and shoulder-tapping, which is like trying to lift a boulder with your bare hands. It's exhausting and ineffective.

The fulcrum—the point where a small effort can create the biggest change—is the **architects' attention and their perception of the project's value.** A small push on this point can swing them from "ignore" to "engage."

### 2. Determine the Desired Outcome

The goal is to get the DPMS migration project reviewed, approved, and unblocked in the most efficient way possible.

### 3. Brainstorm Levers

Here are three levers the team can use, in increasing order of power and effort.

#### Lever 1 (Low Effort, Medium Power): The Professional Proposal

*   **Description:** This is the one-page document or 3-slide deck discussed in the "Framing" analysis. It concisely presents the project using the "Strategic Enabler" frame.
*   **Why it's a Lever:** It multiplies the force of the request by respecting the architect's single most valuable resource: their time. It does the cognitive work for them, allowing them to grasp the strategic importance in 60 seconds. A well-crafted proposal makes it easy to say "yes" to a meeting and hard to justify ignoring.

#### Lever 2 (Medium Effort, High Power): The Formal Process

*   **Description:** This involves discovering and using the official, established channel for architectural review. This might be a specific JIRA queue, a standing "Architecture Review Board" meeting, or a formal submission template.
*   **Why it's a Lever:** It shifts the request from an informal, easily-ignored email into a formal, visible, and trackable work item. It creates accountability for the architecture team *as a group* to respond. It also demonstrates that the team is professional and trying to follow the correct procedure.

#### Lever 3 (High Effort, Highest Power): Stakeholder Escalation

*   **Description:** This involves identifying the senior business leader who is most impacted by the stalled project (e.g., the Head of Product or GM responsible for basket conversion). The lever is not the team asking the architect for help, but the **Head of Product asking the Head of Architecture** why their team is blocked from improving a revenue-critical part of the business.
*   **Why it's a Lever:** It uses the organization's hierarchical structure as a powerful force multiplier. It reframes the problem in the language that senior leaders understand: business impact and blocked objectives. This is the most potent lever but also carries the most risk.

### 4. Assess the Upside and Downside

*   **Lever 1 (Proposal):** Upside: High. Downside: None.
*   **Lever 2 (Formal Process):** Upside: High. Downside: Low (it might be bureaucratic or slow).
*   **Lever 3 (Escalation):** Upside: Extremely high (almost guarantees a response). Downside: High (can burn political capital if used prematurely or clumsily).

### 5. Apply with Measure

The levers should be applied in a measured sequence:

1.  **Start with Lever 1:** Create the high-quality, well-framed proposal. This is a prerequisite for all other steps.
2.  **Combine with Lever 2:** Use the proposal from step 1 as the artifact you submit through the formal process (once you identify it).
3.  **Reserve Lever 3:** Only if the formal process fails or is ignored for an unreasonable amount of time should the team consider escalating to a senior stakeholder. The existence of the formal, ignored request (from Lever 2) provides the perfect justification for the escalation.

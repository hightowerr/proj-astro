# Mental Model Selection Report

## 1. Selection Funnel

From an initial list of over 50 potential models, a candidate list of 5 was created based on the updated problem diagnosis (reframing the issue as one of organizational influence). After a deep-dive review, a final selection of the 3 most impactful models was made.

## 2. Final Ranked Selection

1.  **Framing**
2.  **Incentives**
3.  **Leverage**

## 3. Rationale for Selection

### 1. Framing

*   **Reason for Selection:** This is the most critical model because the root of the problem is a mismatch in perception. The project is stalled because it is currently framed as a "low-level activity." To get the architects' attention, the team must reframe the request, elevating it from a simple data migration to a strategic initiative that reduces technical debt, de-risks a critical user journey (the basket), and accelerates the adoption of the company-wide DPMS strategy.
*   **Reference from Model:** The selection directly aligns with the model's `Thinking Steps`, particularly `Step 3: Choose the Frame`. The team needs to shift from a "technical task" frame to a "strategic value" frame.

### 2. Incentives

*   **Reason for Selection:** This model explains *why* the architects are disengaged. Their behavior is driven by incentives to focus on high-impact, "sophisticated" problems. To succeed, the team must align their project with what the architects are rewarded for. The project should be presented as an opportunity for the architecture team to score a quick and visible win by successfully guiding the first team to adopt the new DPMS standard.
*   **Reference from Model:** The choice is guided by the core principle: "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome." The team needs to create a situation where the architect's incentive is to help them, not ignore them.

### 3. Leverage

*   **Reason for Selection:** The team currently has low leverage in their attempts to engage architecture. This model provides a concrete path to multiply their influence. Creating a professional one-page proposal is a point of leverage because it respects the architects' time. If that fails, using the influence of a senior business stakeholder (who has a vested interest in the basket's performance) is a much stronger lever.
*   **Reference from Model:** This aligns with the `Thinking Steps` of identifying a system's fulcrum. The fulcrum here is not technical; it's either a well-crafted document that makes saying "yes" easy, or a senior leader who can apply organizational pressure.

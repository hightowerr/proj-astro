# Reasoning File 2: Incentives

This document applies the mental model "Incentives" to understand and influence the behavior of the architecture team.

## Analysis using Thinking Steps

### 1. Identify the Desired Outcome

The desired outcome is for a skilled architect to proactively engage with the team, dedicate meaningful time to reviewing the DPMS migration plan, and provide a confident sign-off.

### 2. Analyze the Current Incentives

To understand why the team is being ignored, we must first understand what incentivizes the architects.

*   **Architects are incentivized to:**
    *   Work on large, complex, high-impact strategic projects (e.g., "sophisticated buy vs. build" decisions).
    *   Demonstrate progress on their own long-term initiatives (like the successful adoption of the new DPMS platform).
    *   Maximize their influence and value across the entire organization, not just one team.

*   **Architects are DIS-incentivized from:**
    *   Spending time on tasks perceived as "low-level" or tactical.
    *   Acting as a "rubber stamp" or sign-off authority for individual team implementations, as this provides little personal or strategic value.
    *   Engaging with requests that are poorly defined or seem like administrative overhead.

**Conclusion:** The current request is misaligned with the architects' incentive structure. It is perceived as a low-value, administrative task, so they are correctly (based on their incentives) ignoring it.

### 3. Map Incentives to Actors

The key actor is the architect. Their primary motivation is to demonstrate strategic impact. A request to simply "approve our work so we don't get in trouble" is fundamentally misaligned with this motivation. It asks them to take on responsibility without gaining any strategic reward.

### 4. Design Aligned Incentives

We cannot change the architects' bonus structure, but we can fundamentally change the nature of the project to align with their existing motivations. The "Strategic Enabler" frame we developed is the key to doing this.

*   **The Old, Misaligned Incentive:** "Please do this chore for us."
*   **The New, Aligned Incentive:** "We are offering you a quick, visible, and strategic win."

By framing the project as the **"DPMS Pilot Program,"** we are no longer asking for a favor. We are offering the architect an opportunity:

> "If you help our team succeed, you get a concrete case study demonstrating the success of your strategic DPMS initiative. Our success becomes a powerful tool *you* can use to persuade other, more reluctant teams to migrate. This project makes *you* and *your* strategy look good."

This transforms the project from a burden into a mutually beneficial partnership. The architect is now incentivized to help because the team's success directly contributes to their own goals.

### 5. Stress-Test for Unintended Consequences

*   **Potential Negative Behavior:** An architect, wanting to create a perfect "showcase" for their DPMS strategy, might try to over-engineer the solution, adding unnecessary complexity that would make it difficult for other teams to follow.
*   **Mitigation:** The project proposal must explicitly state that a primary goal is to create a **simple, elegant, and repeatable blueprint**. This frames the desired outcome not as a complex masterpiece, but as a practical template, incentivizing the architect to find the most efficient and reusable solution.

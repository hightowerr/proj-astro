# JTBD Analysis of the Lister Proposition

This document applies the "Jobs to be Done" (JTBD) mental model to the "Lister" Shape Up pitch.

### **1. Spot the Struggle**
The pitch identifies two primary struggles:
- **Organizational Struggle:** Users' personal rankings and lists are fragmented across disparate, unsuitable tools like notes apps, screenshots, or memory. This leads to a low-grade but persistent struggle to maintain and access their own opinions.
- **Discovery Struggle:** Users are frustrated with generic, platform-driven recommendations and desire a system that understands their unique taste, leading to a feeling of being misunderstood by current discovery engines.

### **2. Map the Circumstances**
- **Context:** The "job opening" occurs when a user finishes a piece of media (a show, a game) and wonders "what's next?", or when they are in a social context and wish to share or defend their preferences.
- **Triggers:** The trigger is often a desire for efficient, high-quality discovery or the need to externalize and solidify one's own identity through curated taste.
- **Constraints:** Users are time-poor and have a backlog of content, making efficient and relevant discovery paramount.

### **3. Elicit Desired Progress & Draft Job Stories**
The desired progress is a move from fragmented, implicit taste to a state of clear, explicit, and useful taste.

- **Job Story 1 (Self-Expression/Organization):** *When* I have strong opinions about the media I consume, *I want to* easily create and maintain a definitive ranked list, *so I can* feel that my personal taste is concretely defined and readily available.
- **Job Story 2 (Discovery):** *When* I'm looking for something new, *I want to* leverage my own unique taste profile, *so I can* get recommendations that feel like they were hand-picked for me by someone who "gets" me.
- **Job Story 3 (Social Connection):** *When* I talk with friends about media, *I want to* quickly compare our top rankings, *so I can* spark interesting conversations and discover taste overlaps. (Note: This job is intentionally deferred by the P0's "no social" rule, but is a likely future driver).

### **4. Analyze the Four Forces**
- **Push (from the old way):** Frustration with messy notes, bad algorithmic recommendations, and the inability to recall one's own definitive rankings.
- **Pull (towards Lister):** The promise of a simple, elegant home for one's taste; the "magic" of seeing recommendations instantly adapt to personal input.
- **Anxiety (about Lister):** Will setup be a chore? Will the recommendations be any good? Is this just another app I'll abandon?
- **Inertia (of the old way):** The habits of using a simple notes app or passively browsing Netflix are strong. The bar for Lister to provide superior value is high.

### **5. Widen the Competitive Set**
Lister isn't just competing with other apps, but with behaviors.
- **Direct Competitors:** Feature-rich, category-specific platforms like Letterboxd or Goodreads.
- **Indirect Competitors:** General-purpose tools like Notes apps, Spreadsheets, and social platforms like Reddit.
- **Behavioral Competitors:** Word-of-mouth recommendations and simply doing nothing.
- **What customers will "fire":** The goal is for users to "fire" their note-taking hacks and their reliance on generic, impersonal recommendation algorithms.

### **6. Model the System**
The P0 deliberately keeps the system simple: the **user** is also the **chooser**, **payer**, and **beneficiary**. The "No-Gos" list is a strategic decision to prevent system complexity (e.g., social dynamics, multi-sided markets) before the core job is validated.

### **7. Prototype Around Moments**
The P0 is a perfect example of this step. It is prototyped *entirely* around the single most important moment of struggle: creating a list and getting immediate, personalized feedback. The UI is stripped of everything that does not serve this core interaction.

### **8. Validate with Evidence**
The pitch correctly identifies the evidence the P0 needs to gather:
- **Revealed Preference:** Do users successfully create a list? Do they come back to edit it?
- **Hypothesis Validation:** Are the recommendations compelling enough to prove the "ranking as a signal" hypothesis? Does the core loop work without a social graph?

**Conclusion:** The Lister pitch is strongly aligned with JTBD principles. It focuses on a clear struggle and designs the smallest possible experiment (the P0) to validate that it can deliver the desired progress.

### Reasoning using Opportunity Cost

This model is a powerful communication tool to help all stakeholders understand the true price of the current situation.

**1. Identify the Core Choice and its Opportunity Cost:**

*   **The Choice (Implicit):** To operate in a state where 80% of the engineering team's capacity is consumed by reactive firefighting and incident response.
*   **The Opportunity Cost:** The value of everything that could be accomplished with that 80% capacity if it were free. This is the strategic roadmap that is being implicitly sacrificed.

**2. Frame the Choice to Make the Cost Visible:**

Instead of discussing the 80% figure in the abstract, frame the trade-off in concrete terms for stakeholders:

*   "For every 5 weeks our team works, 4 of those weeks are spent on keeping the current system running, leaving only 1 week for new projects. The opportunity cost of this instability is a 4/5ths reduction in our feature velocity."

**3. Quantify the Next-Best Alternative:**

The value of the "next-best alternative" is the roadmap itself. The conversation with stakeholders should be framed as follows:

*   "Would we rather:
    *   **Option A (Current Path):** Continue with the current level of instability, consuming most of our team's time, and struggle to meet even our most critical deadlines.
    *   **Option B (Strategic Investment):** Dedicate a focused period of time (e.g., the rest of this quarter) to stabilizing the system and tackling core technical debt (like the Next.js migration), even if it means formally pausing all other feature work. This choice has an opportunity cost of a few delayed features *now*, but it buys back our team's capacity to deliver the entire roadmap *later*."

**4. Use as a Communication Tool:**

This framing is essential for justifying the actions derived from the **Bottlenecks** and **Inversion** analysis.

*   When explaining the need to subordinate work (from Bottlenecks), you can say: "The opportunity cost of continuing to firefight is too high. We are choosing to temporarily say 'no' to these smaller features so that we can say 'yes' to a stable platform and our critical deadlines."
*   When justifying the focus on stability and Argos Pay (from Inversion), you can say: "The opportunity cost of a Black Friday failure is catastrophic. We cannot afford to risk it by allocating our limited capacity elsewhere."

By making the opportunity cost explicit, the decision to focus on stability and debt-reduction shifts from being a "delay" to being a sound strategic investment in future velocity.

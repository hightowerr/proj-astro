# Reasoning with Friction and Viscosity (m17)

Applying the "Friction" mental model to the Argos Pay integration within the basket journey.

## 1. Identify the Desired Motion
- The primary desired motion is for the user to move from the basket page to the checkout page with confidence and a clear intent to purchase.

## 2. Pinpoint the Friction/Viscosity
- Introducing a new decision ("Do I want to use credit?") at this critical stage inherently adds cognitive friction.
- **Sources of Bad Friction:**
  - A visually loud or disruptive banner that distracts from the primary "Checkout" button.
  - Confusing language that makes the user stop and try to figure out what the offer means.
  - An entry point that leads to a complex, multi-page application process, derailing the checkout flow.

## 3. Distinguish Good vs. Bad Friction
- **Bad Friction:** Anything that causes confusion, distraction, or anxiety for a user who is not interested in credit.
- **Good Friction:** A well-designed, optional "speed bump" that causes a user who *is* concerned about affordability to pause and consider a helpful solution. For example, a simple, one-click "Learn More" that opens a modal is good friction; it confirms the user's interest before taking them out of the main flow.

## 4. Reduce Bad Friction
- The offer must be presented with extreme clarity and simplicity.
- It should be placed in a location that does not compete with the primary call-to-action (e.g., below the order summary, not above the checkout button).
- The initial interaction should be low-commitment (e.g., a link, not a form).

## 5. Strategically Increase Friction (for competitors)
- This model is less applicable here, but one could argue that by making our own affordability option so seamless and trustworthy, we increase the relative "friction" for a customer to go elsewhere to seek financing for their purchase.

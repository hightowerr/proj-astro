# Mental Models Selected for Analysis

This document outlines the mental models chosen to analyze the 'Interim Design Change Decision' problem.

## Selection Summary

From an initial list of over 170 candidate models, the following three were selected for their high relevance and power in addressing the specific challenges of this problem. The selection focuses on models that clarify trade-offs, explore long-term consequences, and challenge underlying assumptions.

--- 

## Ranked Selection

### 1. Opportunity Cost (m56_opportunity_cost.md)

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The core of the PM's resentment stems from a sense of wasted effort. This model is crucial for articulating the hidden costs of the proposed change. By dedicating developer time to this low-impact UI tweak, the team is explicitly *not* working on other, potentially higher-value tasks. This model provides a formal framework for quantifying and communicating this trade-off.

### 2. Second-Order Thinking (m05_second-order_thinking.md)

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The problem statement already contains seeds of second-order thinking (e.g., "invest in something that may get replaced"). This model is selected to formalize and deepen that instinct. We need to move beyond the immediate, first-order consequence (a "nicer" UI) and analyze the downstream effects. What is the impact on team morale if they work on throwaway tasks? What precedent does this set for future, low-evidence suggestions? How does it impact the timeline and budget for the *actual* redesign?

### 3. Inversion (m07_inversion.md)

*   **Rationale for Selection:** The current debate is framed as "Should we do this small improvement?" Inversion allows us to escape this narrow frame. The model was chosen to flip the question to: "What are the negative consequences if we *don't* do this?" and "How could we guarantee this project is a failure?" This approach forces a direct confrontation with the actual, tangible value of the proposal. If the consequences of not doing it are negligible (which the PM suspects), it exposes the change as a low-priority "want" rather than a critical "need", providing a powerful argument for deferring the work.

# Analysis using Inversion

This document applies the 'Inversion' mental model to the 'Interim Design Change Decision'. Instead of asking how to succeed, we will ask how to fail, and then work to avoid those paths.

---

## Application of Thinking Steps

### 1. Clearly Define the Goal

The primary goal is to **effectively use limited development resources to improve the performance of the empty trolley page**, with a specific focus on increasing the add-to-basket rate from product recommendations.

### 2. Invert the Problem

We flip the goal on its head. The inverted question becomes:

> "How could we guarantee that we **fail** to use our resources effectively and make the empty trolley page project a failure?"

### 3. Brainstorm All Potential Failure Points

To guarantee failure, we would:

*   **Consistently prioritize work with no data:** We would spend our limited developer-days on tasks that are not based on any user research, data, or validated problems. We would focus on what stakeholders *think* looks nice rather than what users *need*.
*   **Focus on low-impact, throwaway tasks:** We would ensure that the work our team does is temporary and will be intentionally discarded in the near future, maximizing wasted effort.
*   **Demoralize the development team:** We would assign tasks that the engineers know are pointless, signaling that their time and expertise are not valued. This would ensure they are disengaged and less productive on future, more important projects.
*   **Undermine the product strategy:** We would create a culture where strategic, evidence-based decision-making can be easily overridden by subjective opinions or stakeholder pressure, ensuring the roadmap is chaotic and ineffective.
*   **Ignore the primary objective:** We would actively divert resources away from the core goal (improving recommendations) and toward ancillary, cosmetic tweaks.

### 4. Create an "Avoidance List"

Based on the failure points brainstormed above, the path to success requires that we actively **AVOID** the following:

1.  **AVOID:** Allocating resources to any change that lacks a clear, evidence-based hypothesis for how it will improve a key metric.
2.  **AVOID:** Working on temporary features that will be knowingly replaced by an upcoming strategic initiative.
3.  **AVOID:** Saying "yes" to stakeholder requests that come at the direct expense of team morale and focus on primary objectives.
4.  **AVOID:** Conflating subjective aesthetic improvements with objective user or business value.
5.  **AVOID:** Ignoring the opportunity cost of even "small" tasks.

## Conclusion from this Model

The proposed interim UI change maps directly onto several of the key paths to failure. Proceeding with it would mean we are actively choosing to waste resources, focus on low-impact work, and risk team morale. 

Inversion provides a starkly clear conclusion: the most direct path to failing at this project is to do exactly what is being proposed. Therefore, the correct course of action is to **avoid** implementing this change and to instead refocus the conversation on the "Avoidance List" and the defined project goals. This model gives the PM a powerful, logical foundation to explain why saying "no" is the correct strategic decision to ensure the project's success.

# Reasoning: Whether or Not Decisions & Vanishing Options Test

## Application to the Insurance Tile Design Decision

These two mental models are applied in conjunction to address the user's tendency to frame the problem as a binary choice and to encourage the generation of a broader range of solutions.

### 1. Identify the "whether or not" language:

*   The user's statement, "Why would we need to show two tiles? Surely what we should be doing is changing the messaging on the on the single tile," clearly presents a binary choice between the designer's suggestion of two tiles and the user's preference for one tile with revised messaging. This is a classic "whether or not" decision frame.

### 2. Treat this language as a red flag and acknowledge narrow framing:

*   The current discussion is narrowly framed around the quantity of tiles (one vs. two). This narrow framing risks overlooking more creative or effective solutions that could better balance compliance, user experience, and business objectives (CLV).
*   The core problem is not merely the number of tiles, but how to effectively, compliantly, and persuasively present two distinct insurance options to customers who have an "okay, not great" understanding of these products, all while reducing clutter and contributing to long-term customer value.

### 3. Force a search for more options:

*   Instead of: "Should we have two tiles or one tile with changed messaging?"
*   **Reframe the question:** "What are all the possible ways we could present these two insurance options to customers in the basket, ensuring compliance with advertising standards, minimizing clutter, enhancing customer understanding, and ultimately improving CLV?"

### 4. Use the Vanishing Options Test:

This technique helps to break free from the existing options and stimulate new ideas.

*   **Scenario 1: Imagine we *cannot* have two separate tiles (the designer's suggestion is impossible). What would we do instead to satisfy compliance and present both options?**
    *   **Neutral & Inclusive Messaging:** The single tile's messaging must be explicitly neutral and clearly indicate the availability of *both* Term Care and Monthly Care (e.g., "Explore Protection Options," "Add Insurance for Your Product").
    *   **Clear Side Drawer Presentation:** Ensure that upon clicking the single tile, the side drawer immediately and equally presents both Term Care and Monthly Care, with clear distinctions and benefits.
    *   **Subtle Indicators:** Consider a small, non-intrusive visual cue on the single tile (e.g., a small icon or text like "2 options available") to hint at the choice within, without adding clutter.
    *   **Alternative UI Element:** Explore if a different UI component, not a traditional "tile," could present the initial choice more effectively while remaining consolidated (e.g., a compact information block with a clear call to action to view details).

*   **Scenario 2: Imagine we *cannot* have a single consolidated tile (even with changed messaging – the user's preference is impossible). What would we do instead to satisfy compliance and reduce clutter?**
    *   **Compact Presentation of Two Options:** Design two *highly compact* and visually distinct elements that are not full "cards" but clearly separate the options (e.g., two small, stacked radio buttons with brief descriptions, or a toggle switch between Term Care and Monthly Care, each leading to the same side drawer).
    *   **Progressive Disclosure:** Present one option prominently, but offer a clear, compliant, and easily discoverable link or button to "View Other Protection Options" for the second, less emphasized choice. (This would need careful compliance review to ensure it doesn't imply favoritism).
    *   **Contextual Presentation:** Could the options be presented at a different stage or in a different area of the basket that is less prone to clutter, or only after a user expresses initial interest in protection?

### 5. Reframe the question to focus on the underlying goal:

*   Instead of focusing on the *number* of tiles, the underlying goal is: "What is the most effective, compliant, and user-friendly way to present insurance protection choices (Term Care and Monthly Care) in the basket to maximize customer value and satisfaction?"

## Evidence Gathered:
*   The user's initial framing of the problem as a choice between "two tiles" or "one tile with changed messaging" is a direct example of a "whether or not" decision, indicating narrow framing.
*   The tension between "advertising standards" (compliance), "reducing clutter" (UX), and "CLV" (business objective) necessitates a broader exploration of solutions than a binary choice allows.
*   The "okay, not great" customer understanding reinforces the need for clear, unambiguous presentation, which might be achieved through novel solutions generated by breaking the narrow frame.

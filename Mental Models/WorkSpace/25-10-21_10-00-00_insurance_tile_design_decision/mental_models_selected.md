# Mental Models Selected for Analysis

## Summary of Selection Process
From an initial list of 35 candidate mental models, 3 were deeply evaluated, and the final 3 were selected based on their direct relevance to the user's problem of balancing design, compliance, and business objectives in the insurance tile decision.

## Final, Ranked List of Chosen Models

1.  **Second-Order Thinking** (`m05_second-order_thinking.md`)
2.  **Whether or Not Decisions** (`m55_whether_or_not_decisions.md`) & **Vanishing Options Test** (`m57_vanishing_options_test.md`)
3.  **Curse of Knowledge** (`m99_curse_of_knowledge.md`)

## Rationale for Each Chosen Model

### 1. Second-Order Thinking
*   **Rationale for Selection:** This model is paramount because the user's problem involves balancing immediate design and compliance concerns with a long-term business objective (Customer Lifetime Value - CLV). First-order thinking might focus only on the immediate visual impact or a superficial compliance check. Second-Order Thinking will force an analysis of the ripple effects of each design choice (one tile vs. two tiles, messaging changes) on customer trust, long-term engagement, and ultimately, CLV. It helps anticipate unintended consequences beyond the initial interaction.

### 2. Whether or Not Decisions & Vanishing Options Test
*   **Rationale for Selection:** The user explicitly frames the problem as a binary choice: "Why would we need to show two tiles? Surely what we should be doing is changing the messaging on the on the single tile." This is a classic "whether or not" decision, which often leads to narrow framing and suboptimal solutions. The `Whether or Not Decisions` model highlights this pitfall, and the `Vanishing Options Test` provides a powerful technique to break out of this narrow frame. Together, they will encourage the generation of a wider, more creative range of solutions beyond simply one or two tiles, potentially leading to innovative approaches that satisfy both compliance (advertising standards) and user experience goals (reducing clutter).

### 3. Curse of Knowledge
*   **Rationale for Selection:** The problem involves differing perspectives between the user (product manager) and the new designer (compliance), and the user noted that customer understanding of the insurance products is "okay, not great." This indicates a high risk of the "curse of knowledge," where internal stakeholders, deeply familiar with the products and compliance nuances, overestimate how clearly customers will understand the presented options. This model will prompt a critical examination of the tile's messaging and design from the perspective of a less informed customer, ensuring clarity, fairness (addressing advertising standards), and ease of comprehension, which is vital for both compliance and a positive user experience.

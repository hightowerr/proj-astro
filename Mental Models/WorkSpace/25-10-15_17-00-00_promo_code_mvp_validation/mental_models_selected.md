# Mental Model Selection

**Analysis:** To define the simplest possible user journey (MVP) to validate the hypothesis that users will value applying a promo code in the basket.

**Selection Funnel:** From an initial list of over 100 models, 3 were deeply evaluated, and the final 3 were selected.

---

### Final Ranked Selection

1.  **First-Principle Thinking**
2.  **Occam's Razor**
3.  **Second-Order Thinking**

---

### Rationale for Selection

1.  **First-Principle Thinking:**
    *   **Reason:** To define an MVP, we must first understand the absolute, irreducible components of the action we want to test. This model forces us to break down "applying a promo code" into its fundamental truths: an input for the code, a trigger for the action, and feedback on the result. This gives us the foundational building blocks.
    *   **Reference:** Its `Thinking Steps` guide us to deconstruct the problem into its most basic components, which is the perfect starting point for building an MVP.

2.  **Occam's Razor:**
    *   **Reason:** The user explicitly asked for the "simplest journey." Occam's Razor is the classic tool for achieving simplicity. After identifying the basic components with First-Principle Thinking, this model will act as a "razor" to shave away any feature or step that is not absolutely essential to test the core hypothesis. It helps us challenge every "nice-to-have."
    *   **Reference:** Its core principle of favoring the explanation with the fewest assumptions directly translates to favoring the solution with the fewest features.

3.  **Second-Order Thinking:**
    *   **Reason:** This model provides a critical counterbalance to Occam's Razor. While we want the simplest journey, we don't want a journey so simple that it's misleading. Second-order thinking forces us to ask, "What are the consequences of this extreme simplicity?" For example, if the journey is simple but buggy or confusing, we might conclude users don't value the feature, when in fact they just don't value a poor experience. It ensures our MVP is viable, not just minimal.
    *   **Reference:** Its `Thinking Steps` prompt us to consider the "and then what?" effects, helping us anticipate unintended consequences of our minimalist design.

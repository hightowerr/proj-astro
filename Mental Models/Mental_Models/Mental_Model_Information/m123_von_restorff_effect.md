**Category:** Information

People notice items that stand out more

Description: Core concept explanation
The von Restorff effect (isolation effect) says that an item that stands out from its surroundings is more likely to be noticed and remembered—e.g., a lone “4” in a string of letters, or an unusual word in a list. Use distinctiveness (novelty, surprise, humor, atypical form) to capture attention and boost recall—especially for otherwise forgettable “middle” items. 

When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):

  * When “everything is highlighted.” Overuse kills contrast; keep true highlights to a small fraction of the display. 
  * When novelty harms comprehension or trust. Balance “new” with “familiar” (MAYA: most advanced yet acceptable). 
  * For accessibility risks. Extreme color contrasts, flashing/animation, or tiny/high-salience elements can hinder legibility or exclude users. 
  * When salience distracts from the primary task (signal-to-noise). Make the standout element the true priority, not just decoration. 

Keywords for Situations:
  Calls-to-action, alerts/notifications, pricing tables, long menus/lists (boost the “middle”), onboarding tips, study notes/mnemonics, email subject lines, slide design, feature tours, release notes, risk/exception messages. 

Thinking Steps:

  1. Define the single thing users must notice/remember. 
  2. Map the context: what everything else looks like (baseline).
  3. Choose one contrast dimension (size, color, shape, wording, imagery, position, or limited motion) and apply it to that one item only. 
  4. Keep total highlighting minimal (aim for “a little, not a lot”). 
  5. Pair distinctiveness with clarity and familiarity (MAYA balance). 
  6. Test recognition/recall: can users recount or find it later, especially among similar items? (Use lists where middle-item recall needs help.) 
  7. Check accessibility (contrast ratios, motion safety) and brand fit. 
  8. Monitor for habituation; rotate salience tactics sparingly. 

Coaching Questions:

  * What’s the one thing here that truly must pop for memory or action? 
  * If I removed all highlights except one, which would I keep? 
  * Which distinctiveness lever (wording, color, size, placement, imagery) best fits users’ expectations without confusing them? (MAYA) 
  * Does the standout element improve recall of a “middle” list item or just add noise? 
  * In a quick recall test, what do users remember first—and is it the right thing?

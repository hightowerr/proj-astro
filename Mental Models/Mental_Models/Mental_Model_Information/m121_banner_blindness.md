# Banner Blindness — Category: Information

**Description:**
“Banner blindness” is the tendency for people to ignore anything that *looks* like an ad—especially top-of-page strips, right-rail boxes, animated panels, or elements boxed off with heavy borders. It stems from selective attention: when users are goal-directed, they tune out visually ad-like areas and focus on navigation, headings, search, and primary content. Eye-tracking shows, for example, that users look far less at top banners than at content, animated ads get the fewest fixations, and SERP ads are noticed mainly when they appear at the very top (e.g., 27% looked at top graphical ads vs. 37% on the right; animated ads 29%; SERP top sponsored links 78% vs. right-side 33%). 

* **When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

  * Don’t place *critical* UI or messages (promo code fields, shipping info, confirmation banners, help links) in ad-like regions (top strip/right rail) or style them like promos—users may miss them due to banner blindness. NN/g examples show cart banners and sidebar info going unnoticed when they resembled ads. 
  * Avoid heavy animation or “boxed” treatments for key elements; animated units are frequently ignored. 
  * Be cautious with “reverse banner blindness”: putting an ad where a logo or core control normally sits can draw glances but risks confusion and trust. 
  * Over-emphasis everywhere backfires; too many visually loud elements create noise and further disengagement. 

* **Keywords for Situations:**
  Ad-like UI; right rail / top banner; animated panel; promotional box; internal promotion; SERP sponsored links; “looks like an ad”; cart/checkout banners; sidebar notices.

* **Thinking Steps:**

  1. **Clarify the user’s top task.** What must they notice or do on this page? (Top-task focus reduces miscues.) 
  2. **Audit for ad-like affordances.** Identify elements with borders, gradients, stock imagery, or animation that resemble ads; restyle critical items to match native UI. 
  3. **Fix placement priority.** Move key info to high-priority content areas (often upper-left of content column) and keep it out of traditional ad zones. Avoid “mismatched priority.” 
  4. **Use clear, utilitarian labels.** Replace cute/brand-y names (“Data Finder,” “We saw these and thought of you”) with plain terms that match user intent. 
  5. **Match site style for internal promos.** If you must promote, style it like native content (text-first, clear typography) to avoid automatic ignoring. 
  6. **Reduce motion; prefer clarity.** Reserve motion for true state change; ensure sufficient contrast without triggering “ad vibes.” 
  7. **Validate with quick tests.** Run 5–7 usability sessions or click-tracking to see if people *find* the element; iterate on style/placement. 

* **Coaching Questions:**

  * If I were trying to complete the main task here, what on this screen would I *safely* ignore—and did I accidentally style my important thing to look like *that*? 
  * Is any essential control or message living in the top strip or right rail? Could I relocate it into the main content flow? 
  * Which elements are animated or boxed off—do they help the task or just look like ads? 
  * Do my labels speak the user’s language (plain, goal-oriented) rather than brand jargon that people gloss over? 
  * If this were a search results page, would users only notice it when it’s at the top? How can I make it discoverable elsewhere? 

**Notes from sources:** Eyetracking research documents users routinely ignore ad-like regions (top/right), miss internal promos that resemble ads, and even overlook helpful cart/checkout elements styled or placed like ads; clear placement and native styling improve discovery.

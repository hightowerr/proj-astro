**Category:** Information

Total amount of mental effort required to complete a task


Description: Cognitive Load
The mental effort required to perceive, interpret, decide, and act. High load slows people down, increases errors, and causes abandonment. In interfaces, excess options, novelty, layout shifts, slow pages, and visual clutter all add up to load; familiar patterns, clear hierarchy, and fast feedback reduce it. 

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**
  * Offering too many choices or unfamiliar patterns (users hesitate, get confused). 
  * Forcing frequent context switches or deep page jumps; each page/screen incurs a “re-orientation” cost. 
  * Lengthy/complex forms or label layouts that increase eye movements and fixation time. 
  * Loud color palettes or competing highlights; saturation and contrast can overload attention. 
  * Onboarding that piles on tours, pop-ups, and steps; new users quickly hit overload and quit. 

**Keywords for Situations**
  * Onboarding & first-run experiences; product tours; “empty states.” 
  * Forms & inputs; labels and field arrangement; forgiving formats. 
  * Navigation design (global vs. local; breadcrumbs; keeping distances short). 
  * Visual hierarchy, color usage, and highlighting. 
  * Consistency/design systems to reduce recall demands and make behavior predictable. 
  * Performance/latency (“fast feels easy”); page loads and progress cues. 

**Thinking Steps**
  1. Define the user’s immediate goal and the single “next best action.” Remove or defer everything else. 
  2. Choose familiar patterns over novelty (navigation, controls, placements). 
  3. Minimize choices: combine, hide, or stage options (progressive disclosure). 
  4. Chunk tasks into short, ordered steps; show progress and keep orientation (breadcrumbs/indicators). 
  5. Optimize forms: use clear label placement, helpful defaults, forgiving input, and inline cues. 
  6. Tighten visual hierarchy: align, group, and use whitespace; reserve color for true emphasis. 
  7. Keep navigation distances short; elevate frequent actions to top level. 
  8. Reduce latency and perceived wait: fast loads, lean assets, meaningful feedback. 
  9. Standardize with a design system so similar things look/behave the same. 
  10. Test where attention stalls (first-clicks, fixations); trim or re-stage content accordingly. 

**Coaching Questions**
  * What is the one action you want a new user to take on this screen—and what competes with it? 
  * Which choices can be removed, combined, or delayed until later? 
  * Where would a familiar pattern work better than a novel control here? 
  * How can you reduce reading/eye travel in this form (labels, grouping, defaults)? 
  * What single element deserves color emphasis—and what can return to grayscale? 
  * If a page takes longer to load, what will the user see/do meanwhile? 
  * Which components behave inconsistently across screens—and how can the design system fix that? 

Sources from the attachment emphasize that: fewer, clearer choices reduce cognitive load; page/screen switches carry mental costs; form label placement affects load; restrained color lowers cognitive burden; onboarding must avoid overload; and design-system consistency aids recognition over recall.

**Category:** Information

People perceive designs with great aesthetics as easier to use

**Description**:
The Aesthetic-Usability Effect refers to the tendency for people to perceive aesthetically pleasing designs as more usable — even when functionality is identical. Positive emotional responses generated by beauty lead users to feel that the product works better, trust it more, and forgive small usability flaws. In short: beautiful things feel easier to use.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When aesthetics distract from poor usability or core functionality (“lipstick on a pig”).
- When visual polish delays essential testing of usability or accessibility.
- When prioritising design aesthetics increases cognitive load or impedes task completion.
- When team or client over-indexes on looks instead of performance or user feedback.

**Keywords for Situations:**
- Product design, web/app UI, branding, marketing
- Trust and emotional perception
- Early concept testing or first-use experience
- Customer satisfaction and forgiveness thresholds

**Thinking Steps**:
1. Baseline Functionality — Ensure product utility and core usability meet needs.
2. Emotional Layer — Add visual harmony, balance, and proportion (e.g., golden ratio, whitespace).
3. Evaluate Perception — Test user reactions to aesthetics; note perceived ease-of-use vs. actual ease-of-use.
4. Balance Trade-offs — Avoid aesthetics that harm accessibility, clarity, or performance.
5. Refine Iteratively — Adjust until aesthetics enhance—not mask—usability.

**Coaching Questions:**
- What emotional impression do you want the design to create at first glance?
- Could the current visual treatment be hiding functional flaws?
- How might aesthetics influence trust or willingness to forgive small issues?
- If users rated usability higher than actual task success, what design cues caused that?
- Does the design’s visual hierarchy guide or mislead the user’s attention?

**Category:** Information

---

**Description:**
*Attentional Bias* is the tendency to focus on certain stimuli — often those that are emotionally charged, threatening, or personally relevant — while ignoring other information. This bias shapes perception, memory, and decision-making by selectively filtering reality through what captures our attention. It can be influenced by prior experience, emotional state, or conditioning (e.g., addiction cues, fears, rewards). In short: *we see what matters most to us, not necessarily what’s most important.*

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

* When strong emotions or personal relevance distort objective attention (e.g., fear, craving, anxiety).
* When designing systems, interfaces, or communications that might unintentionally exploit or reinforce biased attention (e.g., clickbait, sensational imagery).
* When analyzing data or making judgments under distraction or overload.
* When focusing excessively on salient details blinds you to broader patterns (“spotlight effect”).

---

**Keywords for Situations:**

* Selective perception and emotional attention
* Addiction and craving behaviour
* Cognitive load and task prioritization
* UX design and visual emphasis (e.g., eye-tracking, banner blindness)
* Risk perception and situational awareness
* Training attention in mindfulness or decision contexts

---

**Thinking Steps:**

1. **Detect Salience** — Ask what stimuli or ideas are drawing your focus and why.
2. **Map Blind Spots** — Identify what you’re *not* attending to that might matter.
3. **Disentangle Emotion** — Note emotional charge (fear, desire, disgust) influencing focus.
4. **Expand View** — Deliberately broaden perspective or use tools (data, second opinions) to rebalance attention.
5. **Refocus Intentionally** — Reallocate attention toward information aligned with your goals, not your impulses.

---

**Coaching Questions:**

* What’s holding my attention right now — and why?
* What might I be overlooking because it feels less urgent or emotionally relevant?
* How does my current mood or stress level shape what I’m noticing?
* If someone else looked at this situation, what might *they* see that I can’t?
* How can I structure my environment or workflow to minimize unhelpful attentional traps?

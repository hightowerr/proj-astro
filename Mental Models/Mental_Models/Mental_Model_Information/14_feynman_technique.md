# Feynman Technique

## Description
The Feynman Technique is a method for developing deep understanding by explaining a concept in simple language. Richard Feynman, known for making complex scientific ideas accessible, used this approach to expose gaps in reasoning and force clarity.

The technique works because teaching a concept—without jargon or shortcuts—requires you to reconstruct the underlying logic. Any part you cannot explain plainly reveals incomplete understanding. This transforms learning from passive exposure into active sense-making.

The model operates through four steps:

1. **Choose a concept** you want to learn.
2. **Explain it as if to a 12-year-old**, using everyday language.
3. **Identify confusion and refine**, returning to source material to fix gaps.
4. **Organise and review**, producing a clean, simple, coherent explanation.

The value lies in stress-testing your grasp of both _what_ something is and _why_ it works.
## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when communicating with audiences that expect technical precision and jargon. Avoid when dealing with concepts that lose critical nuance when simplified. Don't use when the audience has specialized knowledge that requires technical terminology to convey meaning. Avoid in fields where oversimplification could lead to dangerous misunderstandings. Use caution when working with sophisticated technical systems where complexity is essential. Don't apply when the audience expects or requires formal, technical language to take the communication seriously. Avoid if the simplification might make you appear unqualified or unprofessional in the eyes of your audience.

## Thinking Steps
1. Choose a concept, theory, or topic you want to learn or understand better
2. Explain it in simple terms as if you're teaching it to a 12-year-old
3. Identify areas where you struggled to explain or where your explanation felt weak
4. Return to the source material to fill gaps in your understanding
5. Simplify your explanation further, eliminating jargon and complex terminology
6. Create analogies or examples that make the concept more relatable
7. Organize your explanation in a logical sequence
8. Review your explanation to ensure it covers the core ideas
9. Test your explanation with someone unfamiliar with the topic
10. Refine and iterate until the concept is clearly understood

## Coaching Questions
- How would you explain this concept to a child who has no background knowledge?
- What aspects of this topic do you struggle to explain in simple terms?
- Where are the gaps in your current understanding of this concept?
- What analogies or examples could help clarify this concept?
- What is the core idea behind this complex topic?
- How might different audiences understand this concept differently?
- What would happen if you had to explain this in just 2-3 sentences?
- What are the most fundamental principles underlying this complex topic?
- What would you say if your audience challenged a key aspect of your explanation?
- How can you simplify this without losing the essential meaning?

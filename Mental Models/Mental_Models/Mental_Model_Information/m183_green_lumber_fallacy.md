  - You assume “theory understanding” is required for practical success

  - Hiring, expert selection, and evaluating competence

  - Dismissing practitioners because they can’t narrate their skill
  - Overweighting credentials and elegant stories over performance
  - Confusing epiphenomena for causes and building fragile plans on them

  - Narrative vs Practice Mismatch

  - Practical success can depend on variables outsiders misidentify
  - Selection in the real world is often non-narrative (results-based)

### Description

The Green Lumber Fallacy is the mistake of assuming that success requires understanding the variables that *seem* important to outsiders. In reality, skilled practitioners can succeed with a different (often nonverbal, experience-shaped) map of the domain, and may be “ignorant” of the canonical narratives. The error is confusing a good explanation with causal or operational knowledge.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- In domains where theory truly is operationally binding (some formal engineering constraints).
- When you use the fallacy to dismiss real causal knowledge and safety requirements.

---

### Keywords for Situations

Narratives, expertise, hiring, practitioner knowledge, tacit knowledge, epiphenomena, selection pressure.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Define the success metric**
   What does “working” mean in this domain—profitability, reliability, speed, quality?
2. **Separate explanation from performance**
   Evaluate track record and decision quality under stress, not just eloquence.
3. **Identify the selection mechanism**
   Is the environment filtering incompetence (markets, hard ops) or protecting it (bureaucracy)?
4. **Find the real control variables**
   Ask practitioners what they actually watch and adjust; test which signals predict outcomes.
5. **Avoid epiphenomena**
   Treat pretty narratives as suspect; prefer interventions that remain safe when your story is wrong.
6. **Learn the minimum effective theory**
   Acquire only the theory needed to avoid ruin and make better choices; don’t mistake it for mastery.

---

### Coaching Questions

- Are we selecting for performance or for the ability to tell a compelling story?
- What do top practitioners monitor that outsiders ignore?
- Which variables are “green lumber” in our domain—misidentified but central?
- What would still work if our causal narrative turned out to be wrong?

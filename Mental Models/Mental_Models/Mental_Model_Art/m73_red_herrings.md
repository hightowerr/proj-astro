# m73_red_herrings.md

## Description
Red herrings are deliberate misdirections used to lead people away from the truth or the correct solution. Originally a technique to train hunting dogs by dragging a strong-smelling fish across a trail to create false leads, the term now refers to any diversionary tactic. In literature, red herrings are false clues that lead the audience toward an incorrect conclusion. In logic and argumentation, red herrings divert attention from the real issue by introducing irrelevant information. This technique can be used both to mislead and to test the analytical abilities of an audience.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When transparency and trust are more important than tactical advantage
- In situations requiring honest, direct communication
- When building long-term relationships where deception would cause harm
- In educational contexts where truth is paramount
- When the stakes are too high for misleading information
- When the audience needs accurate information for safety or well-being

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the actual issue or truth you want to protect or reveal
2. Assess what information might lead others away from the real issue
3. Determine if using misdirection serves a legitimate purpose
4. Consider the ethical implications of introducing false leads
5. Evaluate whether the audience would benefit from knowing the truth
6. Design the red herring to be plausible but ultimately misleading
7. Plan how to redirect attention back to relevant information when needed
8. Assess the potential consequences of the misdirection

## Coaching Questions
- What is the real issue or truth that needs protection or revelation?
- What information could divert attention from the real issue?
- Is this misdirection serving a legitimate purpose or just being deceptive?
- What would happen if I was completely transparent about the real issue?
- How would the audience respond to the truth versus the misdirection?
- What are the ethical implications of this red herring?
- How will I eventually bring attention back to the real issue?
- What are the potential consequences of this misdirection?
- Are there more honest alternatives to achieve the same goal?

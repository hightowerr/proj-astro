# m74_subtext.md

## Description
Subtext refers to the underlying meaning that exists beneath the surface of what is directly stated. In any work of art, there are two levels of meaning: the explicit text (what is directly communicated) and the subtext (what is conveyed without being outright stated). Subtext is what audiences intuitively pick up on and understand, sometimes without quite knowing how. It's often present in everyday speech when people say things that have different meanings than the literal ones. Subtext adds depth and layers to communication, allowing for richer experiences and multiple levels of interpretation.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When clear, direct communication is essential for safety or accuracy
- In situations requiring complete transparency and honesty
- When the audience might misinterpret the intended subtext
- In legal or technical contexts where precision is paramount
- When cultural differences might lead to misinterpretation
- When time constraints don't allow for nuanced communication
- In crisis situations requiring immediate, clear action

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the explicit message you want to communicate directly
2. Determine what additional meanings or implications you want to include
3. Consider what the audience will understand without being told directly
4. Assess whether the subtext aligns with your intended message
5. Evaluate how different audiences might interpret the subtext
6. Consider whether the subtext is too subtle or too obvious
7. Plan how to layer multiple levels of meaning appropriately
8. Verify that the subtext enhances rather than confuses the main message

## Coaching Questions
- What am I communicating directly versus indirectly?
- What do I want the audience to understand without being told explicitly?
- How might different audiences interpret this subtext differently?
- Is the subtext I'm conveying aligned with my intended message?
- What cultural or contextual factors might affect subtext interpretation?
- How can I ensure my subtext isn't lost or misunderstood?
- Are there risks to using subtext instead of direct communication?
- What would happen if I stated everything explicitly?
- How does subtext add depth to this piece of communication?

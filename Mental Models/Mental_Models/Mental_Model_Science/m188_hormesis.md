### Description

Hormesis is the idea that a small amount of a harmful stressor, in the right dose, can make an organism (or system) stronger and better prepared for larger stress. The key is nonlinearity: beyond a certain point, the same stressor becomes damaging, and depriving the system of stress altogether can cause weakness and fragility.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When you can’t control dose/intensity or can’t recover (no safety margin).
- When the stressor carries tail risks that are not bounded (avoid “heroic” interventions).

---

### Keywords for Situations

Stressors as information, training, recovery, dose-response, thresholds, variability, less-is-more.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Identify the stressor and the system**
   What exposure is being applied (exercise, fasting, heat/cold, cognitive load), to what system?
2. **Find the safe dose range**
   Determine intensity and frequency that produce adaptation without crossing into damage.
3. **Build recovery into the protocol**
   Adaptation requires rest; avoid constant stress that accumulates harm.
4. **Monitor for threshold crossings**
   Watch signals that the stressor is no longer beneficial (injury, chronic fatigue, degradation).
5. **Use variability intentionally**
   Prefer controlled variation over constant smooth exposure when it improves preparedness.

---

### Coaching Questions

- What is the smallest dose that produces adaptation?
- What signals tell us we crossed into harm?
- Where have we removed too much variability and caused atrophy?
- What recovery constraint makes the protocol sustainable?

**Description:**
Competition is a fundamental principle in nature and human systems where individuals, groups, or entities vie for limited resources such as food, territory, attention, market share, or mates. In biological systems, competition shapes evolution by selecting for traits that enhance survival and reproduction in contested environments. In human systems, competition can drive innovation, increase efficiency, and improve quality, but it can also lead to waste, duplication of effort, and destructive behavior. Understanding competition helps predict behavior patterns and design more effective systems.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- **When Cooperation Yields Better Results:** In situations where collaboration would produce greater collective benefits than individual competition.
- **When Resources Are Abundant:** In environments where resources are plentiful, competition may be unnecessary and create artificial scarcity.
- **When Competition Becomes Self-Defeating:** When competitive behaviors consume more resources than they generate or when competitors damage the resource pool in the process of competing.
- **In Fragile Systems:** When system integrity is at risk, competition might destabilize rather than strengthen the system.

**Keywords for Situations:**
Market dynamics, resource allocation, team performance, survival strategies, market positioning, innovation, efficiency, rivalry, business strategy, evolutionary pressures, competitive advantage, scarce resources, zero-sum situations, market analysis, performance improvement.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify the Resource:** Determine what is being competed for (e.g., customers, talent, territory, funding, market share).
2. **Map Competitive Landscape:** Identify all competitors and their resources, strengths, weaknesses, and strategies.
3. **Assess Resource Scarcity:** Evaluate how limited the resource is and whether competition is necessary or artificial.
4. **Analyze Competitive Dynamics:** Understand the rules of the competitive game and how they affect behaviors and outcomes.
5. **Consider Time Frame:** Determine whether the competition is sustainable in the long-term or will lead to resource depletion.
6. **Evaluate Alternatives:** Explore if cooperation or resource expansion could yield better outcomes than direct competition.
7. **Plan Your Strategy:** Determine whether to compete directly, differentiate, or avoid competition entirely based on your position and resources.

**Coaching Questions:**
- "What resource is everyone competing for?"
- "How is the competition affecting the quality or availability of the resource?"
- "Are we in a zero-sum or positive-sum competitive environment?"
- "What would happen if we tried cooperation instead of competition?"
- "How do our competitors' strategies affect our own strategy?"
- "What are we doing that forces others into competition we're not aware of?"
- "How can we compete in a way that strengthens rather than depletes the resource pool?"

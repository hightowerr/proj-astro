**Description:**
Dunbar's Number is a cognitive limit suggesting that humans can maintain stable social relationships with approximately 150 people. Proposed by anthropologist Robin Dunbar, this number is based on the correlation between primate brain size and average social group size, applied to humans. It represents the maximum number of people with whom one can maintain meaningful social relationships, where each person knows who each other person is and how they relate to one another. This model helps explain the natural limits of human social networks and has implications for organizational design, team sizes, and community structure.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- **When Assessing Individual Capacity:** Not everyone fits the exact number; individual cognitive capacity and social skills vary.
- **When Technology Changes Connection Types:** Digital communication may allow more connections, though they may be less meaningful than face-to-face relationships.
- **In Highly Specialized or Professional Settings:** Technical or professional relationships might function differently than social relationships.
- **When Applying to Temporary or Formal Relationships:** The model applies to stable, meaningful relationships, not to casual, professional, or one-time interactions.

**Keywords for Situations:**
Team size optimization, organizational structure, social networks, community building, management span of control, relationship maintenance, social media connections, group dynamics, collaboration, communication overhead, scaling organizations, network effects, community management.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify Your Core Network:** Determine who currently falls within your 150-person meaningful relationship circle.
2. **Assess Network Quality:** Evaluate the strength and value of your relationships within this circle.
3. **Map Organizational Implications:** If managing a team or organization, consider how Dunbar's Number affects communication and cohesion.
4. **Evaluate Expansion Needs:** Determine if you need to add new people to your circle and who might need to be de-emphasized to accommodate new connections.
5. **Optimize Communication:** Structure your communication and relationship maintenance efforts within the constraint of cognitive capacity.
6. **Apply to Team Design:** When building teams, consider keeping them under 150 members for optimal coordination and cohesion.
7. **Plan for Growth:** When organizations must exceed 150 people, implement structures to maintain connection quality.

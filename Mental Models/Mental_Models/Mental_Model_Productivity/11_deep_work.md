# Deep Work

## Description
Deep Work is the activity of focusing without distraction on a cognitively demanding task. It refers to professional activities performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit. These efforts create new value, improve skill, and are hard to replicate. The opposite of deep work is shallow work, which is noncognitively demanding, logistical-style tasks often performed while distracted. These tasks tend to create little new value and are easy to replicate. Deep work allows for peak productivity and the creation of new value, requiring significant focus and time. The model emphasizes the need to develop deep work skills by practicing attention control, building up focus gradually, and making deep work a habit.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Avoid deep work when you're in a role that requires constant communication and responsiveness to others. Don't apply when your work environment doesn't allow for uninterrupted focus periods. Use caution if your role involves many urgent tasks that require immediate attention. Avoid this approach when you're in an industry where quick responses to external changes are more valuable than deep focus. Don't use this model if you're in a collaborative environment where constant communication and brainstorming are more valuable than individual focused work. Be cautious when the work itself is primarily routine, administrative, or logistical in nature.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the cognitive tasks that create the most value in your work
2. Determine when and where you can schedule distraction-free work periods
3. Remove or minimize all potential distractions for focused work sessions
4. Develop rituals or routines that help you transition into deep work mode
5. Set specific goals for each deep work session
6. Practice extending the length of deep work sessions gradually
7. Create accountability mechanisms to maintain deep work commitment
8. Establish boundaries with others during deep work periods
9. Plan when to switch from deep work to other types of tasks
10. Regularly assess and refine your deep work approach based on effectiveness

## Coaching Questions
- What work activities would benefit most from deeper concentration?
- How much uninterrupted time do you typically allow for cognitively demanding tasks?
- What distractions prevent you from engaging in deep work regularly?
- What would change if you dedicated specific time blocks to deep work?
- How do you currently handle interruptions when trying to focus deeply?
- What types of work do you do when you're unable to focus deeply?
- How do you feel about your current level of work depth versus shallowness?
- What changes to your schedule might be needed to accommodate deep work?
- How do you measure the value created during deep work versus shallow work?
- What would happen if you doubled the amount of deep work in your schedule?

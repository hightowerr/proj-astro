# Deep Work

## Description
Deep Work is sustained, distraction-free concentration on cognitively demanding tasks. The point is not “working hard” in general, but protecting uninterrupted attention long enough to produce high-quality output and to improve skill through deliberate practice. It contrasts with shallow work: logistical, reactive tasks that can be done while distracted and often create the illusion of productivity.

Implementation typically follows one of four approaches:

- **Monastic:** remove most shallow obligations to create long stretches of focus.
- **Bimodal:** split time into clearly separated deep periods and everything else.
- **Rhythmic:** build a repeatable daily/weekly routine (same time, same place).
- **Journalistic:** insert deep work whenever time appears, requiring rapid ramp-up.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When you must be continuously reachable (support, incident response, certain management roles).
- When work quality depends on tight, frequent collaboration and rapid iteration with others.
- When you’re new to a role and still need high exposure to people, processes, and context.
- When client work requires spontaneity and unpredictable scheduling.
- During organizational crises where fast triage and flexibility matter more than protected focus.
- When you’ll create hidden costs by going offline without explicit coordination norms.

## Thinking Steps
1. Assess your current work schedule and identify time blocks suitable for deep work
2. Determine which type of deep work philosophy suits your role and lifestyle best
3. Create physical and digital environments that minimize distractions
4. Develop rituals and routines that signal the beginning of deep work sessions
5. Establish rules and processes for managing interruptions during deep work
6. Identify your most important cognitively demanding tasks for focus
7. Schedule deep work sessions in advance and protect them fiercely
8. Establish rules for shallow work to prevent it from overtaking your schedule
9. Cultivate the ability to quickly shift into focused mode when needed
10. Regularly assess and adjust your deep work practices based on results

## Coaching Questions
- What specific task would improve most from 60–120 minutes of uninterrupted focus?
- Which shallow activities are pretending to be important because they are urgent?
- What would “good output” from the next deep session look like in one sentence?
- Which philosophy fits your reality right now: monastic, bimodal, rhythmic, or journalistic?
- What are the top two predictable interruption sources, and what rule would block each?
- What does your environment currently cue you to do: focus or check messages?
- If you protected one deep block per day, what would you stop doing to make room?
- Where is responsiveness genuinely required, and where is it just habit or culture?
- How will you communicate availability so deep work doesn’t create hidden coordination costs?
- What evidence would tell you deep work is working: faster learning, fewer errors, better outputs?

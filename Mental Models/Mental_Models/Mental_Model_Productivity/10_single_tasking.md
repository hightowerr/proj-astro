# Single Tasking

## Description
Single Tasking is a focused work methodology involving concentrating on one task at a time with full attention and concentration until completion, rather than attempting to multitask. This approach emphasizes doing one thing at a time with complete focus, devotion, and dedication. The concept is based on research showing that multitasking reduces both the quality and efficiency of work output. Single Tasking involves creating a to-do list of activities, focusing on accomplishing the list while eliminating all distractions, and allowing the mind to fully engage in the task at hand without mental switching costs.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Avoid this approach when working in an environment with constant interruptions that make sustained focus impossible. Use caution if your role requires rapid context switching or immediate responsiveness to urgent matters. This model may not be appropriate for roles that naturally involve juggling multiple simultaneous streams of work. Don't apply when external factors (deadlines, emergencies, team requirements) necessitate rapid task switching. Be cautious when your work involves many small, quick tasks that would be more efficiently handled together. Avoid if your position requires simultaneous monitoring of multiple ongoing processes or situations.

## Thinking Steps
1. Create a clear to-do list of activities you need to accomplish
2. Identify the most important task that deserves your focused attention
3. Remove all potential distractions (notifications, phones, unnecessary tabs)
4. Commit to focusing on just one task until completion
5. Set a specific time period for dedicated single-tasking
6. Allow your brain to fully engage with the task at hand
7. Resist the urge to check other tasks or communications during the focused session
8. Monitor how long you can maintain single-tasking focus without distraction
9. Evaluate the quality and speed of work when single-tasking versus multitasking
10. Adjust the length and frequency of single-tasking sessions based on effectiveness

## Coaching Questions
- What is your current relationship with multitasking, and how does it affect your work?
- Which tasks in your current workflow would benefit most from single-tasking focus?
- What distractions commonly interrupt your ability to focus on a single task?
- How do you currently handle interruptions when trying to focus?
- What would happen if you committed to single-tasking for your most important work?
- How do you feel about your productivity when you multitask versus single-task?
- What changes could you make to your environment to support single-tasking?
- How might single-tasking affect the quality of your work output?
- What strategies do you have for maintaining focus when under pressure?
- How do you measure the effectiveness of single-tasking versus multitasking?

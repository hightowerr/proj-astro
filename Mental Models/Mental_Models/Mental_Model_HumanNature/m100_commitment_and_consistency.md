**Category:** Human Nature

**Description:**
People feel internal and social pressure to behave in line with their prior commitments—especially when those commitments are **active (spoken/written), public, and voluntary**. Even very small initial “yeses” (signing a petition, agreeing to learn more) increase the likelihood of later, larger compliance (donating, showing up, buying). This “foot-in-the-door” dynamic works because we want to see ourselves—and be seen by others—as consistent. 

* **When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

  * When a public commitment could lock you into a *bad* or outdated course (risk of escalation of commitment). 
  * When the initial “yes” is not truly voluntary; coerced or deceptive setups can backfire and are unethical. 
  * When you need openness to disconfirming evidence; strong consistency pressure can blind leaders and teams. 

* **Keywords for Situations:**
  Sales funnels; fundraising; onboarding; habit formation; goal setting; *implementation intentions* (“make a plan”); product launches; petitions/surveys; voter turnout; public pledges; written agreements. 

* **Thinking Steps:**

  1. **Define the single behavior** you want (e.g., “attend training”, “vote at 7:30am at X location”). 
  2. **Elicit a small, easy commitment** (“Can we count on you to…?”) that aligns with the target behavior. 
  3. **Make it active, public, voluntary:** have people *write it down*, *say it aloud*, or *share it*—never coerce. 
  4. **Lock in specifics with a plan** (implementation intentions): time, place, route, potential obstacles. 
  5. **Follow up with reminders** that mirror their own words (“You said you’d…”) and reduce friction to act. 
  6. **Provide an easy, ethical path to consistency** (clear next step, low effort) and **an exit ramp** to prevent unhealthy escalation. 

* **Coaching Questions:**

  * What’s the *smallest* meaningful first step they can say “yes” to today? 
  * How can we make that commitment **active, public, and voluntary** (not pressured)? 
  * What concrete **plan** (when/where/how) will help them follow through when life gets busy? 
  * How will we phrase reminders to reflect **their own prior words** rather than our demands? 
  * Where might consistency bias lead us astray—and what checkpoints will let us **reconsider** without shame?

---
model: Authority
category: Psychology, Persuasion
detectors:
- You are deferring to someone's "expert" opinion without critical thought.
- A person's title (Dr., Prof., CEO) or uniform (lab coat, police uniform) is heavily influencing your behavior.
- You feel a strong sense of obligation to comply, even if the request makes you uncomfortable.
- The use of expensive "trappings" of status (e.g., fancy car, suit) to signal authority.
triggers:
- A command from someone in a position of power.
- The presence of symbols of authority: titles, clothes, and trappings.
- An endorsement from a recognized expert.
failure_modes:
- Can lead to blind obedience, where people follow orders that are unethical or harmful (e.g., Milgram experiment).
- Authority can be easily faked with symbols, leading you to trust non-experts.
- Can lead to "Captainitis," where a subordinate fails to correct an obvious error made by a leader, potentially leading to disaster.
- Suppresses critical thinking and personal responsibility.
aliases:
- Directed Deference
- Expert Opinion
- Blind Obedience
- Milgram's Ghost
core_mechanics:
- We are socialized from birth to obey legitimate authorities.
- Obeying authority is a mental shortcut that is often beneficial (e.g., following a doctor's advice).
- We often respond to the mere symbols of authority rather than the substance of it.
---
**Description**: The Authority principle describes our deep-seated sense of duty to authority. We are trained from birth that obedience to proper authority is right and disobedience is wrong. This creates a powerful mental shortcut: it is often beneficial to follow the advice of genuine experts and authority figures. However, this tendency can be exploited. Compliance professionals can trigger our automatic deference to authority by displaying its symbols, such as titles (Dr., Judge), clothing (uniforms, well-tailored business suits), and trappings (expensive cars, fancy offices), even when they lack true substance.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Always be cautious when an authority figure asks you to do something that makes you uncomfortable or seems wrong. The biggest danger is mindlessly obeying without engaging your own critical thinking. It is crucial to check if the authority is legitimate and relevant. Just because someone is an expert in one field doesn't mean their authority transfers to another. Remember that symbols of authority can be easily faked. The defense is to ask two key questions: "Is this authority truly an expert?" and "How truthful can we expect this expert to be here?" (i.e., do they have anything to gain?).

---

**Keywords for Situations**: Expert Advice, Medical Opinions, Legal Counsel, Leadership, Hierarchies, Marketing (expert endorsements), Compliance, Rules and Regulations.

---

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Identify the Authority Figure and Symbols**: Who is presenting themselves as an authority? What symbols are they using to convey this (title, uniform, etc.)?
2.  **Question the Authority's Legitimacy**: Ask the first key question: "Is this person a genuine expert in this specific field?" Look at their credentials and their relevance to the situation at hand.
3.  **Question the Authority's Trustworthiness**: Ask the second key question: "How much can I trust this expert's motives in this situation?" Do they have a vested interest in your compliance? Could they be biased?
4.  **Evaluate the Request on Its Own Merits**: Temporarily remove the authority from the equation. Does the request still make sense? Is it logical and ethical?
5.  **Seek Countervailing Opinions**: Are there other experts who hold a different view? A consensus of trustworthy experts is more reliable than a single opinion.

---

**Coaching Questions**:
*   Am I obeying the person or their title/uniform?
*   What are this person's actual credentials, and are they relevant right now?
*   What does this authority have to gain if I comply with their request?
*   If this person were an ordinary citizen, would I still follow their instructions?
*   Am I thinking for myself, or am I just deferring to the "expert"?
*   Is it possible this authority figure is wrong? What would the consequences be?

# Mental Accounting

**Description:**
Mental Accounting is the tendency for people to categorize and handle money differently depending on where it came from, where it is kept, or how it is intended to be spent. People treat money from different sources (salary vs. bonus vs. inheritance) as if they were not fungible, even though money is interchangeable. This behavioral bias leads to irrational financial decisions, such as spending windfall gains frivolously while being frugal with regular income, or keeping money in low-interest savings accounts while carrying high-interest debt simultaneously.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When making financial decisions based on the source of money rather than its actual value
- When treating money differently based on mental categories rather than economic reality
- When avoiding necessary purchases with one type of money while spending unnecessarily with another
- When making investment decisions based on different mental accounts rather than overall portfolio optimization
- When failing to consider opportunity costs across different mental accounts

**Keywords for Situations:**
Personal finance, budgeting, spending, saving, investing, cognitive biases, behavioral economics, money management, opportunity cost, financial decision-making, mental budgets, psychological accounting.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Recognize Mental Accounts:** Identify how you separate money into different mental categories.
2. **Treat Money as Fungible:** Remember that money is interchangeable regardless of source or intended use.
3. **Evaluate Overall Impact:** Consider how spending decisions affect your total financial situation.
4. **Apply Consistent Standards:** Use the same criteria for evaluating spending regardless of money source.
5. **Calculate Opportunity Costs:** Consider what you're giving up when spending money in one area versus another.
6. **Align with Financial Goals:** Ensure spending decisions support your overall financial objectives.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Am I treating money differently based on where it came from or what I intended to use it for?"
* "How would I evaluate this purchase if the money came from a different source?"
* "What is the opportunity cost of spending this money in this area versus another?"
* "Am I being consistent in my spending standards across different sources of money?"
* "Is this expenditure aligned with my overall financial goals regardless of the source?"
* "Would I make this decision if I viewed all money as completely fungible?"

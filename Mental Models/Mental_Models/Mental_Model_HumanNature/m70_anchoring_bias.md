
# Anchoring Bias

**Description**: A cognitive bias where an individual depends too heavily on an initial piece of information offered (the "anchor") when making decisions. Subsequent judgments are made by adjusting away from that anchor, and there is a common cognitive error of making insufficient adjustments. The book "Bulletproof Problem Solving" mentions this as a cause of flawed problem solving, for example, by "selecting a numerical range you have seen already."

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This is a cognitive bias to be aware of and actively mitigate, not a tool to be used (except perhaps strategically in a negotiation). Be especially wary in any negotiation, when evaluating something with a stated price, or when considering an initial forecast or estimate.

**Keywords for Situations**: Negotiation, pricing, forecasting, budgeting, valuation, setting initial estimates, haggling.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  In any decision involving numbers, consciously identify the first number presented. This is the anchor.
2.  Actively question the validity and source of the anchor. Is it based on facts, or is it arbitrary? Don't assume the initial number is rational.
3.  Before looking at the anchor (if possible), generate your own independent estimate or position based on first principles or your own data.
4.  When presented with an anchor, try to reframe the problem from a different perspective to generate new starting points. For example, instead of focusing on the sticker price of a car, focus on what your budget is or what the dealer's invoice price might be.
5.  In a group setting, have people write down their own estimates before any numbers are shared aloud to avoid anchoring the entire group to one person's initial thought.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "What was the first number we heard in this discussion? Are we unconsciously anchored to it?"
*   "Let's ignore their initial offer for a moment. Based on our own analysis, what do we think this is worth from the ground up?"
*   "Where did that initial forecast come from? Let's challenge the assumptions behind it instead of just making small adjustments to it."
*   "Before anyone says a number, let's all write down our independent estimates and reveal them at the same time."

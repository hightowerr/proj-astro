# Do Something Bias

**Description:**
Do Something Bias is the tendency to want to take action or make decisions even when the best course may be to do nothing or wait. This bias stems from the human desire to feel productive and avoid the discomfort of inaction. People often feel that doing something is better than doing nothing, even when the action may be counterproductive or when waiting for more information would be more beneficial. This bias can manifest as a need to "do something" regardless of whether the action is helpful, particularly in situations where inaction would be more appropriate.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When time and patience are needed for information to reveal itself
- When market fluctuations require a "buy and hold" strategy
- When complex problems need time to unravel naturally
- When making investment decisions during market volatility
- When facing problems that require contemplation rather than immediate action

**Keywords for Situations:**
Decision paralysis avoidance, market timing, investment decisions, management decisions, panic responses, emotional reactions, overtrading, premature interventions, hasty decisions, action bias, restlessness, impatience, urgency.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Assess Urgency:** Determine if the situation genuinely requires immediate action or if time can be taken.
2. **Question the Urge:** When you feel compelled to act, pause and ask whether action is truly needed.
3. **Consider Inaction:** Evaluate the consequences of doing nothing for a predetermined period.
4. **Wait for Conviction:** Only act when you have a clear, rational reason to do so, not just to satisfy the urge to act.
5. **Set Waiting Periods:** Establish specific timeframes to observe before taking action.
6. **Monitor Motivation:** Distinguish between action taken for strategic reasons versus action taken to satisfy emotional needs.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Am I acting out of genuine need or just to feel like I'm doing something?"
* "What would happen if I waited one more day/week/month before acting?"
* "Am I confusing activity with results?"
* "Is this action driven by a strategic need or by my discomfort with inaction?"
* "What is the downside of waiting to act?"
* "Would I be making this decision if I weren't feeling pressured to act?"

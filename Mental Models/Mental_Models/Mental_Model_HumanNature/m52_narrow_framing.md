
# Narrow Framing

**Description**: The tendency to define our choices too narrowly, to see them in binary, "whether or not" terms. It's like shining a spotlight on one or two options while ignoring the wider landscape of possibilities. This is the first villain of decision-making and often manifests as a "whether or not" choice, which research shows leads to a higher failure rate for decisions.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: While widening options is generally good, it can lead to choice paralysis if taken to an extreme. At some point, you must stop generating options and start evaluating them. The goal is to have a sufficient set of *diverse* options, not an infinite one. In a true emergency or a very simple choice, spending time to widen options may be unnecessary.

**Keywords for Situations**: Brainstorming, problem-solving, career planning, investment choices, relationship decisions, feeling "stuck," making a choice between only two options.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Be alert for "whether or not" decisions (e.g., "Should I fire this employee or not?"). This is a red flag for narrow framing.
2.  When you spot a narrow frame, force yourself to generate more alternatives. Ask simply, "What are my other options?"
3.  Use the **Vanishing Options Test**: "If I couldn't choose any of my current options, what else could I do?" This forces your mental spotlight to move.
4.  Consider the **Opportunity Cost**: "What am I giving up by choosing this option? What else could I do with the same time and money?"
5.  Look for ways to combine or modify options using **"AND not OR" thinking**. Instead of choosing between A or B, see if you can do both, or a modified version of both.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "You're asking 'whether or not' to do this. What if that option wasn't available? What would you do then?"
*   "Instead of 'this OR that,' is there a way we could do 'this AND that'?"
*   "What's the opportunity cost here? If we spend these resources on this project, what are we saying 'no' to?"
*   "Let's not decide anything until we have at least three viable options on the table. What else could we do?"

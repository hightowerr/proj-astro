# Status Quo Bias

**Description:**
Status Quo Bias is the human tendency to prefer things to stay the same rather than change. It's rooted in loss aversion and the psychological concept of inertia - the tendency of objects at rest to remain at rest. People often prefer the current state of affairs and resist change, even when change could be beneficial. This bias can prevent people from taking advantage of better opportunities or making improvements because they prefer the comfort and certainty of the current situation, even if it's suboptimal.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When better opportunities exist but are being resisted due to comfort with current situation
- When the status quo is clearly inferior to alternatives
- When change is necessary for growth, survival, or improvement
- When the costs of maintaining status quo outweigh the costs of change
- When external circumstances require adaptation but status quo bias resists necessary change

**Keywords for Situations:**
Change management, decision making, investment decisions, career transitions, habit change, organizational behavior, risk aversion, cognitive biases, behavioral economics, inertia, resistance to change.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Acknowledge the Bias:** Recognize when you or others are defaulting to the status quo out of comfort rather than analysis.
2. **Evaluate Alternatives:** Objectively assess whether the current situation is truly optimal.
3. **Calculate Opportunity Cost:** Consider what is being missed by maintaining the status quo.
4. **Frame Change Positively:** Look for the benefits and opportunities that change could bring.
5. **Address Fear of Loss:** Identify and address specific fears about what might be lost in change.
6. **Take Small Steps:** When appropriate, make gradual changes rather than dramatic shifts.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Am I choosing the status quo because it's better or because it's familiar?"
* "What opportunities am I missing by maintaining the current situation?"
* "What would I recommend someone else do in this situation?"
* "Am I giving sufficient weight to potential benefits of change?"
* "What specific fears am I having about potential changes?"
* "How might I feel about this situation in one year if nothing changes?"

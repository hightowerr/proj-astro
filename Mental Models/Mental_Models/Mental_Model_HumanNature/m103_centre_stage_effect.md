**Description**: Core concept explanation
The Centre-Stage Effect is a cognitive bias in which individuals perceive options or items placed in the physical or visual center of a simultaneously presented array (such as a pricing sheet, product display, or menu) as inherently more important, popular, or desirable than those positioned on the periphery. This preference is driven by a form of marketplace meta-cognition—the implicit belief that marketers typically place the most popular or recommended options in the center. This central placement acts as a powerful default cue, leading consumers to choose the middle option more often because people generally dislike choosing the extremes.[1] The effect is a combination of visual preference, cognitive efficiency, and the tendency to value the most prominent option (focalism).

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):** Situations where the model may mislead or fail
 * High Intrinsic Preference: The preference for the central option is significantly reduced when a person focuses on their own strong, established preferences for the intrinsic attributes of the product, overriding the position-based belief.
 * Layout Non-Diagnostic: The effect is weakened if the display configuration does not clearly suggest a strategic placement by the seller, making the central position less trustworthy or relevant as a cue.
 * Complex Differences: The persuasive power of the central position is maximized when the differences between the options are simple and clearly communicated. If the options are highly complex or difficult to compare, the simple positioning cue may fail to drive the decision.
 
**Keywords for Situations**: Common contexts or triggers where it applies
	Pricing Tiers, Product Layouts, E-commerce Configuration, Visual Preference, Anchoring Bias, Framing Effect, Focalism, Impulse Choice, Implicit Popularity, Subscription Models.

**Thinking Steps**: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively
 1. Identify the Optimal Choice: Determine which option (e.g., a mid-range plan or product with the highest margin) is the target choice for conversion.
 2. Center the Target Option: Place this target option in the visual center of the array to leverage the inherent bias toward the middle position.
 3. Verify Simplicity: Ensure the functional or feature differences between the options are simple and clearly communicated, as clarity maximizes the persuasive power of the central placement cue.
 4. Layer Secondary Biases: Strengthen the effect by having the central option also serve as a cognitive Anchor (a moderate price reference point) or by using the placement to Frame it as the most balanced or professional choice.
 5. Evaluate for Implied Popularity: Confirm that the central placement implicitly signals to the user that this is the popular, safe, or recommended default option, fulfilling the consumer's meta-cognition about marketer strategy.

**Coaching Questions**: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model
 * Am I treating the centrally placed option as a default because I believe it’s what others are choosing, rather than based on my own detailed analysis of its features?
 * Does the physical or digital positioning of this option suggest implicit importance or popularity?
 * What happens if I focus purely on the intrinsic attributes of the products—does the center still feel like the best choice, or is the position skewing my judgment?
 * Is the choice presented simply enough that I can rely on the center cue, or does the complexity force me to abandon the visual shortcut?
 * How is the central option Framing my view of the remaining options (e.g., does it make the high-priced option seem excessive or the low-priced option seem insufficient)?

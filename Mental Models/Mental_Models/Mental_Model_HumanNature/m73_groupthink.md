
# Groupthink

**Description**: A psychological phenomenon that occurs within a group of people in which the desire for harmony or conformity results in an irrational or dysfunctional decision-making outcome. Dissenting views are discouraged, and the group isolates itself from outside opinions. The book "Bulletproof Problem Solving" mentions it in the context of a team of managers with similar backgrounds and a traditional hierarchy making it "hard for them to see the real alternatives clearly," leading to poor decisions.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This is a dysfunctional pattern to be actively avoided in any team-based decision-making process. It is especially dangerous in cohesive, homogenous groups that are insulated from outside opinions and are working on high-stakes decisions under pressure.

**Keywords for Situations**: Team meetings, strategic planning, committee decisions, cohesive groups, high-stakes decisions, corporate culture, brainstorming, crisis management.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Be aware of the symptoms of groupthink: pressure on dissenters, self-censorship, an illusion of unanimity, and stereotyping of outsiders or opponents.
2.  The leader of the group should intentionally withhold their own opinion at the beginning of a discussion to avoid anchoring the group and encouraging conformity.
3.  Actively encourage and reward dissent. Assign one or more members the formal role of **"devil's advocate"** to challenge assumptions and arguments.
4.  Break the group into smaller, independent teams to work on the same problem, then come together to compare their conclusions.
5.  Bring in outside experts or fresh perspectives to challenge the group's assumptions and introduce new information.
6.  Before a final decision, hold a "second-chance" meeting where members are encouraged to express any remaining doubts or second thoughts.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "It seems like we're all agreeing very quickly here. What are we missing? What is the strongest argument against this plan?"
*   "For this discussion, I want you to be the devil's advocate. Your only job is to find every flaw in this plan and argue against it forcefully."
*   "Before we finalize this, let's go around the room and have everyone state one potential risk or downside of this plan that we haven't discussed enough."
*   "Are we sure we're not just agreeing because it's easier? What would a real, healthy debate on this topic sound like?"

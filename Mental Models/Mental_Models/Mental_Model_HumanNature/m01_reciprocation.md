---
model: Reciprocation
category: Psychology, Persuasion
detectors:
- Receiving an unsolicited gift or favor.
- Feeling a sense of obligation or indebtedness after an interaction.
- The phrase "No obligation" which often signals an attempt to trigger one.
- Unequal exchanges where a small favor is followed by a much larger request.
triggers:
- Receiving a gift (free samples, drinks, information).
- Receiving a concession in a negotiation.
- Someone doing you a favor.
failure_modes:
- Can be exploited to create guilt and force compliance for unwanted actions.
- Can lead to unequal exchanges where you give back much more than you received.
- Can inhibit asking for needed help for fear of being unable to repay.
- In communal relationships, strict reciprocation can feel transactional and damage the relationship.
aliases:
- The Old Give and Take
- Quid pro quo
- Indebtedness
core_mechanics:
- Humans have a deeply ingrained social rule to repay favors and concessions.
- Violating this rule leads to social disapproval (being seen as a "moocher" or "ingrate").
- An uninvited favor still creates an obligation to receive and an obligation to repay.
- The psychological discomfort of being indebted motivates us to settle the debt, sometimes by overcompensating.
---
**Description**: The Reciprocation Principle is a fundamental social rule that obligates people to repay what they have received from others. This applies to gifts, favors, invitations, and even concessions in a negotiation. This "web of indebtedness" is a unique adaptive mechanism of human societies, allowing for division of labor, exchange of goods and services, and the creation of interdependencies that bind individuals together. The rule is overpowering and can make us comply with requests from people we don't even like.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Use caution when a favor is unsolicited or comes from a person or organization with a clear motive to influence you (e.g., a salesperson, a political lobbyist). The favor may be a manipulation tactic designed to indebt you into a larger, unequal exchange. In these cases, you can mentally reframe the "gift" as a sales tactic, which frees you from the psychological obligation to reciprocate. Also, in long-term communal relationships like close friendships and family, keeping a strict, transactional ledger of who-owes-who can be inappropriate and even harmful to the relationship. The exchange in these contexts is based on mutual support as needed, not a tit-for-tat accounting.

---

**Keywords for Situations**: Negotiation, Sales, Fundraising, Networking, Relationship Building, Team Collaboration, Due Diligence, Compliance Requests, Favors.

---

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Identify the Initial Act**: Recognize when you have received something—a gift, a favor, a concession.
2.  **Assess the Giver and Intent**: Who provided it? What is their likely motivation? Is this a genuine act from a friend or colleague, or is it a compliance professional attempting to create an obligation?
3.  **Acknowledge the Feeling of Obligation**: Notice the internal psychological pressure to repay. Understand this is a normal, deeply socialized response.
4.  **Separate the Gift from the Giver**: If you determine the initial act was a compliance tactic, make a mental separation. The "gift" is not a gift; it is a persuasive device. You are not obligated to respond in kind to a trick.
5.  **Evaluate the Follow-Up Request**: If the initial act is followed by a request, evaluate the request on its own merits, completely independent of the fact you were given something. Would you comply with this request if they hadn't given you that "gift"?
6.  **Choose Your Response**: If the initial act was genuine, reciprocate in a proportional and appropriate way to maintain the social bond. If it was a manipulative tactic, you are free to either decline the follow-up request or comply if it's something you wanted to do anyway, without feeling like you "owe" them. You can even accept the initial item and thank them for the "promotional item" without feeling indebted.

---

**Coaching Questions**:
*   What have I been given here, and by whom?
*   Was this an unsolicited favor? What might be the giver's motivation?
*   Do I feel a sense of obligation? Is that feeling being used to influence me?
*   If I did not receive this initial favor, would I still be willing to agree to their subsequent request?
*   Is the return favor being asked of me proportional to the initial favor?
*   How can I accept this person's gesture without accepting the obligation they are trying to impose?
*   In this negotiation, is the other party's concession genuine, or is it a setup for the Rejection-then-Retreat tactic?


# Availability Bias

**Description**: A cognitive bias where people overestimate the importance of information that is most readily available to them. We give undue weight to recent, vivid, or emotionally charged examples when making decisions, rather than more representative statistical data. The book "Bulletproof Problem Solving" mentions this in the context of incorrectly "asserting the answer" based on a memorable past experience ("I've seen this before") without testing if the situation is truly analogous.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This is a cognitive bias to be aware of and actively mitigate, not a tool to be used. Heuristics based on immediate experience can be fast, but they are often unreliable for complex problems and can lead to significant errors in judgment, particularly in risk assessment and strategic planning.

**Keywords for Situations**: Decision making, risk assessment, brainstorming, relying on anecdotes, strategic planning, forecasting, performance reviews.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  When making a judgment or decision, pause and ask: "Is my conclusion based on a vivid memory, a recent event, or a powerful story?"
2.  Actively seek out less available, but potentially more relevant, base-rate information or statistical data. For example, instead of relying on one salient news story about a market crash, look at the long-term historical data.
3.  Challenge analogies. When someone says "I've seen this before," ask, "How is this situation *different* from the one you remember?"
4.  Consult with others who have different experiences and memories to broaden the pool of available information and counter your own personal availability bias.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Are we overweighting this recent example? What does the broader data say over the last five years?"
*   "You're saying 'you've seen this before.' Let's spend five minutes brainstorming all the ways this current problem is different from that past experience."
*   "Is our thinking being influenced by a particularly memorable success or failure, rather than the typical outcome?"
*   "Let's try to find data instead of relying on anecdotes for this decision."

---
model: Liking
category: Psychology, Persuasion
detectors:
- You feel a strong, positive connection to a salesperson or requester very quickly.
- A requester highlights similarities between you ("You're from Ohio? I'm from Ohio!").
- You are receiving many compliments.
- You find yourself agreeing to a request from someone you find physically attractive.
- The use of "good cop/bad cop" routines.
triggers:
- Interacting with someone who is physically attractive.
- Discovering a similarity (hometown, hobby, political view).
- Being complimented.
- Cooperating with someone toward a mutual goal.
- Positive association with a liked person, brand, or celebrity (celebrity endorsements).
failure_modes:
- Can cause you to make poor decisions by agreeing to requests from people you like, even if the request is not in your best interest.
- It's easy to be manipulated by insincere compliments or manufactured similarities.
- The "halo effect" from physical attractiveness can cause us to automatically assign other positive traits like talent, kindness, and intelligence to attractive people.
- Guilt by association can work in reverse, causing us to dislike people or products associated with things we dislike.
aliases:
- The Friendly Thief
- In-group favoritism
- Halo Effect
- Association Principle
core_mechanics:
- We are more likely to be influenced by and say "yes" to people we know and like.
- Several factors increase liking: Physical Attractiveness, Similarity, Compliments, Contact and Cooperation, and Conditioning and Association.
- Compliance professionals can exploit this by creating a manufactured sense of liking to gain compliance.
---
**Description**: The Liking principle states that we prefer to say yes to the requests of people we know and like. This is one of the most common and effective influence tools. Compliance professionals take advantage of this by identifying and leveraging factors that cause us to like someone. These factors include physical attractiveness (we automatically assign positive traits to good-looking people), similarity (we like people who are like us), compliments (we are suckers for flattery), positive contact and cooperation (we like people we work with toward common goals), and association (we like people and things connected to other things we like, such as celebrity endorsements).

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: The primary danger is failing to separate your feelings about the requester from the merits of the request itself. Be especially alert when you find yourself liking a requester more than you'd expect, especially in a short amount of time or in a situation where they stand to gain from your compliance (like a sale). This is a red flag that influence tactics may be at play. The appropriate defense is to notice the liking, mentally separate the person from the product or request, and then evaluate the request on its own objective merits.

---

**Keywords for Situations**: Sales (especially relationship-based), Networking, Friendships, Team Building, Politics, Negotiation, Marketing.

---

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Acknowledge the Liking**: Step back and ask yourself: "Do I like this person?"
2.  **Assess the Degree and Speed of Liking**: "In the time I've known this person, have I come to like them more than I would have expected?"
3.  **Identify the Source of the Liking**: If you feel an unusual level of liking, try to identify the cause. Have they complimented you? Highlighted similarities? Are they particularly attractive? Are they associating themselves with something you like?
4.  **Mentally Separate the Requester from the Request**: This is the key defensive maneuver. Say to yourself, "Okay, this person is very nice, but putting them aside, is their offer a good one for me?"
5.  **Make the Decision Based on Merits**: Base your final decision on the value and logic of the offer itself, not on your positive feelings for the person making it.

---

**Coaching Questions**:
*   Do I feel a special connection to this person? Why?
*   What has this person done or said to make me like them?
*   If someone I disliked made this exact same offer, how would I feel about it?
*   Am I focusing more on the person making the request or on the request itself?
*   Is my decision being influenced by a positive association that is irrelevant to the quality of the deal (e.g., a celebrity endorsement)?

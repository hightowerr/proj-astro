# Framing Effect

**Description:**
Framing Effect is a cognitive bias where people react to a particular choice in different ways depending on how it's presented (framed). The same information, when presented in terms of gain or loss, can lead to different decisions. For example, people react differently to a medical procedure described as having a "90% survival rate" versus a "10% mortality rate," even though these are equivalent. This bias shows that the way information is structured and presented significantly influences decision-making.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When making important decisions based on how information is presented rather than its substance
- When information is presented in an emotionally charged way to manipulate decisions
- When the frame is designed to hide important information or alternatives
- When making investment or financial decisions based on how options are presented
- When evaluating options that have been selectively framed to favor certain outcomes

**Keywords for Situations:**
Decision making, risk assessment, marketing, advertising, news reporting, political messaging, negotiations, financial planning, medical decisions, cognitive biases, behavioral economics, information presentation.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify the Frame:** Recognize how information is being presented and what frame is being used.
2. **Reframe the Information:** Flip the frame to see the same information presented differently (e.g., from loss to gain perspective).
3. **Focus on Substance:** Extract the core factual information regardless of how it's presented.
4. **Look for Missing Information:** Consider what might have been left out of the frame.
5. **Use Multiple Frames:** Evaluate the same information using different framing perspectives.
6. **Make Independent Assessment:** Form your own judgment based on core facts rather than presentation.

**Coaching Questions:**
* How is this information being framed? What is the presentation emphasizing?
* How would I view this if it were framed as a loss instead of a gain?
* What facts remain the same regardless of how this is presented?
* What alternative framings could exist for this situation?
* Am I making decisions based on the frame or the underlying facts?
* What would this look like from the opposite perspective?

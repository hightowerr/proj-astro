---
model: Skin in the Game
category: ["Human Nature", "Ethics", "Risk"]
detectors:
  - Someone makes decisions or predictions that impose downside on others but not on themselves
  - Rewards are immediate and visible; harms are delayed and externalized
  - Advice is cheap, confident, and consequence-free
triggers:
  - Selecting advisors, forecasters, leaders, and vendors
  - Designing incentives and accountability
  - Evaluating “expert” recommendations in opaque systems
failure_modes:
  - Mistaking credentials and narratives for embodied exposure
  - Creating asymmetric payoffs (people become antifragile at others’ expense)
  - Applying skin-in-the-game rules in contexts where liability is not feasible (need substitutes)
aliases:
  - "Soul in the Game"
  - Captain and Ship Rule
core_mechanics:
  - Ethical systems require symmetry: decision makers share downside with those affected
  - Absence of exposure enables hidden risk-taking and fragility transfer
  - Talk without risk separates knowing from doing and increases societal fragility
---

### Description

Skin in the game is the principle that people who make decisions, give advice, or benefit from a system should also bear meaningful downside when things go wrong. It is an ethical and practical risk-management rule: it reduces hidden fragility by aligning incentives and preventing the transfer of tail risk to others.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When strict liability is impractical; use alternative alignment mechanisms rather than abandoning the principle.
- When punishment is retrospective and doesn’t change incentives (the goal is ex-ante alignment).

---

### Keywords for Situations

Incentives, accountability, agency, fragility transfer, asymmetric payoffs, opinion markets, liability, alignment.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Trace who gets upside and who gets downside**
   Map beneficiaries and victims, including delayed and hidden harms.
2. **Identify “optionality at others’ expense”**
   Look for actors who win when right and don’t pay when wrong.
3. **Require exposure or alignment**
   Prefer advisors and decision makers who are materially exposed to outcomes (or have reputational/financial liability).
4. **Apply the Captain-and-Ship heuristic**
   If the operator isn’t on board, question safety; require shared fate in high-stakes contexts.
5. **Design incentives for tail risks**
   Align outcomes over long horizons; avoid paying for short-term gains that hide long-term fragility.
6. **Use substitutes when needed**
   Where direct skin in the game isn’t possible, use independent audits, redundancy, and constraints that cap harm.

---

### Coaching Questions

- Who benefits if this works—and who pays if it fails?
- What downside does the decision maker personally face?
- Are rewards immediate but risks delayed?
- If we had to enforce symmetry, what rule would we implement?

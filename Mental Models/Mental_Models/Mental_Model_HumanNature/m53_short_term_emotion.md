
# Short-Term Emotion

**Description**: The third villain of decision-making, where immediate, visceral feelings like anger, fear, excitement, or anxiety cloud our judgment. When in the grip of short-term emotion, we lose perspective, our long-term priorities become obscured, and we are tempted to make choices that we may later regret. It's the state of churning over a decision, replaying the same arguments, and changing our minds day to day without any new information.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This model is a diagnostic tool for identifying a flawed state of mind. It is not a solution in itself. It is crucial to distinguish between fleeting, short-term emotions and deep-seated values or principles, which are essential for good decision-making. The goal is not to eliminate emotion, but to prevent transient feelings from overpowering long-term wisdom.

**Keywords for Situations**: Agonizing over a choice, replaying arguments in your head, feeling conflicted, high-stakes negotiations, personal conflicts, impulse buys, making decisions when angry or overly excited.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Acknowledge the presence of strong, immediate emotions. Simply naming the emotion (e.g., "I am feeling angry right now") can begin to lessen its power.
2.  Ask if the feelings are transient. How might you feel about this situation in a day, a week, or a year? This helps separate immediate feelings from long-term consequences.
3.  Use a formal technique to gain distance, such as the **10/10/10 rule**: Ask yourself, "How will I feel about this decision in 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years?"
4.  Shift your perspective to that of an observer. Ask, **"What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?"** This external viewpoint often provides instant clarity.
5.  For organizational or legacy decisions, use a depersonalizing question like Andy Grove's: **"What would our successors do?"** This removes personal feelings and history from the equation.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "This feels like a very emotional decision. What are the core emotions you're feeling right now?"
*   "How can we get some distance from this? Let's try looking at it from an outsider's perspective."
*   "Let's try the 10/10/10 method. How will we feel about this outcome 10 months from now? And in 10 years?"
*   "If you weren't so personally invested, what would the objective advice be here? What would you tell me to do if I were in your shoes?"

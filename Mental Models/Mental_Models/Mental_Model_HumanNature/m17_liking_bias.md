# Liking Bias

**Description:**
Liking Bias is the tendency to say yes to requests from people we like, respect, or find attractive. This bias encompasses several factors that increase our fondness: attractiveness, similarity, compliments, cooperation, and association. People tend to like those who are similar to them, who give them compliments, who cooperate with them, and who like them in return. This bias can lead to poor decisions simply based on the appeal of the person making the request rather than the merit of the request itself.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When making decisions based on the person rather than the proposal
- When someone's charm or likeability influences your judgment about their competence or ideas
- When salespeople, politicians, or others use likability tactics to influence your decisions
- When you're more inclined to trust someone based on superficial similarities
- When physical attractiveness or charm affects your assessment of proposals or people

**Keywords for Situations:**
Sales, negotiations, hiring decisions, political choices, social interactions, decision making, influence, attraction, cognitive biases, behavioral psychology, trust.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Separate Person from Proposal:** Distinguish between how you feel about the person and the merits of their request or proposal.
2. **Evaluate Objectively:** Assess proposals based on facts and logic rather than the person presenting them.
3. **Recognize Tactics:** Be aware when someone is intentionally using likability strategies.
4. **Focus on Merits:** Evaluate the content of requests independently of the presenter.
5. **Assess Qualifications:** Judge people based on their competence rather than their appeal.
6. **Question Decisions:** Reconsider decisions where likeability may have been the primary factor.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Am I making this decision based on the person or the proposal?"
* "Would I feel the same way about this idea if it came from someone I didn't like?"
* "Am I being influenced by charm or attractiveness rather than competence?"
* "What would my decision be if this person were different in appearance or personality?"
* "Have I evaluated the facts independently of my feelings about the person?"
* "Is my positive feeling toward this person clouding my judgment?"

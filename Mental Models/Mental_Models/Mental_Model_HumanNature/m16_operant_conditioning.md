# Operant Conditioning

## Description
Operant conditioning, also referred to as instrumental conditioning, is a learning model that uses rewards and punishments to train a learner to adopt a particular desired behavior. Developed and popularized by American psychologist Edward L. Thorndike, operant conditioning associates a particular behavior with a consequence that may be pleasant or unpleasant. If the consequence is pleasant, it is called a reward or reinforcer, and it encourages the repetition of the behavior. If it is unpleasant, the consequence is a punishment, and it dissuades the learner from repeating the behavior. Reinforcement and punishment may be both positive and negative. Positive reinforcement involves adding something favorable after a behavior, while negative reinforcement involves removing something unfavorable. Positive punishment involves adding something unpleasant after an undesirable behavior, while negative punishment involves removing something favorable. This model helps shape behavior through systematic application of consequences.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
This model should be used with caution when the behavior being shaped is complex and requires creativity or critical thinking. Avoid using it when intrinsic motivation is important for long-term engagement with the activity. Be careful when using it with individuals who may develop dependency on external rewards rather than internal motivation. It may be less effective for tasks requiring innovation or problem-solving where rigid reinforcement patterns could limit creative approaches. Avoid when the goal is to build self-direction and autonomy. Be cautious in situations where the punisher lacks consistency in applying consequences. It may not work well when the person being conditioned has limited control over the targeted behavior or when the desired behavior is already intrinsically rewarding.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the specific behavior you want to encourage or discourage
2. Determine what consequences are currently associated with the behavior
3. Choose the appropriate type of conditioning (positive/negative reinforcement or punishment)
4. Establish clear and consistent consequences for the behavior
5. Ensure the consequences are immediate and proportional to the behavior
6. Monitor the effectiveness of the conditioning approach
7. Adjust the reinforcement schedule as needed for optimal results
8. Gradually reduce reliance on external rewards as the behavior becomes internalized
9. Watch for unintended side effects or negative consequences
10. Transition from external conditioning to internal motivation over time

## Coaching Questions
- What specific behaviors are you trying to encourage or discourage?
- What rewards or consequences are currently in place for these behaviors?
- How could you structure rewards to reinforce the behaviors you want?
- What punishments might be discouraging positive behaviors?
- How immediate and consistent are the consequences for behaviors?
- Are you using positive reinforcement more than punishment?
- How can you gradually shift from external to internal motivation?
- What unintended consequences might your current conditioning be creating?
- How do you know if your conditioning approach is working effectively?
- What would happen if you removed the external rewards or consequences?

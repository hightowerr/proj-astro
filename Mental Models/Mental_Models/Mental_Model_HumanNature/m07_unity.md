---
model: Unity
category: Psychology, Persuasion
detectors:
- A requester emphasizes a shared background, hometown, religion, or family connection.
- Appeals to patriotism, team spirit, or family ties.
- You are asked to do something "for one of our own."
- Brands that create "tribes" or communities around their products.
triggers:
- Kinship (family and blood ties).
- Shared location (hometown, neighborhood, nation).
- Shared experiences, especially co-creation or struggle.
- Any shared identity that makes someone feel like "one of us."
failure_modes:
- Can be used to justify in-group favoritism and out-group hostility.
- Artificial or trivial similarities can be used to create a false sense of unity for manipulative purposes.
- Can lead to poor decisions by prioritizing the group's interests over your own, even when it is not warranted.
- Can be a foundation for cults and extremist groups by creating a powerful "us vs. them" mentality.
aliases:
- Shared Identity
- The "We" Relationship
- In-Group Favoritism
- Tribalism
core_mechanics:
- The more we perceive someone as being part of "us" (i.e., sharing an identity), the more we are willing to be influenced by them.
- This is a step beyond "Liking." It's not about liking someone who is *similar* to us, but about them *being* us.
- Appeals to family and kinship are the most powerful form of Unity.
- Co-creating or going through a challenging experience with others can also generate a powerful sense of unity.
---
**Description**: Unity is the principle of shared identity. It is the seventh, and most recently added, principle of influence. It states that the more we perceive a person as being part of "us," the more we are willing to be influenced by them. This shared sense of "we" can be based on family (the most powerful form of unity), shared location (hometown, nationality), shared religion, or any other form of group identity. When a requester can create a sense of shared identity with their audience, their persuasive power increases dramatically because they are seen not as an outsider, but as "one of us," and we are more inclined to help our own.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Be cautious when someone is making a strong appeal to a shared identity to gain your compliance, especially if the link seems superficial. This can be a powerful tool for manipulation, fostering in-group favoritism while justifying prejudice against out-groups. It's important to separate the genuine bonds of community from artificial attempts to manufacture a "we" relationship for personal gain. Always evaluate the request on its own merits, independent of the shared identity you may have with the requester.

---

**Keywords for Situations**: Family Relationships, Community Building, Teamwork, Nationalism, Brand Loyalty (tribes), Cults, Political Movements, Co-creation.

---

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Identify the "Unity" Appeal**: What shared identity is the requester invoking? Is it family, location, religion, or another group?
2.  **Assess the Genuineness of the Connection**: Is this a deep, meaningful shared identity, or is it a superficial one being highlighted for persuasive effect?
3.  **Evaluate the Requester's Motive**: Is this person appealing to our shared identity to build a genuine connection and for mutual benefit, or are they trying to use it to get something from me?
4.  **Separate the Relationship from the Request**: Even if the shared identity is real and important, evaluate the request on its own. Is this request truly in my best interest, or the group's best interest, or does it primarily serve the requester?
5.  **Consider the Out-Group Implications**: Is this appeal to unity being used to create a harmful "us vs. them" dynamic?

---

**Coaching Questions**:
*   What shared identity does this person and I have?
*   Is this shared identity being used to make me more compliant?
*   Would I agree to this request if it came from someone outside my "group"?
*   Is this request good for me personally, or am I being asked to sacrifice for the "group" in a way that is not fair?
*   Is this sense of unity being used to create positive community or to foster division and exclusion?

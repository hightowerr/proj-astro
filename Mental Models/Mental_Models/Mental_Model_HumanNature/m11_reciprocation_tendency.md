# Reciprocation Tendency

**Description:**
Reciprocation Tendency is a powerful psychological bias that compels people to repay, in kind, what another person has provided to them. This deeply ingrained social norm makes people feel obligated to return favors, gifts, or kindnesses. The rule operates automatically and powerfully, often producing a "yes" response to requests that would have surely been refused if no prior favor had been given. This tendency is leveraged by salespeople, marketers, and negotiators who offer small gifts or favors first to increase compliance with later requests.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When someone offers a gift, discount, or favor that creates an obligation to reciprocate with a purchase or agreement
- When making decisions based on feeling obligated rather than on merit
- When others try to create artificial obligations through small favors
- When you're vulnerable to the psychological pressure of reciprocal exchange
- When the initial "gift" is designed to manipulate rather than genuinely help

**Keywords for Situations:**
Sales tactics, negotiations, gift giving, social interactions, compliance, marketing, influence, bargaining, cognitive biases, behavioral psychology, social norms, manipulation.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Recognize Unwanted Obligations:** Identify when someone is attempting to create a reciprocal obligation you don't want.
2. **Separate Gift from Request:** Evaluate the actual value of the request separate from the initial favor.
3. **Assert Independence:** Make decisions based on merit, not obligation.
4. **Politely Decline:** Learn to politely refuse reciprocation when it's not in your interest.
5. **Assess Motives:** Consider whether the initial "gift" was genuinely generous or manipulative.
6. **Maintain Standards:** Keep your normal decision-making criteria regardless of any supposed obligations.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Am I feeling obligated to return a favor that was designed to manipulate?"
* "Would I make this decision without the influence of the initial favor?"
* "What is the actual value of what I'm being asked for, separate from the gift received?"
* "Am I making this choice based on merit or on feeling indebted?"
* "How can I politely decline without appearing ungrateful?"
* "Was the initial gift given with the genuine intention to be helpful?"

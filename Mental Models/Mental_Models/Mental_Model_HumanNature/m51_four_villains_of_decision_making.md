
# The Four Villains of Decision Making

**Description**: A framework identifying the four most common and pernicious biases that consistently undermine our ability to make good choices. They are: (1) Narrow Framing, which makes us miss options; (2) The Confirmation Bias, which leads us to gather self-serving information; (3) Short-Term Emotion, which tempts us to make the wrong choice; and (4) Overconfidence, which makes us too certain about an unpredictable future. The WRAP process is designed specifically to counteract these four villains.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This model is a diagnostic tool for identifying potential flaws in a decision process. It is not a solution in itself. Avoid using it to simply label a problem without moving on to a solution-oriented framework like WRAP. It is most useful for analyzing decisions of consequence, not minor, everyday choices.

**Keywords for Situations**: Decision analysis, post-mortems, self-reflection, coaching others on decisions, identifying cognitive biases, strategic planning.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  When facing a significant choice, pause and consider which of the four villains is most likely to be at play.
2.  **Narrow Framing**: Am I only considering one or two options? Am I asking "whether or not" to do something, which limits my choices?
3.  **Confirmation Bias**: Am I only looking for information that supports my gut feeling? Have I actively sought out dissenting opinions or facts that challenge my assumptions?
4.  **Short-Term Emotion**: Is my judgment being clouded by strong, immediate feelings like anger, excitement, or anxiety? Are these feelings transient?
5.  **Overconfidence**: Am I being too certain about my predictions for the future? Have I considered the full range of possible outcomes, both good and bad?

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Let's look at this through the lens of the 'Four Villains.' Which one do you think is most likely to trip us up here?"
*   "Are we falling into a narrow frame? What other options might we be missing?"
*   "To fight the confirmation bias, let's play devil's advocate. What are the strongest arguments *against* what we want to do?"
*   "This is an emotional decision. How can we step back and get some distance before we commit?"
*   "We seem very confident this will work. What if we're wrong? What might the future look like then?"

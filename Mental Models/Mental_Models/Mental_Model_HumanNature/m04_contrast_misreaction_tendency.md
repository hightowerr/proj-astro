# Contrast-Misreaction Tendency

**Description:**
Contrast-Misreaction Tendency is a human psychological bias where the nervous system can't naturally measure in absolute scientific units; it must rely on something simpler. The eyes have a solution that limits their programming needs: the contrast in what is seen is registered. And as in sight, so does it go, largely, in the other senses. Moreover, as perception goes, so goes cognition. The result is man's Contrast-Misreaction Tendency. This means we judge stimuli by differences and changes rather than absolute magnitudes, based on contrast with a reference point.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When comparing absolute values rather than relative values is more important
- When reference points might be misleading or inappropriate
- When making long-term financial or investment decisions
- When evaluating performance based on relative rather than absolute standards
- When the context or comparison group is not representative of the true situation

**Keywords for Situations:**
Price perception, market positioning, performance evaluation, decision making, cognitive biases, comparative analysis, relative judgment, anchoring, sales, pricing, negotiations, perception, behavioral economics.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify Your Reference Point:** Recognize what you're comparing against to form your judgment.
2. **Assess the Appropriateness:** Evaluate whether your reference point is a fair or appropriate comparison.
3. **Consider Absolute Values:** Look at the actual, absolute values rather than only relative differences.
4. **Broaden Your Comparison:** Look at multiple reference points to avoid being anchored to one.
5. **Step Back Mentally:** Try to evaluate the situation as if you had no prior context or anchor.
6. **Delay Decisions:** When possible, wait to make judgments until you can evaluate with fresh perspective.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "What am I comparing this to? Is that an appropriate comparison?"
* "How might my judgment change if I had different reference points?"
* "What are the absolute values that matter in this situation?"
* "Am I being influenced by an arbitrary anchor or reference point?"
* "Would my opinion change if I evaluated this in isolation?"
* "What other reference points could I use to form a more accurate judgment?"

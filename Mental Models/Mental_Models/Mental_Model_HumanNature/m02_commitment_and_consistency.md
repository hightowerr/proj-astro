---
model: Commitment and Consistency
category: Psychology, Persuasion
detectors:
- A small, initial request that seems trivial (Foot-in-the-door).
- Being asked to state your position publicly.
- A feeling of being "locked in" to a course of action.
- When you find yourself defending a choice you wouldn't make again.
triggers:
- Making a public statement or declaration.
- Making a choice, especially one that requires effort.
- Filling out a form, signing a petition, or agreeing to a small request.
failure_modes:
- Can lead to "foolish consistency," where you stick to a bad decision to avoid looking inconsistent.
- Can be exploited to escalate requests from small to very large.
- Can prevent you from changing your mind in the face of new evidence.
- Can be used to create loyalty to a brand, group, or cause based on a minor initial interaction.
aliases:
- Hobgoblins of the Mind
- Foot-in-the-Door Technique
- Public Commitment
core_mechanics:
- We have a nearly obsessive desire to be (and to appear) consistent with what we have already done.
- Consistency is a mental shortcut; it lets us avoid having to rethink issues once we've made up our mind.
- Public commitments are the most powerful, as they create external pressure to stay consistent.
- Commitments that require effort are more likely to be stuck to.
---
**Description**: The Commitment and Consistency principle states that once we make a choice or take a stand, we will encounter personal and interpersonal pressures to behave consistently with that commitment. This desire for consistency is a powerful motivator because consistency is typically associated with personal and intellectual strength. It provides a shortcut for navigating a complex world; instead of re-evaluating every situation, we can simply act in accordance with our prior decisions.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Be highly cautious of making small, seemingly inconsequential commitments, especially in writing or in public. This is the foundation of the "foot-in-the-door" technique, where a small initial commitment is used to pave the way for a much larger one. It's crucial to be aware of the difference between healthy consistency and "foolish consistency," where you continue a course of action that no longer makes sense just to avoid the psychological discomfort of being inconsistent. Be willing to change your mind and cut your losses, even if it means appearing inconsistent.

---

**Keywords for Situations**: Goal Setting, Habit Formation, Sales Funnels, Negotiations, Public Declarations, Branding, Identity, Persuasion, Self-Image.

---

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Identify the Commitment**: What prior commitment, choice, or statement is influencing the current situation? Was it made freely, publicly, and with effort?
2.  **Feel for the Pressure**: Acknowledge the internal (self-image) and external (public perception) pressures to remain consistent.
3.  **Listen to Your Gut**: Cialdini suggests listening to your "stomach signs." If you feel trapped or uneasy about a course of action you feel obligated to take, it's a signal that the consistency pressure might be leading you astray.
4.  **Ask the "Foolish Consistency" Question**: "Knowing what I know now, if I could go back in time, would I make the same commitment?" If the answer is no, then the current course of action is likely foolish consistency.
5.  **Separate the Initial Commitment from the Current Request**: Evaluate the current request on its own merits, not as an extension of a past decision. It is okay to say "I have changed my mind based on new information."

---

**Coaching Questions**:
*   What small commitment am I being asked for right now, and where might it lead?
*   Have I made a public statement that is now locking me into a specific path?
*   Am I continuing this course of action because it's still the best one, or simply because it's the one I started?
*   What are the costs of being inconsistent in this situation versus the costs of sticking to a bad decision?
*   Would I advise a friend in the same situation to do this, or would I tell them to reconsider?
*   Is my identity tied up in this commitment, and is that clouding my judgment?

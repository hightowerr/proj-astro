# Kantian Fairness Tendency

**Description:**
Kantian Fairness Tendency is a concept from psychology and behavioral economics that relates to the human desire for fairness and justice based on Immanuel Kant's philosophical framework. It refers to the tendency of people to want transactions and interactions to be fair. The basic idea is that we have devised certain rules that, when followed by everyone, result in a pretty smooth life for all involved. The key is that everyone needs to follow along with 'socially acceptable guidelines for conduct' which constructs the framework for fairness. When behavior doesn't seem to fall in this framework, we label it as unfair.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When fairness considerations might override more efficient or practical solutions
- When pursuing fairness could lead to suboptimal outcomes for the majority
- When fairness in individual cases conflicts with greater fairness for all
- When the pursuit of fairness becomes an obstacle to necessary change or adaptation
- When cultural or contextual differences make universal fairness standards unworkable

**Keywords for Situations:**
Justice systems, business negotiations, employee compensation, resource allocation, ethical decision making, moral reasoning, social contracts, fairness, equity, Kantian ethics, behavioral economics, reciprocity, conflict resolution.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Define the Fairness Framework:** Establish what constitutes fairness in the specific context or situation.
2. **Evaluate Individual vs. Group Impact:** Consider whether fairness for one person or group conflicts with fairness for others.
3. **Assess Long-term vs. Short-term Impacts:** Determine if pursuit of fairness now might create unfairness later.
4. **Seek Balanced Solutions:** Look for approaches that maintain fairness while serving overall goals.
5. **Consider Alternative Perspectives:** Examine the situation from viewpoints of different stakeholders.
6. **Tolerate Imperfection:** Accept that sometimes fairness must be balanced against other important values.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "What would constitute fair treatment in this situation?"
* "Am I focusing too much on fairness for individuals at the expense of fairness for the group?"
* "Is my pursuit of fairness preventing necessary changes or outcomes?"
* "How do different stakeholders define fairness in this situation?"
* "What would happen if I tolerated a small amount of unfairness to achieve a greater good?"
* "Am I being too rigid in applying fairness standards?"

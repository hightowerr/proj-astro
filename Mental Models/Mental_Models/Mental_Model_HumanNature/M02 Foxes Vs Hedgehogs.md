**Category: Human Nature**

**Description: Core concept explanation**
The Fox–Hedgehog model contrasts two thinking styles. **Foxes** use many ideas, consider different perspectives, and update their views often. **Hedgehogs** focus on one big idea they apply everywhere. The phrase comes from the line, "The fox knows many things, but the hedgehog knows one big thing." In studies on forecasting, foxes were more accurate. They were flexible and adjusted their thinking. Hedgehogs often stuck to one theory, ignoring new evidence that didn’t fit.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

* When the task needs both a clear focus and flexibility (e.g. science or innovation).
* When specialists are dismissed too quickly—hedgehog focus can lead to breakthroughs.
* When situations reward clear, consistent direction (e.g. crisis leadership).
* When using it to label people rigidly—many fall somewhere between.

**Thinking Steps:**

1. **Spot your default:** Are you relying on one big idea or using many?
2. **Check your reaction to new info:** Are you adjusting or defending your view?
3. **Match style to context:** Is the situation simple and stable (hedgehog) or messy and changing (fox)?
4. **Try the opposite lens:** What would someone with the other mindset see or do?
5. **Review and adapt:** Is your approach still working, or is it time to switch?

---

**Coaching Questions:**

* Am I stuck on one idea?
* What views or data am I ignoring?
* What would a good counter-argument be?
* What would make me change my mind?
* Is clarity or flexibility more useful here?
* How would someone with the opposite style approach this?

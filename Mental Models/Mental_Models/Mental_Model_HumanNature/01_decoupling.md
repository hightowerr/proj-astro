# Decoupling

## Description
Decoupling refers to the breaking apart of the chain of consumption activities that customers traditionally fulfill in partnership with a single company as customers go about acquiring goods and services. In today's new wave of disruption, upstart firms are breaking apart these chains, offering customers the chance to fulfill just one or a few activities with them, and leaving incumbents to fulfill the rest. Startups decouple to gain a foothold in the market, and they grow by offering to fill a specific activity for customers - what the author calls "coupling." Both the initial decoupling and the subsequent coupling allow startups to quickly steal a substantial amount of market share at incumbents' expense. This process represents a fundamental shift in how markets operate, where disruption is driven by customers rather than technology.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Decoupling should be avoided when the activities in the customer value chain are inherently interdependent and provide more value when performed together. It should also be used with caution in highly regulated industries where legal or regulatory barriers prevent separation of activities. Additionally, avoid this approach when the customer base is not yet ready to embrace specialized service providers, or when the cost of coordinating between multiple service providers exceeds the benefits of specialization.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the complete customer value chain for your target market, mapping all activities from initial need recognition to disposal
2. Analyze which activities create value for customers, which capture value for businesses, and which erode value for customers
3. Look for weak links in the chain where customers might prefer to work with different companies for different activities
4. Determine if there are activities that customers perform that create value but don't necessarily need to be coupled with other activities
5. Calculate the total cost (monetary, time, and effort) customers incur at each stage of the value chain to identify high-cost activities
6. Consider how technology could enable separation of currently coupled activities
7. Assess whether customers would benefit from separating specific activities and if they'd be willing to pay for the separation
8. Develop a business model that captures value from the decoupled activity while creating value for the customer

## Coaching Questions
- What activities do customers in your market currently perform with a single provider that could potentially be separated?
- Where in the customer journey do consumers experience the highest costs (monetary, time, or effort)?
- Which activities in your industry create the most value for customers versus those that simply capture value?
- How might customers' needs and behaviors change in the future that would make decoupling more attractive?
- What activities do customers complain about or express frustration with in your industry?
- If you had to break apart one key activity from the traditional value chain, which would provide the most benefit to customers?
- What would prevent customers from accepting a decoupled approach in your industry?
- How might technology enable you to separate currently coupled activities more efficiently than incumbents?
- What value could you create by decoupling an activity that incumbents currently perform as part of a bundle?
- How would you capture value from a decoupled activity while still creating value for the customer?

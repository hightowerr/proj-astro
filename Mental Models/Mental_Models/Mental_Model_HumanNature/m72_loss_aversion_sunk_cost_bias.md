
# Loss Aversion (Sunk-Cost Bias)

**Description**: Loss aversion is the tendency to prefer avoiding losses to acquiring equivalent gains; the pain of losing is psychologically more powerful than the pleasure of winning. This bias often leads to the **sunk-cost fallacy** (or sunk-cost bias), where we irrationally continue a venture or project because of previously invested resources (time, money, effort), rather than assessing its future prospects. The book "Bulletproof Problem Solving" gives the example of a company refusing to exit a losing business to avoid realizing a loss on the "accounting book value," ultimately losing much more money as a result.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This is a cognitive bias to be actively mitigated, especially in investment, portfolio management, and project management decisions. It is a major driver of throwing "good money after bad."

**Keywords for Situations**: Project management, investment decisions, exiting a failing venture, personal commitments, cutting losses, escalation of commitment.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Explicitly identify the sunk costs. Ask, "What resources (time, money, energy) have we already invested that are irrecoverable?"
2.  Reframe the decision based *only* on future costs and future benefits. Ask the critical question: **"If we were starting from scratch today, knowing what we know now, would we invest in this project?"**
3.  Try to get an outsider's perspective, as they are not emotionally attached to the sunk costs. Ask, "What would a new manager or an outside consultant advise us to do with this project?"
4.  Before starting a new project, set clear exit criteria or "stop-loss" points. This creates a future tripwire to force a rational decision, rather than an emotional one based on sunk costs.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Are we continuing this project because it's a good idea for the future, or because we've already spent so much on it?"
*   "Let's completely ignore the money and time we've already spent. Based purely on the future outlook, is this the best use of our resources going forward?"
*   "If an outsider offered to buy this project from us for $1 today, would we be relieved, or would we fight to buy it back? If we'd be relieved, why are we continuing to fund it?"
*   "What were our original exit criteria for this project? Have we met them? If so, why are we not following through?"

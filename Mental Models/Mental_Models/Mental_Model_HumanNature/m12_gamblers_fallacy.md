# Gambler's Fallacy

**Description:**
Gambler's Fallacy is the mistaken belief that if something happens more frequently than normal during a certain period, it will happen less frequently in the future (or vice versa). This fallacy is based on the incorrect assumption that independent events somehow influence each other to "even out." For example, believing that after a series of red numbers on a roulette wheel, black is "due" to come up next. The fallacy ignores that each spin of the wheel is an independent event with the same probability regardless of previous outcomes.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When making decisions based on past independent events
- When investing based on the expectation that poor performance will reverse
- When gambling or making financial bets expecting "regression to the mean"
- When believing that a series of good or bad outcomes affects future independent events
- When assuming that random sequences will balance out in the short term

**Keywords for Situations:**
Gambling, investing, decision making, probability, randomness, cognitive biases, behavioral economics, independent events, sequences, regression to the mean, chance, statistics.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Determine Independence:** Assess whether events are truly independent or if past events actually influence future ones.
2. **Focus on Base Rates:** Consider the fundamental probability of the event rather than recent outcomes.
3. **Ignore Recent Sequences:** Don't let recent runs of outcomes affect your assessment of future probabilities.
4. **Understand Randomness:** Accept that streaks can and do happen naturally in random sequences.
5. **Calculate Proper Probabilities:** Use mathematical probability rather than intuitive feelings about "due" outcomes.
6. **Look for Real Causes:** Distinguish between random fluctuations and actual patterns with underlying causes.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Are these events truly independent, or do past results affect future ones?"
* "Am I basing my expectations on mathematical probability or recent streaks?"
* "What is the base rate of this outcome regardless of recent history?"
* "Would I make this decision if I only knew the overall probability?"
* "Am I confusing the law of large numbers with short-term results?"
* "Am I looking for patterns that don't actually exist?"

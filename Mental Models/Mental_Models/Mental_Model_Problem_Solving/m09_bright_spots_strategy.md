# Mental Model: Bright Spots Strategy

## Description
The Bright Spots Strategy focuses on looking for situations or places where the problem is not as bad or where it may even be entirely absent. Rather than focusing on what's wrong, this approach examines what's working and finds positive exceptions. It's based on the premise that if someone has found a way to solve or avoid the problem, even occasionally, we can learn from that approach. The strategy helps identify situations where people or systems are succeeding despite facing the same challenges others are struggling with.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The problem is completely uniform with no exceptions
- Time constraints don't allow for searching for positive exceptions
- The issue is a one-time event with no patterns to identify
- Stakeholders are not open to learning from exceptions
- The bright spots are due to unique circumstances that can't be replicated
- The problem is truly new with no prior examples to study
- When immediate action is required rather than systematic learning

## Keywords for Situations
- When problems seem intractable despite multiple solution attempts
- When facing persistent organizational or behavioral challenges
- When stakeholders are focused only on what's wrong
- When dealing with "people problems" that seem to affect everyone
- When encountering recurring issues that haven't been solved
- When trying to understand how some people succeed while others fail
- When facing challenges in process improvement or culture change
- When looking for evidence-based approaches that have worked

## Thinking Steps
1. Look for times when you've already solved the problem at least once
2. Identify positive outliers in your group or organization who are succeeding
3. Research who else deals with this type of problem and how they handle it
4. Examine bright spots carefully to understand what was different
5. Recreate the circumstances that led to positive exceptions when possible
6. Scale up successful approaches identified through bright spots
7. Look for bright spots in unexpected places or among unlikely people
8. Validate that bright spot approaches are applicable to other contexts

## Coaching Questions
- When did we not have this problem or when was it less severe?
- Who else faces this challenge and handles it successfully?
- What was different about situations where the problem didn't occur?
- Are there positive outliers in our group who have solved this?
- What can we learn from the few times when this worked well?
- Where might we find examples of people handling this issue differently?
- What conditions allowed success in the bright spot examples?
- How might we replicate the success of positive outliers?

model: Via Negativa (Subtractive Thinking)
category: ["Problem Solving", "Risk"]
detectors:
  - Plans rely on additive complexity (“do more things”) without clear fragility removal
  - You can’t define “what to do,” but you can identify what is harmful or fragile
  - You are tempted to intervene because “doing something” feels like progress
triggers:
  - Strategy and decision-making under uncertainty
  - Health, operations, and policy where interventions can backfire
  - Simplifying systems and removing failure modes
failure_modes:
  - Confusing inaction with neglect (via negativa is deliberate subtraction)
  - Removing variability/stressors that are actually beneficial (denying hormesis)
  - Subtracting the wrong thing because the system boundary is misdrawn
aliases:
  - Subtractive Knowledge
  - Negative Way
  - "Say No" Discipline
core_mechanics:
  - It is often easier and more reliable to identify what something is not than what it is
  - Negative knowledge (what breaks, what harms) is more robust than positive narratives
  - Removing fragility and harmful interventions often outperforms adding sophistication

### Description

Via negativa is the discipline of improving decisions by subtracting: removing fragilities, harmful actions, and unnecessary complexity. When direct, positive definitions are unreliable, you proceed by elimination—listing what something is *not* and what tends to break. This aligns with antifragility: first remove downside; let the system’s natural adaptation do the rest.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When subtraction removes a necessary constraint or safety mechanism.
- When the situation is an acute emergency where a known, high-confidence intervention is required.

---

### Keywords for Situations

Removal, simplification, omission vs commission, “less is more,” fragility reduction, robustness, elimination.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **List what breaks**
   Identify the fragile parts of the system—what fails irreversibly, compounds, or hides risk.
2. **Remove the largest fragility first**
   Prioritize subtracting ruin exposures (leverage, tight coupling, single points of failure).
3. **Stop the iatrogenics**
   Identify interventions that create harm as a side effect and reduce or eliminate them.
4. **Say “no” to the extra reasons**
   If you need many reasons to convince yourself, treat it as a warning sign; prefer robust choices.
5. **Simplify the narrative**
   Prefer rules and constraints that still work when your explanation is wrong.
6. **Re-check what remains**
   After subtraction, confirm you still have the essential capacity and that new fragilities were not introduced.

---

### Coaching Questions

- What is one thing you can stop doing that reduces downside immediately?
- Which intervention would be hardest to undo if it backfires?
- What fragility is hidden because things have been stable lately?
- If you had to improve results by removing only one element, what would it be?

# Mental Model: Negativity Bias

## Description
Negativity Bias is the psychological tendency for people to pay more attention to bad things than to good things. When confronting problems, we're wired to focus on what's going wrong, thereby failing to learn from what's working well. This bias causes us to dwell on problems and challenges while overlooking positive exceptions, successes, and bright spots that could provide valuable insights for solving issues. Understanding this bias helps shift attention toward identifying and learning from positive aspects of situations.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The situation genuinely requires focus on problems or risks
- Safety concerns make attention to negative factors critical
- There are genuine threats that need immediate attention
- The organization is in crisis and needs to address immediate problems
- Positive thinking might cause dangerous complacency
- The situation involves genuine and significant negative consequences
- Stakeholders are already overly optimistic and not seeing real risks

## Keywords for Situations
- When teams are stuck in problem-focused thinking without considering successes
- When facing persistent challenges that seem impossible to solve
- When stakeholders focus only on what's not working
- When trying to identify successful approaches or strategies
- When conducting post-mortems or lessons learned exercises
- When trying to motivate teams or individuals
- When looking to understand why some approaches work better than others
- When facing organizational or cultural challenges

## Thinking Steps
1. Notice when the focus is predominantly on negative aspects
2. Consciously shift attention to identify positive aspects or successes
3. Look for bright spots where the problem doesn't occur or is less severe
4. Examine what's working well that could be scaled up or replicated
5. Consider positive deviants who are succeeding despite challenges
6. Actively ask "What has been going well and why?"
7. Balance attention between problems and successes during analysis
8. Validate that you're seeing the complete picture, not just the negative

## Coaching Questions
- What has been going well in this situation that we might be overlooking?
- Where does this problem NOT occur or is less severe?
- Who is succeeding despite facing the same challenges?
- What positive exceptions can we identify and learn from?
- How might focusing more on what's working change our approach?
- What would happen if we spent as much time analyzing successes as problems?
- What bright spots exist that we could build upon?
- Are we paying attention to both sides of the story?

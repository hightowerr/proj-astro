### Description

Naive interventionism is the tendency to intervene in complex systems as if outcomes were predictable and controllable. It privileges acts of commission over acts of omission, often mistaking short-term calm for improvement. Over time, it can fragilize systems by suppressing variability and accumulating hidden tail risks.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When the intervention is not reversible and the system is fat-tailed/opaque.
- When you are acting mainly to appear active or competent.

---

### Keywords for Situations

Overreaction, overstabilization, “expert problem,” iatrogenics, noise vs signal, tail risk, control illusion.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Ask what you’re trying to prevent**
   Is the goal to remove minor discomfort/variance, or to prevent catastrophic harm?
2. **Identify the system’s natural antifragility**
   What small stressors does the system need to adapt and learn?
3. **Estimate iatrogenic risk**
   How likely is your intervention to cause harm, and how large could that harm be?
4. **Switch to non-naive intervention**
   Prefer small, reversible moves; maintain kill-switches and clear stop rules.
5. **Keep variance where it is safe**
   Don’t suppress normal variability if it is informative and not ruinous.
6. **Audit for hidden fragility**
   After intervention, check whether risk migrated to the tail (rare but severe events).

---

### Coaching Questions

- Are we solving a real problem, or just removing normal variability?
- What could this intervention break that won’t self-repair?
- How would we roll this back if the system responds badly?
- What would “do no harm” look like here?

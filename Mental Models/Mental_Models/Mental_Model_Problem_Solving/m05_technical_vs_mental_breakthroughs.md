# Mental Model: Technical vs Mental Breakthroughs

## Description
Technical vs Mental Breakthroughs distinguishes between solutions that rely on new technology or complex systems and those that come from questioning our beliefs and changing our perspective. Technical breakthroughs depend on new technology, data-sharing standards, and behavioral changes in the population, while mental breakthroughs come from reinterpreting existing information or situations differently. Often, solving tough problems depends not on technological but on mental breakthroughs, requiring interpretation and sense-making rather than just technical improvements.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The problem genuinely requires new technology or technical capabilities
- The solution is clearly technical in nature (e.g., engineering, infrastructure)
- Existing technology is insufficient to address the core issue
- The problem is purely technical in nature without human interpretation factors
- Time constraints don't allow for mental reframing work
- Stakeholders require technical fixes rather than perspective changes
- The team lacks experience in mental reframing approaches

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing persistent problems despite technological advances
- When stakeholders focus on technical solutions while ignoring mental models
- When existing technology could work better with different approaches
- When dealing with human-centered problems (behavioral, cultural, communication)
- When resources are available but perspectives are limiting outcomes
- When encountering "people problems" that aren't truly technical
- When solutions fail despite good technical implementation
- When facing challenges in innovation, communication, or organizational change

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify whether the problem appears to need a technical or mental solution
2. Question if beliefs, assumptions, or perspectives might be the real barrier
3. Consider what would change if we approached the same situation differently
4. Look for existing resources that might be underutilized due to perspective
5. Ask if technology is being used to solve a mental problem
6. Examine if people issues are being treated as technical problems
7. Consider what mental shift might allow current resources to work better
8. Validate whether a mental breakthrough would be more effective than technical investment

## Coaching Questions
- Are we trying to solve a mental problem with technical solutions?
- What if the solution doesn't require new technology but a different perspective?
- What beliefs or assumptions might be limiting our current approach?
- How might someone from a different industry solve this problem?
- What would happen if we looked at this situation in a completely different way?
- Are we investing more in technology than in changing perspectives?
- What would we do differently if we couldn't use any new technology?
- Could the same resources achieve better outcomes with a different approach?

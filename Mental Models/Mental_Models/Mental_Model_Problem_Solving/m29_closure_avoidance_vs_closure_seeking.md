# Mental Model: Closure Avoidance vs Closure Seeking

## Description
Closure Avoidance vs Closure Seeking describes two emotional responses to ambiguity and decision-making. Closure-avoidant people dislike moving forward early and prefer having more data before acting, often leading to extended analysis and delayed decisions. Closure-seeking people find it unnatural to consider multiple possibilities simultaneously and prefer to move forward quickly once they have a plausible explanation, often jumping too quickly into solution mode. Understanding these tendencies helps manage the tension between adequate analysis and timely action in problem-solving processes.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The situation requires immediate action without accommodation of different styles
- Time constraints override the need for managing cognitive styles
- The team composition is fixed and cannot be adjusted for balance
- The problem has extreme consequences where either too much or too little analysis is dangerous
- Stakeholders have strong preferences that cannot be accommodated
- The decision-making culture is firmly established and unchangeable
- The problem is straightforward and doesn't require careful process management

## Keywords for Situations
- When managing diverse teams with different decision-making preferences
- When facing time-sensitive problems that still need thorough analysis
- When developing problem-solving processes for mixed teams
- When encountering resistance to moving forward or staying in analysis mode
- When working with stakeholders who have different comfort levels with ambiguity
- When designing decision-making frameworks that work for different people
- When facing challenges in project management with mixed preferences
- When trying to balance thoroughness with timeliness in problem resolution

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the closure tendencies of team members and stakeholders
2. Recognize when the process tips too far toward analysis or action
3. Build in mechanisms that satisfy both closure-avoidant and closure-seeking needs
4. Set clear timelines and decision points that work for both styles
5. Design processes that allow for both thorough analysis and timely action
6. Monitor group dynamics to prevent one style from dominating
7. Adjust facilitation approaches based on the group's composition
8. Validate that the process serves the problem-solving needs effectively

## Coaching Questions
- How do we balance the need for thorough analysis with timely action?
- Are we staying in analysis mode too long or moving to action too quickly?
- How might we design a process that works for both analysis-loving and action-oriented team members?
- What would help people who prefer more data feel comfortable moving forward?
- What would help people who want quick decisions feel comfortable with more analysis?
- How can we manage the tension between different decision-making styles?
- Are we accommodating the cognitive styles of our key stakeholders?
- How might we structure our problem-solving process to be more inclusive of different preferences?

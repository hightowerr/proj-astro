## Mental Model = Opportunity Solution Tree

**Category = Problem Solving**
**Description:**
The Opportunity Solution Tree (OST) is a visual representation of the paths a team might take to reach a desired outcome. It gives structure to the "ill-structured" problem of product discovery by hierarchically connecting a clear business outcome (root) to customer opportunities (branches), potential solutions (leaves), and assumption tests. This framework helps product trios resolve the tension between business needs and customer needs, ensuring that the team creates business value *by* solving customer problems (opportunities) rather than just shipping features (outputs).

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- **Optimization Problems:** When the problem is "tame" or well-defined (e.g., optimizing a button color or a known linear funnel) and does not require broad discovery, a full OST may be overkill. In these cases, a simple traction metric or A/B test plan might suffice.
- **Linear Roadmapping:** Do not use the tree as a fixed roadmap or a "to-do" list. It is a living document that must evolve as the team learns; treating it as a static plan leads to a waterfall process disguised as discovery.
- **Solo Documentation:** Avoid creating the tree in isolation just to document a decision. Its primary power lies in *building shared understanding* within the product trio (PM, Design, Engineering) through the co-creation process.

**Keywords for Situations:**
Product discovery, strategic alignment, roadmap planning, wicked problems, stakeholder management, prioritizing, continuous improvement.

**Thinking Steps:**
1.  **Define the Desired Outcome (Root):** Identify a single, clear business outcome (e.g., "Increase retention") rather than an output (e.g., "Ship the Android app"). Ideally, use a leading indicator that the team can influence.
2.  **Map the Opportunity Space (Branches):** Conduct customer research (continuous interviewing) to discover unmet needs, pain points, and desires. Map these as "opportunities" connected to the outcome. Break large opportunities down into smaller, solvable child opportunities.
3.  **Prioritize an Opportunity:** Choose *one* target opportunity to focus on based on data and strategy, rather than trying to solve everything at once.
4.  **Generate Solutions (Leaves):** Ideate multiple potential solutions *specifically* for that target opportunity. This ensures solutions are purpose-built to solve a verified need.
5.  **Define Assumption Tests:** Below the solutions, list the critical assumptions (desirability, viability, feasibility, usability) that must be true for the solution to work. Run rapid tests to validate these before building.
6.  **Evaluate via "Compare and Contrast":** Use the visualization to compare opportunities against each other and solutions against each other. Avoid "whether or not" decisions (e.g., "Should we build X?") and instead ask "Which of these options best solves the target opportunity?"

**Coaching Questions:**
- "What specific customer need or pain point does this solution address?"
- "If we solve this problem, do we have evidence that it will drive our desired outcome?"
- "Have we considered enough alternative solutions for this opportunity, or did we fall in love with our first idea?"
- "Are we making a 'whether or not' decision on this feature, or are we comparing it against other ways to solve the same problem?"
- "Does this tree reflect our current understanding of the customer, and have we updated it based on our latest interviews?"

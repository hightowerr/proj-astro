# Mental Model: Multiple Working Hypotheses

## Description
Multiple Working Hypotheses is the practice of exploring several different explanations for what might be going on simultaneously, rather than committing to just one explanation upfront. This approach, developed by geologist Thomas C. Chamberlin, guards against confirmation bias by preventing people from falling in love with a single theory. Instead of considering one problem framing, this mental model suggests simultaneously exploring multiple possible explanations until sufficient empirical testing reveals the best choice.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- Time constraints require immediate decision-making
- The situation demands a single clear direction
- Resources are too limited to explore multiple approaches
- Stakeholders require a single, clear problem statement
- The team lacks capacity to manage multiple hypotheses
- The situation requires immediate crisis response
- There is already a clearly validated single approach

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing complex problems with unclear causes
- When stakeholders have different understandings of the problem
- When previous attempts to solve the problem have failed
- When dealing with controversial or politically sensitive issues
- When facing challenges where multiple factors might be at play
- When trying to understand customer problems with various possible causes
- When encountering organizational challenges with several potential factors
- When trying to understand why solutions haven't worked as expected

## Thinking Steps
1. Generate multiple possible explanations for the situation
2. Keep all hypotheses active without prematurely selecting one
3. Look for evidence that would support or contradict each hypothesis
4. Design tests that can help differentiate between hypotheses
5. Be open to the idea that the best fit may be a mix of several explanations
6. Explore multiple perspectives simultaneously
7. Avoid committing to just one explanation too early
8. Be prepared to walk away from hypotheses if better ones emerge

## Coaching Questions
- What other explanations might there be for this situation?
- How might I test multiple possible explanations simultaneously?
- What would prove each of my theories wrong?
- Am I focusing too much on evidence that supports my current theory?
- How might someone with a different perspective explain this problem?
- What evidence would I need to change my mind about the problem?
- Are there multiple ways to frame this situation?
- How can I avoid falling in love with just one explanation?

# Mental Model: Look in the Mirror

## Description
Look in the Mirror is the practice of examining your own role in creating or perpetuating problems. This mental model addresses our tendency to overlook our own contribution to situations, as we've learned to tell stories that conveniently leave out our agency. It involves honestly confronting how our own behavior might be contributing to the problem, whether as individuals or as part of a group. This approach helps identify solutions that may be closer than expected, focusing on what we can change rather than only what others need to change.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The problem is entirely external with no personal contribution
- Cultural norms make self-examination inappropriate
- The situation involves genuine victimization where self-blame would be harmful
- Time constraints don't allow for personal reflection
- The problem is caused by factors completely beyond personal control
- Stakeholders are not psychologically safe for honest self-reflection
- The issue is clearly systemic without individual responsibility factors

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing interpersonal conflicts or relationship challenges
- When encountering resistance to initiatives you're leading
- When problems seem to follow you or your team despite context changes
- When stakeholders react negatively to your approaches or solutions
- When facing recurring challenges that others don't seem to encounter
- When dealing with team dynamics or organizational culture issues
- When solutions work elsewhere but not in your context
- When facing feedback that's difficult to hear or accept

## Thinking Steps
1. Examine your own contribution to the problem situation
2. Consider how your behavior might be contributing to the issue
3. Look for self-imposed limitations or assumptions in your approach
4. Ask yourself how you might react if you were on the other side
5. Consider what you could do differently to change the dynamic
6. Focus on contribution rather than blame when analyzing the situation
7. Seek feedback from others about blind spots in your approach
8. Identify specific changes in your behavior that could shift the situation

## Coaching Questions
- How might my behavior be contributing to this problem?
- What part do I play in creating or maintaining this situation?
- How would someone else describe my role in this issue?
- What feedback have others given me that I haven't fully considered?
- If I were observing this situation from the outside, what would I notice?
- What would I advise a friend to do in this situation?
- What assumptions am I making that might be part of the problem?
- What could I do differently to change this dynamic?

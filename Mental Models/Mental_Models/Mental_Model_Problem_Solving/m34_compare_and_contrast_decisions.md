## Mental Model = Compare and Contrast Decision Making

**Category = Problem Solving**
**Description:**
This model combats the "narrow framing" bias where teams get stuck asking "Should we do this or not?" (a binary decision). Instead, effective decision-making requires shifting to a "compare and contrast" mindset: "Which of these options best drives our desired outcome?" By forcing a comparison between multiple viable solutions, teams reduce emotional attachment to a single idea, lower the risk of confirmation bias, and make more rational, evidence-based choices.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- **Analysis Paralysis:** Don't use this to generate infinite options just to delay deciding. A comparison of 3 good options is usually sufficient.
- **Lack of Options:** You cannot compare if you haven't done the work to generate alternatives. If you only have one idea, go back to ideation first.

**Keywords for Situations:**
Prioritization, backlog grooming, strategic planning, overcoming confirmation bias, resource allocation.

**Thinking Steps:**
1.  **Catch the "Whether or Not" Question:** Listen for questions like "Should we build this feature?" or "Is this a good idea?"
2.  **Reframe the Question:** Change the prompt to "Which of our current ideas will have the biggest impact on our outcome?"
3.  **Generate Alternatives:** If you don't have other options to compare, pause and generate them. Ask, "What else could we do to solve this same problem?"
4.  **Evaluate Against the Outcome:** Assess each option not in a vacuum, but relative to the others using the same criteria (e.g., impact on the specific key result).
5.  **Select the Best fit:** Choose the option that wins the comparison, knowing you explored the alternatives.

**Coaching Questions:**
- "Are we asking a 'whether or not' question?"
- "What other options did we consider before settling on this one?"
- "If we couldn't build this feature, what would we do instead to achieve the goal?"
- "Which of these three solutions has the highest probability of success, and why?"

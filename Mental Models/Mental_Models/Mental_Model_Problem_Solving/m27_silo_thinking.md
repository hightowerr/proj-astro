# Mental Model: Silo Thinking

## Description
Silo Thinking is the tendency to remain within familiar groups, departments, or perspectives, which prevents access to diverse viewpoints and potential solutions. This cognitive bias limits problem-solving effectiveness by restricting input to similar types of people with comparable backgrounds, experiences, and assumptions. Silo thinking leads to missed opportunities for creative solutions and can perpetuate blind spots that could be addressed by involving boundary spanners or outsiders who bring different perspectives to problem identification and solution development.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The problem requires specialized expertise that outsiders don't possess
- Time constraints don't allow for involving additional perspectives
- The situation involves highly sensitive or confidential information
- The team already has diverse perspectives and additional input isn't needed
- Stakeholders are not open to outside input or perspectives
- The problem is well-understood and requires only technical expertise
- External involvement might compromise decision-making authority

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing complex organizational challenges that cross departments
- When solutions keep failing despite competent internal efforts
- When developing strategies or initiatives that will affect multiple groups
- When trying to understand customer problems across different segments
- When facing innovation challenges that require diverse thinking
- When encountering resistance to changes from other departments
- When trying to solve problems that have persisted despite internal efforts
- When developing solutions that will be implemented across the organization

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the groups or departments currently involved in the problem
2. Look for perspectives that are missing from the current problem-solving group
3. Consider what different backgrounds or expertise might contribute
4. Evaluate the potential value of boundary spanners or outsiders
5. Consider how to involve different perspectives without disrupting process
6. Plan how to manage potential conflicts from diverse input
7. Design communication approaches that bridge different perspectives
8. Validate that diverse input is being considered and integrated effectively

## Coaching Questions
- What perspectives are missing from our current problem-solving approach?
- How might people from different departments or backgrounds view this issue?
- Who outside our immediate group might have insights into this problem?
- What are we not seeing because we're working within our usual group?
- How might we involve people who understand the problem differently?
- What would change if we brought in someone from a completely different field?
- How can we overcome the natural resistance to outside perspectives?
- What benefits might we gain from including diverse viewpoints?

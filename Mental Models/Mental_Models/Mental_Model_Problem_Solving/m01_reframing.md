# Mental Model: Reframing

## Description
Reframing is the practice of shifting the way you see a problem to find radically better solutions. Rather than accepting the problem as it's initially presented ("the elevator is too slow"), reframing involves questioning the underlying assumptions and considering alternative ways to understand the problem. This approach can lead to breakthrough solutions that address the root issue in unexpected ways, such as installing mirrors near elevators to reduce complaints about slow service by giving people something engaging to look at while they wait.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Reframing should be used with caution when:
- Time is extremely limited and there's no opportunity for exploration
- The initial problem statement is clearly accurate and urgent
- Stakeholders are not open to questioning assumptions
- The team lacks the psychological safety to challenge established thinking
- There are already proven, effective solutions to the identified problem
- The cost of exploring alternative framings exceeds potential benefits

## Keywords for Situations
- When solutions to an identified problem keep failing
- When stakeholders seem stuck on the same ineffective approaches
- When facing seemingly intractable organizational challenges
- When customers complain about products/services in unexpected ways
- When competing solutions in a market all follow the same approach
- When dealing with interpersonal conflicts that persist despite surface-level fixes
- When facing resource constraints that make traditional solutions unfeasible
- When encountering resistance to change that seems disproportionate to the change itself

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the initial problem statement as presented to you
2. Question the framing: "Is this the real problem, or just how someone described it?"
3. Explore possible alternative framings of the same situation
4. Consider what different stakeholders might see as the "real" problem
5. Look for what's missing from the current frame (what isn't being considered?)
6. Examine bright spots where the problem doesn't occur or is less severe
7. Test your new problem framing against the evidence you have
8. Validate your reframed problem with stakeholders before developing solutions

## Coaching Questions
- How might someone from a different department/frameview the same situation?
- What if the opposite of our current problem definition were true?
- What would someone who disagrees with our problem statement say?
- Are there stakeholders we haven't consulted who might see the problem differently?
- Where and when does this problem NOT occur? What's different in those instances?
- What happens if we change the verb in our problem statement (e.g., from "lack of" to "resistance to")?
- If we were to explain this situation to a newcomer with fresh eyes, how would we frame it?
- What would be the question behind the question that's really at stake here?

# Mental Model: External vs Internal Self-Awareness

## Description
External vs Internal Self-Awareness distinguishes between two types of self-understanding: Internal Self-Awareness is being in touch with your own emotions, values, goals, thoughts, and feelings - essentially knowing yourself from the inside. External Self-Awareness is your awareness of how other people see you - understanding the impact your behavior has on others. The mental model highlights that these two qualities aren't necessarily linked; someone can be deeply attuned to their own values and beliefs but unaware of how others perceive them, which can significantly impact problem-solving effectiveness.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The problem doesn't involve interpersonal interactions
- Cultural differences make external feedback inappropriate
- The situation requires internal focus rather than external perception
- Stakeholders are not trustworthy or safe to seek feedback from
- Time constraints don't allow for seeking external perspectives
- The problem is purely technical without human interaction elements
- Privacy concerns prevent seeking external perspectives

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing interpersonal conflicts or relationship challenges
- When trying to understand why others respond negatively to your approaches
- When solutions or communications aren't resonating with stakeholders
- When leadership effectiveness is being questioned
- When encountering resistance to initiatives you're leading
- When dealing with team dynamics or organizational culture issues
- When facing challenges in cross-functional or cross-cultural collaboration
- When receiving feedback that contradicts your self-perception

## Thinking Steps
1. Assess your current level of internal self-awareness
2. Seek feedback about how others perceive you and your approach
3. Compare your self-perception with others' perceptions
4. Identify blind spots between your internal understanding and external impact
5. Consider how your behavior might be perceived by different stakeholders
6. Examine the gap between how you think you come across and how you actually do
7. Look for patterns in feedback from multiple sources
8. Adjust your approach based on understanding of both internal and external perspectives

## Coaching Questions
- How do I think others perceive my approach to this situation?
- What feedback have others given me about my behavior or impact?
- How might someone else describe my contribution to this issue?
- What would I learn if I asked a trusted colleague how I come across?
- How do my intentions align with my actual impact on others?
- What aspects of my behavior might be contributing to the problem?
- How might different stakeholders view my actions differently?
- What would change if I better understood my impact on others?

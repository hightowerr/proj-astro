# Mental Model: Boundary Spanners

## Description
Boundary Spanners are people who understand but are not fully part of your world, providing valuable external perspectives without the complexity of involving complete outsiders. These individuals serve as bridges between different groups, organizations, or domains of knowledge, offering the benefits of outside perspective while maintaining enough connection to understand the context and communicate effectively. They have both the internal understanding to speak the group's language and the external perspective to challenge assumptions and blind spots.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The issue requires complete external objectivity without any connection
- Time constraints don't allow for involving additional people
- The boundary spanner lacks sufficient knowledge of the situation
- The problem requires highly specialized expertise that outsiders can't provide
- Confidentiality concerns prevent involving people from other groups
- The situation requires complete neutrality without any stake in the outcome
- The boundary spanner has conflicts of interest in the situation

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing organizational challenges requiring fresh perspectives
- When trying to overcome silo thinking or groupthink
- When developing new initiatives that need broader input
- When facing challenges that span multiple departments or functions
- When seeking to validate problem definitions without bringing in outsiders
- When trying to implement changes that require cross-functional support
- When facing complex challenges requiring diverse perspectives
- When seeking to avoid the complexity of involving extreme outsiders

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify people who understand your situation but aren't fully part of your group
2. Look for individuals from adjacent departments, roles, or organizations
3. Consider people who have worked in your area but come from outside
4. Evaluate how these boundary spanners might challenge your assumptions
5. Determine what unique perspectives they can bring to the problem
6. Consider how they can communicate effectively with both groups
7. Plan how to involve them in challenging your thinking
8. Validate that they have enough distance to be objective but enough knowledge to be helpful

## Coaching Questions
- Who might see this situation differently but still understand our context?
- How can I bring in people who are "close enough" to understand but "far enough" to challenge?
- What perspectives are missing from our current problem-solving group?
- Who might challenge our assumptions without requiring extensive background explanation?
- How might someone from a related but different function view this issue?
- What are the advantages of involving partial outsiders rather than complete strangers?
- Who could serve as a bridge between our team and external perspectives?
- How might these boundary spanners help us see blind spots in our approach?

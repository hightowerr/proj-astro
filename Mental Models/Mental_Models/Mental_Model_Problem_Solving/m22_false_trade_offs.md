# Mental Model: False Trade-offs

## Description
False Trade-offs occur when problems present themselves as a choice between two predefined options, creating the illusion of completeness and freedom of choice when better alternatives may exist. People often accept apparent trade-offs (quality vs. cost, speed vs. accuracy) without questioning whether these are the only options. Creative thinkers push back on such trade-offs, exploring the issue more deeply to generate a superior option that transcends the apparent either-or choice. The approach challenges whether the presented options are truly the only possibilities.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The trade-off represents real, physical or logical constraints
- Time constraints don't allow for exploring alternatives
- The trade-off has been thoroughly validated as the only options
- The situation involves strict regulatory or compliance requirements
- The stakeholders absolutely require a binary decision
- The cost of exploring alternatives exceeds potential benefits
- The trade-off represents genuinely incompatible requirements

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing "can't do both" scenarios in business decisions
- When stakeholders present only two options for decision-making
- When resource allocation seems to require zero-sum choices
- When encountering traditional either-or decisions in business
- When trying to improve products or services with apparent constraints
- When facing decisions between competing priorities
- When stakeholders seem to have "limited options" that may not be exhaustive
- When encountering "industry standard" trade-offs that might not apply

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the apparent trade-off being presented
2. Question the framing: "Are these the only options available?"
3. Examine the assumptions embedded in the trade-off
4. Look for ways to satisfy both sides of the apparent trade-off
5. Consider what conditions would make both sides possible
6. Explore what successful people do in similar situations
7. Research how others have approached similar trade-offs
8. Create new options that transcend the apparent either-or choice

## Coaching Questions
- Who defined these as the only two options?
- What assumptions are embedded in this trade-off?
- How might someone from a different industry approach this?
- What would need to change to make both sides of this trade-off possible?
- Are we accepting the framing others have provided without questioning?
- What if the constraints we think exist aren't actually constraints?
- How do the most successful people handle this apparent trade-off?
- What would happen if we refused to accept this trade-off?

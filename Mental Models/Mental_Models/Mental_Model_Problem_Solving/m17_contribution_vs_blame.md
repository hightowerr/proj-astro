# Mental Model: Contribution vs Blame

## Description
Contribution vs Blame is the practice of focusing on understanding how different factors contributed to an outcome rather than assigning fault. Instead of asking "Who's to blame?" which implies someone did something objectively wrong and should be punished, this approach asks "How did this happen?" and focuses on understanding what everyone contributed to the situation. This perspective is forward-looking, helping identify all possible avenues of improvement rather than focusing on just one person's actions, and recognizes that mistakes are typically the result of multiple people's actions.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- Legal accountability or compliance requires identifying specific blame
- Safety protocols require specific accountability for violations
- The situation involves criminal or highly unethical behavior
- Stakeholders require clear accountability before moving forward
- The organization culture is so punitive that contribution analysis won't be accepted
- There's a need to enforce clear consequences for specific actions
- The situation involves fraud or deliberate harmful intent

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing team conflicts or interpersonal issues
- When trying to understand why projects failed or had problems
- When dealing with process breakdowns or system failures
- When encountering resistance to change initiatives
- When facing customer issues or complaints
- When analyzing why solutions haven't worked as expected
- When trying to improve organizational culture or processes
- When facing challenges in cross-functional collaboration

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify all parties who might have contributed to the situation
2. Focus on understanding each person's role without assigning fault
3. Look for system-level factors that influenced everyone's behavior
4. Consider how different incentives might have contributed to the outcome
5. Examine environmental or structural factors that contributed
6. Avoid the assumption that there's a single cause or responsible party
7. Focus on what can be changed going forward rather than past accountability
8. Identify all possible improvement avenues rather than just one person's behavior

## Coaching Questions
- How did different people contribute to this situation?
- What system or environmental factors influenced the outcome?
- How can I understand this situation without focusing on blame?
- What would change if we looked at everyone's contribution rather than fault?
- How might different incentives have contributed to this outcome?
- What improvement opportunities exist beyond changing one person's behavior?
- How can we learn from this without making someone feel defensive?
- What environmental or structural factors contributed to this issue?

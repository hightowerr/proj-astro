# Mental Model: Anchoring and Adjustment

## Description
Anchoring and Adjustment describes the cognitive process where people start with an initial piece of information (the "anchor") and then make adjustments to reach their final estimate or judgment. In perspective-taking, anchoring happens when you imagine yourself stepping into someone else's shoes and ask "How would I feel if I were in their situation?" Adjustment then means deliberately modifying away from your own preferences, experiences, and emotions to consider how others might see things differently. This mental model helps understand why people often fail to adequately consider other perspectives.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The other person has significantly different experiences or values
- Time constraints don't allow for adequate adjustment from your anchor
- The stakes are extremely high and perspective error could be costly
- You lack sufficient knowledge about the other person's context
- The situation requires expert knowledge you don't possess
- Cultural differences are so significant that your anchor is completely inappropriate
- You're under stress or time pressure which reduces adjustment ability

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing conflicts or disagreements with others
- When trying to understand customer needs or behaviors
- When developing solutions for different user groups
- When encountering resistance to initiatives or changes
- When making decisions that affect other stakeholders
- When facing challenges in cross-cultural or diverse teams
- When trying to understand why others make decisions you disagree with
- When developing communication or marketing approaches

## Thinking Steps
1. Start by considering your own perspective as the initial anchor
2. Deliberately identify differences between your situation and theirs
3. Adjust your thinking based on their different context, values, and experiences
4. Consider what they know that you don't know (and vice versa)
5. Examine their incentives and what they're being measured on
6. Think beyond your first assumption about their perspective
7. Remember that groups may react in different ways, not as one entity
8. Validate your adjusted perspective when possible with real feedback

## Coaching Questions
- How might I be anchoring my assumptions about others based on my own experience?
- What would change if I adjusted significantly from my initial assumption?
- What context do they have that I don't have?
- How might different incentives shape their perspective?
- What if I'm not adjusting enough from my own perspective?
- How might people with different backgrounds view this issue?
- Are there multiple perspectives within the group I'm considering?
- How can I test whether my adjusted perspective is accurate?

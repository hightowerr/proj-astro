# Mental Model: Law of the Instrument

## Description
Law of the Instrument, also known as "Maslow's Hammer," describes the cognitive bias where people tend to frame problems to match whatever tools or techniques they are most proficient in. The phrase comes from Abraham Kaplan's observation that "Give a small boy a hammer, and he will find that everything he encounters needs pounding." In problem-solving contexts, this means that people often default to solutions that match their expertise, even when those solutions may not be appropriate for the problem at hand.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The person's expertise is genuinely the most relevant to the problem
- Time constraints require leveraging existing expertise quickly
- The problem is clearly within the domain of the expert's knowledge
- There are no alternative perspectives available
- The stakes are low and any solution will work adequately
- The team is already diverse and considering multiple approaches
- The solution expertise has a proven track record with similar problems

## Keywords for Situations
- When team members immediately propose solutions from their area of expertise
- When problems are being defined to fit available solutions rather than actual needs
- When stakeholders have different solutions for the same problem based on their roles
- When facing cross-functional challenges requiring diverse expertise
- When encountering resistance to approaches outside people's expertise
- When solutions seem to be "looking for problems" rather than vice versa
- When single-discipline approaches consistently fail to solve complex issues
- When stakeholders are siloed by functional expertise

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify your own preferred "hammer" or go-to solution approach
2. Question whether the problem genuinely fits your preferred approach
3. Look for problems that might be better solved by other tools/expertise
4. Invite perspectives from people with different expertise areas
5. Explicitly ask "what if our preferred approach is not the right one?"
6. Consider if the problem statement is being shaped to fit the solution
7. Test the same problem with multiple different approaches/frames
8. Validate that your expertise is truly appropriate for the problem at hand

## Coaching Questions
- Am I trying to use my favorite tool for a problem that doesn't require it?
- What problem am I trying to solve versus what solution am I trying to use?
- How might someone from a different discipline approach this issue?
- Are we framing this problem to match our expertise or does the problem genuinely require our expertise?
- What would happen if we approached this without our typical tools?
- Who else should be involved whose expertise might be more appropriate?
- Am I solving the right problem or just using my preferred approach?
- What other perspectives would challenge my go-to solution?

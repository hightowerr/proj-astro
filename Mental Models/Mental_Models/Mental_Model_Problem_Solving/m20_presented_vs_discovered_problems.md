# Mental Model: Presented vs Discovered Problems

## Description
Presented vs Discovered Problems distinguishes between two types of problems: Presented problems are clearly stated with known methods of solving and clear endpoints (like typical math problems in school). Discovered problems are ill-defined, may not even be recognized initially, have no known method for solving, and it may not be clear when the problem can be considered solved. Understanding this distinction helps problem-solvers recognize when they need to spend more time defining and exploring the problem rather than jumping into solution mode.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The problem is truly straightforward with a clear solution path
- Time constraints require immediate action without problem exploration
- The solution is standardized and well-understood in the domain
- The situation is an emergency requiring immediate response
- The problem is well-documented with known parameters
- Resources are limited for problem exploration
- The context is well-understood and the problem is routine

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing complex organizational challenges without clear solutions
- When stakeholders present problems that seem too simplistic
- When solutions to identified problems keep failing
- When dealing with ambiguous customer needs or complaints
- When encountering resistance to solutions that should work
- When facing challenges that cross multiple departments or functions
- When dealing with "wicked problems" that have multiple perspectives
- When traditional approaches to problems aren't working

## Thinking Steps
1. Assess whether the problem was presented to you or discovered through observation
2. Determine if the problem is well-defined with known solution methods
3. Identify if the problem statement is complete or might be masking deeper issues
4. Look for signs that this might be a discovered problem in disguise
5. Question whether the stated problem matches the symptoms you observe
6. Explore whether the problem definition is too narrow or too broad
7. Consider what might be missing from the problem presentation
8. Validate whether the problem definition aligns with desired outcomes

## Coaching Questions
- Is this problem clearly defined with a known solution path?
- What if the problem as presented is masking a different underlying issue?
- Have I encountered similar symptoms that might suggest a different problem?
- How might different stakeholders define the problem differently?
- What would this situation look like if the stated problem was wrong?
- Are there aspects of this situation that don't fit the problem description?
- What if the solution requirements change how we should frame the problem?
- How might I approach this differently if it were a discovered rather than presented problem?

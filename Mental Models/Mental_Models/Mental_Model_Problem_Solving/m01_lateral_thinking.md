# Lateral Thinking

## Description
Lateral thinking is a creative approach to problem-solving that involves thinking "outside the box" - often illogically and unconventionally. It challenges conventional wisdom and encourages finding alternative solutions by looking at problems from unexpected angles. The concept was developed by Edward de Bono who proposed four principles: (1) Recognize dominant ideas and ways of thinking, (2) Search for other ways to look at things, (3) Relax the strict control applied to rational-logical thinking, and (4) Consciously make use of choice. Rather than following traditional problem-solving approaches, lateral thinking explores alternative pathways to reach solutions.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Lateral thinking should be used with caution in highly regulated environments where strict compliance is required, or in safety-critical situations where unconventional approaches could have serious consequences. It may also be problematic in time-sensitive crisis situations that require immediate, conventional responses. Avoid using it when dealing with well-established, proven procedures that have demonstrated effectiveness, or when working with stakeholders who are resistant to change and innovation. This approach may also not be suitable for straightforward, routine tasks that follow clear, established protocols.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the conventional approach to your problem - what is everyone else doing?
2. Recognize the dominant ideas and assumptions about how things are typically done
3. Challenge the assumptions by asking "what if the opposite were true?" or "what if we did nothing like this?"
4. Explore completely different perspectives on the problem - how would someone from another field approach this?
5. Use techniques like alteration (changing elements), mind-mapping (visualizing relationships), or random simulation (introducing random elements)
6. Suspend judgment and criticism temporarily to allow for wild ideas to emerge
7. Look for analogies from other industries or situations that might apply
8. Consider what elements are taken for granted and question whether they're necessary
9. Deconstruct large problems into smaller, separate components to address differently
10. Relax the rigid control of logical thinking to allow creative connections to form

## Coaching Questions
- What is the conventional way of approaching this problem in your industry?
- What assumptions are you making that might be limiting your thinking?
- How would someone from a completely different field approach this challenge?
- What elements of the current approach could you remove or completely change?
- If the obvious solution wasn't available, what would you do instead?
- How could you reverse the traditional approach to this problem?
- What random elements or inspirations from other contexts could apply here?
- What would happen if you ignored the constraints you think you have?
- How might a child approach this problem differently?
- What would your competitors be least expecting you to do?

# Mental Model: Fundamental Attribution Error

## Description
Fundamental Attribution Error is the cognitive bias where people instinctively conclude that others' behaviors are due to their character or personality rather than situational factors. When faced with problematic behavior, we tend to think "they are bad people" or "they are inherently difficult," rather than considering external circumstances that might be driving their actions. This bias causes us to attribute others' negative behaviors to their disposition while attributing our own negative behaviors to external circumstances.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- Dealing with genuinely malicious or harmful individuals who pose real threats
- Safety is at immediate risk and understanding context isn't the priority
- The person's behavior genuinely stems from character flaws that are unchangeable
- Time constraints don't allow for considering situational factors
- You need to protect yourself from individuals who are genuinely harmful
- The situation requires immediate accountability rather than understanding
- The person has consistently shown no regard for consequences of their actions

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing interpersonal conflicts or difficult relationships
- When employees or team members are not performing as expected
- When customers are complaining or behaving negatively
- When encountering resistance to organizational changes
- When dealing with stakeholder opposition to initiatives
- When facing challenges in cross-cultural or diverse environments
- When trying to understand why others make decisions you disagree with
- When encountering recurring problems with people behaviors

## Thinking Steps
1. Notice when you're attributing someone's behavior to their character
2. Ask "What situational factors might be influencing their behavior?"
3. Consider what external pressures, constraints, or incentives might be at play
4. Look for evidence that contradicts your initial attribution
5. Think about how you might behave in their exact situation
6. Research what information they might have that you don't have
7. Consider what reasonable explanations might exist for their actions
8. Test your situational explanations against available evidence

## Coaching Questions
- What external factors might be influencing this person's behavior?
- How might I act if I were in their exact situation?
- What pressures or constraints might they be facing that I'm not seeing?
- What information do they have that I don't have?
- What would I do if I faced the same incentives or requirements?
- How might their values or priorities be different from mine?
- What reasonable explanations might exist for their actions?
- What if they're trying to do the right thing but from a different perspective?

# Mental Model: Promotion vs Prevention Focus

## Description
Promotion vs Prevention Focus describes two different motivational orientations that affect how people evaluate new ideas and approaches. People with a promotion focus are motivated to act when there are things to be gained and opportunities to pursue. They are oriented toward advancement, growth, and achievement of positive outcomes. People with a prevention focus are concerned with avoiding failure and losses, and are motivated by responsibilities and safety. Understanding these different orientations helps frame problems and solutions in ways that resonate with different stakeholders based on their motivational approach.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The audience is mixed with both promotional and preventive orientations
- The situation requires a single unified message for all stakeholders
- Time constraints don't allow for message customization
- The problem doesn't align well with either promotional or preventive framing
- The context is too complex to simplify into motivational orientations
- Stakeholders' orientations are not clearly identifiable
- The team lacks understanding of how to tailor messages effectively

## Keywords for Situations
- When developing communication strategies for diverse audiences
- When facing resistance to change initiatives from different stakeholder groups
- When trying to gain buy-in for new initiatives or solutions
- When marketing products or services to different customer segments
- When developing incentive systems or reward structures
- When trying to influence decision-makers with different priorities
- When facing challenges in cross-functional team collaboration
- When developing training or change management approaches

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the motivational orientation of key stakeholders
2. Assess whether they are primarily promotion-focused (seeking gains) or prevention-focused (avoiding losses)
3. Tailor problem framing to match their orientation (gains vs. risks)
4. Adjust solution presentation to align with their motivations
5. Consider how to present the same solution differently for different orientations
6. Evaluate the language and metaphors that resonate with each orientation
7. Test your approach with representatives from each orientation group
8. Adapt communication strategies based on feedback and effectiveness

## Coaching Questions
- How do different stakeholders view the potential outcomes of this situation?
- Are we speaking to people's desire for gains or their need to avoid losses?
- How might someone focused on advancement view this opportunity?
- How might someone concerned about safety view this change?
- What language resonates with each stakeholder group?
- How can we frame this solution to appeal to different motivational orientations?
- Are we unintentionally appealing to the wrong orientation?
- How might we adapt our message for both orientations?

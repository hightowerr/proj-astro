# Mental Model: Discovery-Oriented Conversations

## Description
Discovery-Oriented Conversations are discussions specifically designed for learning rather than telling, with the primary intent of uncovering new information, insights, or perspectives about problems. These conversations involve active listening, asking open-ended questions, and creating psychological safety for others to share information they might not otherwise reveal. The approach helps uncover hidden causes of problems, identifies unexpected factors, and surfaces information that might only emerge through careful dialogue rather than formal data collection methods.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- Time constraints require immediate action rather than information gathering
- The conversation partner is not trustworthy or reliable
- The situation requires formal documentation or evidence
- The person being spoken with might provide misleading or false information
- The context is too sensitive for informal conversations
- The problem requires technical validation rather than human insights
- The conversation partner lacks relevant knowledge or experience

## Keywords for Situations
- When facing problems with unknown or unclear causes
- When trying to understand customer needs or behaviors
- When encountering resistance to solutions without understanding reasons
- When seeking to understand organizational culture or dynamics
- When trying to identify root causes of recurring issues
- When facing challenges that formal surveys or studies haven't resolved
- When seeking to understand the "real story" behind reported problems
- When needing to gather insights from people who might not speak up in formal settings

## Thinking Steps
1. Set the intention to listen and learn rather than to provide solutions
2. Create a safe space for the other person to share openly
3. Ask open-ended questions that encourage detailed responses
4. Listen for unexpected details or implications in their responses
5. Pay attention to emotional undertones and what's not being said
6. Look for clues that might explain the root causes of problems
7. Resist the urge to offer solutions during the discovery phase
8. Validate understanding by reflecting back what you've heard

## Coaching Questions
- What can I learn from this person that I might not get from other sources?
- How can I create a safe space for them to share what's really happening?
- What questions might reveal unexpected insights about the situation?
- How can I listen for details that might explain the problem differently?
- What assumptions am I making that might be challenged by this conversation?
- How might I encourage them to share information they might otherwise withhold?
- What emotional aspects of the situation might be important to understand?
- How can I listen more than I talk during this discussion?

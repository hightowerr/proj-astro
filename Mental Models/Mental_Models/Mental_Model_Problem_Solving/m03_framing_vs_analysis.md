# Mental Model: Framing vs Analysis

## Description
Framing vs Analysis distinguishes between two different approaches to problem-solving. Analysis involves asking "Why is the elevator slow?" and trying to understand the various factors that influence the speed - being precise, methodical, detail-oriented, and good with numbers. Framing, on the other hand, asks "Is the speed of the elevator the right thing to focus on?" - focusing on seeing the big picture and having the ability to consider situations from multiple perspectives. Effective problem-solving requires both approaches, with framing helping to identify the right problem to solve and analysis helping to understand the selected problem deeply.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
This distinction should be used with caution when:
- The problem is already well-defined and clearly understood by all stakeholders
- Time constraints require immediate analytical action
- The problem is straightforward with clear cause-and-effect relationships
- There is a critical need to optimize an already-identified solution quickly
- The team has already completed a thorough framing exercise
- You are implementing a proven solution to a known problem
- In crisis situations requiring immediate analytical response rather than reframing

## Keywords for Situations
- When teams are getting lost in technical details without questioning the problem
- When solutions to identified problems are failing despite good analytical work
- When stakeholders seem to be solving the wrong problem despite good analysis
- When facing complex challenges with multiple potential approaches
- When there's disagreement between analytical results and actual outcomes
- When encountering resistance to data-driven solutions
- When resources are being wasted on technical fixes to problem-framing issues
- When facing recurring problems despite thorough analytical solutions

## Thinking Steps
1. Assess if the current challenge is an analysis problem (we know what to solve) or a framing problem (we need to identify the right problem)
2. If analysis-focused, ask: "Is this the right problem to be analyzing?"
3. If framing-focused, ask: "What would we need to analyze once we've identified the right problem?"
4. Determine if your team is spending too much time on details (analysis mode) or not enough time on details (framing mode)
5. Switch between modes when getting stuck in one approach
6. Validate your problem framing with analysis before diving deep into solutions
7. Balance time spent on broad perspective taking vs. detailed analysis
8. Recognize when a shift in approach is needed based on results and progress

## Coaching Questions
- Are we solving the right problem, or are we just getting better at solving the wrong problem?
- What would change if we approached this from a different angle?
- How much time are we spending on analysis versus questioning our assumptions?
- Is our data helping us understand the right problem or just giving us precise details about the wrong one?
- What if we reversed our current approach - would that provide new insights?
- How might someone from a different field frame this situation?
- What are we not seeing because we're focused on analysis details?
- Is our team spending more time on technical precision or strategic problem identification?

# Mental Model: Look Outside the Frame

## Description
Look Outside the Frame is the practice of deliberately examining what's missing from your current problem statement or perspective. The mental model addresses the danger of framing problems too narrowly, where everything within the frame is carefully scrutinized while everything outside receives zero attention. This unconscious framing effect often leads to missing critical elements that could provide better solutions. The strategy involves zooming out and asking "What's missing from the current problem statement?" or "Are there elements we're not considering?"

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- Time constraints require immediate action within the current frame
- The frame has been thoroughly validated and is accurate
- Resources are extremely limited for broader exploration
- The problem is well-defined with clear boundaries
- Stakeholders are not open to changing the problem scope
- The current frame is already addressing all critical elements
- You're implementing a time-sensitive emergency response

## Keywords for Situations
- When solutions within the current frame keep failing
- When facing complex problems with multiple contributing factors
- When stakeholders have different perspectives on the problem
- When encountering unexpected complications to planned solutions
- When dealing with system-wide challenges affecting multiple areas
- When analyzing recurring problems that haven't been solved
- When stakeholders feel important elements are being ignored
- When solutions work in some contexts but not others

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify what's currently included in your problem frame
2. Ask "What might be missing from this picture?"
3. Consider perspectives from stakeholders outside the current frame
4. Look at what happened before the problem emerged (prior events)
5. Examine if there are hidden influences or confounding variables
6. Identify nonobvious aspects of the situation that might be relevant
7. Consider how different systems or contexts might be connected
8. Validate that your frame captures all relevant elements

## Coaching Questions
- What assumptions am I making about what's relevant to this problem?
- Who might be affected by this issue that I haven't considered?
- What happened before this problem became apparent?
- Are there stakeholders whose influence I'm missing?
- What aspects of this situation might have non-obvious properties?
- How might different systems be interacting to create this problem?
- What do I think I know that might be incorrect?
- Who outside my immediate context might have a completely different view?

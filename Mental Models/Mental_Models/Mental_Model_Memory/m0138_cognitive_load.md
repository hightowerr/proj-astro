**Description: Core concept explanation**  
Cognitive load is the amount of mental effort required to understand information or complete a task. Interfaces, workflows, or communications that present too many choices, unfamiliar patterns, or frequent context switches increase load—slowing decisions, causing errors, and prompting abandonment. Reducing options (e.g., Hick’s Law), using familiar patterns, clear navigation, and fast feedback lowers the mental overhead so people can focus on their goal.

- **When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
    
    - When simplification hides essential choices or removes expert controls users need.
    - When novelty/originality breaks learned conventions (e.g., non-standard field shapes), creating confusion.
    - When forms or layouts force long visual saccades (e.g., poorly aligned labels), increasing effort and time.
    - When navigation scatters tasks across many pages or loads slowly, adding “context switch” cost and discouraging exploration.
        
- **Keywords for Situations:**
    
    - Too many choices, “busy” screens, hidden controls, endless menus, deep hierarchies, slow page loads, inconsistent patterns, unfamiliar layouts, dense forms, lack of whitespace, nonstandard components, long onboarding, missing signposts.
        
- **Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively**
    
    1. **Clarify the job-to-be-done.** What must the user decide or complete right now? Eliminate or defer everything else (progressive disclosure).
        
    2. **Trim choices.** Collapse, sequence, or prioritize options; surface only the most common/frequent actions at the top level (apply Hick’s Law + frequency weighting).
        
    3. **Leverage conventions.** Prefer familiar patterns (standard fields, buttons, navigation placements) over novelty for its own sake.
        
    4. **Shorten paths.** Reduce clicks/taps between A→B; keep critical actions one step away when possible.
        
    5. **Stabilize context.** Keep lists and details visible together (e.g., split/two-panel views) to avoid memory burdens and reorientation.
        
    6. **Design the visual hierarchy.** Use grids, grouping, whitespace, and consistent styling to guide scanning and reduce visual noise.
        
    7. **Optimize forms for effort.** Align labels to minimize eye travel; use forgiving formats, input masks, and smart defaults; split complex flows into small steps.
        
    8. **Give timely cues and feedback.** Provide clear signposts (breadcrumbs, progress), helpful empty states, and visual prompts to the next step.
        
    9. **Engineer speed.** Reduce latency; slow loads magnify cognitive cost and hurt exploration.
        
    10. **Test and iterate.** Observe where users hesitate, backtrack, or abandon; adjust layout, copy, and sequencing to lower load.
        
- **Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model**
    
    - What single decision do users need to make on this screen—and what can I hide or defer until after that decision?
    - Where are we breaking conventions that would otherwise help users “recognize” instead of “recall”?
    - Can we shorten the path to the top 3 actions by one tap/click each?
    - Which elements compete for attention, and how can grid, spacing, and contrast clarify hierarchy?
    - Do our forms and onboarding flows minimize eye travel and steps, with forgiving formats and smart defaults?
    - Where does latency or page reloads force reorientation? How can we keep context stable (e.g., split views, inline updates)?
    - What cues (progress, breadcrumbs, prompts, empty-state guidance) help users know where they are and what’s next?
        

**Assigned Category:** Memory

_Notes from source:_

- Minimizing choices and following conventions lowers cognitive load; overly original components confuse users.
    
- Page/screen changes and loads create context-switch costs; flatten hierarchies and elevate frequent actions.
    
- Two-panel layouts reduce memory burden by keeping list + detail in view.
    
- Visual grids, grouping, and whitespace reduce visual cognitive load.
    
- Form design choices (label alignment, forgiving formats) materially affect cognitive load and completion time.

**Category:** Memory

**Description:**  
The _Availability Heuristic_ describes how people judge the likelihood or frequency of events based on how easily examples come to mind. The easier it is to recall an instance, the more common or probable it feels — regardless of actual statistical frequency. Media coverage, personal experiences, and emotional salience distort these judgments, leading people to overestimate vivid or recent risks (e.g., shark attacks, plane crashes) and underestimate mundane but frequent ones (e.g., heart disease, car accidents).

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

- When judging probabilities based on memorable or emotional cases instead of data.
- When exposure frequency (e.g., news, anecdotes) misrepresents base rates.
- When planning for rare but catastrophic events (e.g., financial crises, pandemics).
- When scenarios are vivid or plausible but not representative of reality.
- When using “gut feeling” forecasts rather than structured analysis.

---

**Keywords for Situations:**

- Risk perception and forecasting
- Media influence and public fear
- Decision-making under uncertainty
- Behavioral finance and probability estimation
- Scenario planning and narrative persuasion
- Cognitive biases in memory and prediction

---

**Thinking Steps:**

1. **Recognize the Cue** — Notice when your judgment relies on what’s most easily recalled.
2. **Check Representativeness** — Ask whether the recalled instance is typical or extreme.
3. **Seek Base Rates** — Look up or estimate actual frequencies before judging likelihood.
4. **Neutralize Recency** — Adjust for how recent or emotionally vivid an example is.
5. **Test Counterexamples** — Actively recall opposite or less salient cases.
6. **Quantify** — Where possible, anchor reasoning in data or historical rates.

---

**Coaching Questions:**

- What examples came to mind first, and why might they be so vivid?
- How often does this event _actually_ occur compared to how often it’s discussed?
- Would I judge this differently if I hadn’t recently seen or heard about a similar event?
- What data or evidence could contradict my current sense of likelihood?
- How might emotions like fear or familiarity be distorting my perception of risk?

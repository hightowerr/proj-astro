**Category:** Memory

People remember grouped information better

Description: Chunking is grouping information or actions into small, meaningful units so our limited short-term memory can process and recall them more easily. Classic guidance suggested “7±2” items, but modern evidence often points closer to “4±1,” and chunking is especially helpful under stress or when tasks are complex. In interfaces and workflows, chunking also means breaking features or steps into bite-size sections (e.g., menus, tabs, wizards). 

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

  * Long, continuous indexes where scanning is faster than grouping (e.g., A–Z lists) — chunking can slow people down. 
  * Over-chunking (too many tiny groups or steps) that creates tedious navigation (e.g., a 15-step wizard). 
  * Treating “7±2” as a hard rule; design for fewer items when possible, since short-term memory may be closer to ~4 chunks. 
  * Expert workflows where users want dense, direct control rather than staged, chunked guidance. 

**Keywords for Situations:**

  * Onboarding • Step-by-step wizards • Tabs/accordions • Menu grouping • Card layouts • Progressive disclosure • Smart defaults • High-stress controls • Feature prioritization by “feature chunks.” 

**Thinking Steps:**

  1. Define the unit: What exactly should be remembered or decided? Convert raw items into meaningful, labeled groups. Aim for ~3–5 per level. 
  2. Match the task: If users must evaluate options, group into chunks; if they must locate in an ordered list, consider alphabetical/time anchors instead. 
  3. Structure the UI: Use menus, tabs, titled sections, accordions, or collapsible panels to surface a few groups, then reveal details within each. 
  4. Sequence complex tasks: Turn long processes into a short series of clear steps (wizard/progressive disclosure), balancing step count vs. step size. 
  5. Reduce cognitive load: Prefer recognition over recall (labels, icons), apply smart defaults, and keep each chunk self-contained. 
  6. Stress-proof: For critical or high-pressure actions, limit visible options and cluster essential controls for fast, reliable use. 
  7. Test & tune: Card-sort to find natural groupings; watch for over-chunking (too many clicks) vs. under-chunking (overwhelm). 

**Coaching Questions:**

  * If a new user had to explain this screen from memory, what 3–5 labeled groups would they name? 
  * Would alphabetical/time anchors be faster here than grouping? Why? 
  * Which step in this process feels heaviest — can we split or prefill it? 
  * Under stress, which controls must remain visible, and which can be hidden until needed? 
  * Are we leaning on the outdated “7±2,” or can we simplify to ~4 items per level?

**Category:** Memory

People remember incomplete tasks better than completed ones

**Description:**
Incomplete or interrupted tasks stick in mind more than completed ones. The mind seeks closure, so “open loops” keep attention and are easier to recall; once a task is finished, the recall advantage fades. This is why cliffhangers work and why waiters remember unpaid orders better than settled ones. 

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

  * Creating too many open loops can fuel stress, rumination, and sleep disruption. Close or externalize low-value loops. 
  * Low motivation tasks show weaker effects; don’t rely on it to drive follow-through where intrinsic/extrinsic motivation is absent. 
  * Ethically dubious to manipulate attention with never-ending cliffhangers; overuse can frustrate users/learners. 
  * The advantage vanishes at completion—don’t assume lasting memory without reinforcement. 

**Keywords for Situations:**
  Studying with breaks; to-do lists & task handoffs; onboarding/progress indicators; episodic storytelling & “to be continued”; puzzles/games; sales & content teasers; writing workflows & GTD. 

**Thinking Steps:**

  1. **Define the loop:** State the specific question or task so the mind can “grab” it. 
  2. **Introduce (or notice) an interruption:** Pause intentionally (e.g., mid-chapter, mid-draft) to harness attentional pull. 
  3. **Externalize to manage load:** Write the open loop and next action in a trusted system; this preserves the benefit without mental clutter. 
  4. **Use micro-closures:** Set sub-goals and checkpoints to prevent anxiety while keeping momentum. 
  5. **Re-expose strategically:** Revisit the loop after a break; attention and recall are primed, especially when motivation is high. 
  6. **Close decisively:** Complete or consciously park the task (captured in writing) to release cognitive tension. 

**Coaching Questions:**

  * What single question or unfinished task do I want my mind to keep working on? 
  * Which open loops deserve to stay “active,” and which should I capture and park to reduce noise? 
  * Where can I insert a purposeful break or cliffhanger to boost recall/return (lesson plans, emails, product tours)? 
  * What’s the smallest “next action” that moves this loop toward closure without killing momentum? 
  * After closing this loop, how will I reinforce retention (review, summary, spaced follow-up)? 

**Notes from the source:**

* Core effect and waiter example; stronger under high motivation; effective in cliffhangers and study breaks. 
* Writing tasks down can quiet intrusive thoughts by assuring the brain the task will be handled—useful for GTD-style systems.

**Description: Core concept explanation**
A method for structuring thinking and communication as a pyramid: start with the governing thought (answer/point) at the top, then group and order supporting ideas underneath so that each higher-level point is a concise summary of the ideas below (vertical logic) and sibling points form a coherent argument (horizontal logic). Readers process this best because they naturally ask “why/how?” after each statement; moving down the pyramid answers those questions in order. Intros are framed as a short story using Situation → Complication → Question → Answer (SCQA) to set up the top point. Groupings must be logically ordered (by time/sequence, structure/parts, or degree/classes), and each group should be justified—either actions that together create an effect or items sharing a meaningful similarity. 

- When ideas are arranged in a pyramid, you can communicate by stating the top point first, then descending each branch in a Q&A flow; this mirrors how readers think and clarifies your own thinking.  
- Logical ordering options for grouped ideas: time order (process/steps), structural order (whole→parts), or degree order (classes/MECE).
- Intros use the SCQA story form to tee up the governing thought and key line.  

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**
- When you don’t yet have a single governing thought: do bottom-up thinking first to discover it; otherwise you’ll force a weak top point.  
- When your “groups” are merely lists of loosely related items or “news” facts with no internal logic—convert to true inductive/deductive groupings or leave them out.  
- When the logic within a group isn’t clear (e.g., mixed causes, parts, and categories). Choose one analytical lens (time/structure/degree) and reorder; don’t blend.  
- When sibling points aren’t MECE (overlapping or with gaps). Refine the grouping so the summary above remains valid and complete.  

**Keywords for Situations**
Executive summary; slide decks; memos & reports; strategy narratives; proposals; problem definition; logic trees; SCQA introductions; MECE groupings; deductive vs. inductive arguments; top-down communication. 

**Thinking Steps**
1. Clarify the ask with SCQA. Write the Situation and Complication that raise the reader’s key Question; draft your Answer (top point).  
2. Draft the key line (Level 2). List 2–4 reasons or actions that, together, prove the top point; ensure they are mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive and pick one logical order (time, structure, or degree).  
3. Build down each branch. For every key-line point, ask “why/how?” and answer with the next level of ideas; continue until you reach facts, data, or exhibits.  
4. Check vertical logic. Confirm each parent is a true summary (effect of actions or insight from similarity) and each child answers the parent’s implied question.  
5. Check horizontal logic. Make sure siblings form a valid argument (deductive “therefore” line or inductive similarity) and obey the chosen order type.  
6. Eliminate “news.” Remove unrelated facts that don’t support a higher point; convert lists into insight-bearing groupings.  
7. Present top-down. State the governing thought first, then walk the pyramid branch by branch; in prose/slides, reflect the hierarchy so readers can “see” the structure.  

**Coaching Questions**
- What single governing thought (answer) do I want the reader to walk away with?  
- Does my introduction clearly show the Situation, the Complication it creates, the implied Question, and my Answer?  
- For each group: am I summarizing an effect of actions or an insight from similarity—and have I stated that explicitly?  
- Are my sibling points in time, structure (parts), or degree (classes) order—and strictly one of those? What would make them MECE?  
- At every level, what “why/how?” will the reader ask next—and do I answer it in the very next layer?  
- Which items are just “news” or decoration that don’t defend a higher point—and can be cut?

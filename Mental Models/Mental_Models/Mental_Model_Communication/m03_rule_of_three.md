# Rule Of Three

## Description
The Rule of Three is a principle stating that when trying to persuade someone to do something, you should always begin by articulating three reasons. Not two, not four, but three reasons. This rule originated as advice for consultants, who were told to respond with three reasons when clients asked, "Why should we do this?" The rule is effective because it captures attention, improves recall, forces analytical thinking, and projects confidence and structure. The rule stems from ancient Greek philosophy, where Aristotle observed that people remember things grouped in threes best. The Rule of Three appears in rhetoric, literature, fairy tales, and many other contexts, suggesting our human psyche equates the number 3 with stability, completeness, and balance.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
This approach should be used with caution when the situation calls for more than three reasons, which would require sacrificing important information. Avoid using it if three reasons would be insufficient to convince your audience or if the complexity of the topic requires a more nuanced explanation. It may also not work when your audience expects brevity and even three reasons would be too much detail. Additionally, be careful not to artificially force only three reasons when the situation genuinely warrants other numbers, as this might seem manipulative or inauthentic.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the core decision or action you want your audience to make
2. Brainstorm all possible reasons that support your recommendation
3. Categorize and prioritize the reasons based on impact and relevance
4. Select the three most compelling reasons that support your case
5. Arrange the three reasons in a logical or impactful sequence
6. Ensure the three reasons are distinct and not repetitive
7. Test that the three reasons together form a compelling argument
8. Verify that three reasons are sufficient for your audience's decision-making needs
9. Prepare supportive evidence for each of your three main reasons
10. Practice delivering the three reasons in a memorable and impactful way

## Coaching Questions
- What are the three most important reasons your audience should take your recommended action?
- How do these three reasons address your audience's main concerns or values?
- Would adding a fourth reason strengthen or dilute your argument?
- Are your three reasons distinct and not just variations of the same point?
- How can you make your three reasons more memorable to your audience?
- What objections might be raised, and how do your three reasons address them?
- How can you present these three reasons in a compelling narrative?
- Which of these three reasons is most likely to resonate with your specific audience?
- How can you sequence your three reasons for maximum impact?
- What evidence or examples can support each of your three reasons?

# Rule Of Three

## Description
The Rule of Three is a mental model stating that when trying to persuade someone, you should always begin by articulating three reasons. This principle originates from advice to consultants who were told to respond with three reasons when clients asked, "Why should we do this?" The rule is effective because it captures attention, improves retention, forces analytical thinking, and projects confidence and structure. The concept stems from ancient Greek philosophy where Aristotle observed that people remember things grouped in threes best. The human brain finds information in groups of three easier to remember and more persuasive than other groupings.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when the situation genuinely requires more or fewer than three reasons. Avoid when three reasons might be insufficient for complex decisions requiring more detailed justification. Don't use this model when your audience expects comprehensive analysis with many supporting factors. Avoid the rule when the three-reason constraint might limit important information needed for decision-making. Also be cautious when the three-reason constraint might seem artificial or manipulative to your audience.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the core decision or action you want your audience to take
2. Brainstorm all possible reasons that support your recommendation
3. Categorize and prioritize the reasons based on impact and relevance
4. Select the three most compelling reasons that support your case
5. Arrange the three reasons in a logical or impactful sequence
6. Ensure the three reasons are distinct and not repetitive
7. Test that the three reasons together form a compelling argument
8. Verify that three reasons are sufficient for your audience's decision-making needs
9. Prepare supportive evidence for each of your three main reasons
10. Practice delivering the three reasons in a memorable and impactful way

## Coaching Questions
- What are the three most important reasons your audience should take your recommended action?
- How do these three reasons address your audience's main concerns or values?
- Would adding a fourth reason strengthen or dilute your argument?
- Are your three reasons distinct and not just variations of the same point?
- How can you make your three reasons more memorable to your audience?
- What objections might be raised, and how do your three reasons address them?
- How can you present these three reasons in a compelling narrative?
- Which of these three reasons is most likely to resonate with your specific audience?
- How can you sequence your three reasons for maximum impact?
- What evidence or examples can support each of your three reasons?

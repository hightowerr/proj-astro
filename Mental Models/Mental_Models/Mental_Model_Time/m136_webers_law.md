**Category:** Time

Users adapt better to small incremental changes

**Description:**
Weber’s law says that people notice a change only when it’s a *proportion* of the starting level—a “just noticeable difference” (JND) scales with the baseline. Add a match to a dark room and it’s striking; add the same match to a bright room and no one notices. The same proportional rule shows up in vision (brightness/color), weight, sound, taste—and even how we perceive changes in charts and correlations. In scatterplots, for example, larger proportional shifts are needed to *see* a difference when the baseline correlation is lower; this insight lets us rank chart types by how easily they convey correlation. 

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

* When absolute thresholds/rules dominate (e.g., legal limits, hard specs) rather than perception. 
* In high-emotion/identity contexts where visuals or facts don’t persuade regardless of proportional differences. 
* When the “baseline” is unstable or unclear (new products, zero-to-one launches) making ratios meaningless. 
* If small, absolute changes carry outsized meaning (e.g., a €5 fee may matter even if “small” vs. a big purchase). Note: consumers notice deviations relative to context—€5 off wine feels bigger than €5 off a computer—so context framing can override simple ratio thinking. 

---

**Keywords for Situations:**
pricing changes; discounts & surcharges; UI/UX updates; product redesigns; sensory tweaks (brightness, volume, weight); data visualization (correlation, slope/line vs. scatter); marketing messages and in-store signage; spatial “indifference zones” for store choice. 

---

**Thinking Steps:**

1. **Define the baseline.** What is the current level users perceive (price, brightness, correlation strength, etc.)? JND depends on *this* level. 
2. **Estimate the needed ratio.** Plan changes as a *percentage* of baseline, not an absolute nudge (e.g., a larger % off for high-ticket items if you want it to be felt). 
3. **Choose the right representation.** If you need people to *see* correlation shifts, prefer chart types with higher perceptual sensitivity (e.g., scatterplots over certain lines/areas). 
4. **Bundle/sequence changes.** For disruptive updates, ship in small, incremental steps to keep each change under the JND—or, conversely, exceed the JND deliberately when you *want* attention. 
5. **Test perception, not just numbers.** A/B test whether people actually *notice* and correctly interpret the change (in store, on screen, or in charts). 
6. **Mind context effects.** What feels “big enough” varies by category, expectations, and surrounding cues (store environment, competing offers, layout). Adjust ratios accordingly. 

---

**Coaching Questions:**

* What’s the true baseline your audience perceives here? How stable is it? 
* Do you want this change to be *felt* or to be *frictionless*? What percentage would achieve that? 
* Is your visualization the most perceptually efficient for the pattern you’re highlighting? 
* Could framing (category, expectations, environment) make a small absolute change feel large—or vice versa? 
* Where might proportional thinking fail here because stakes, rules, or identities dominate perception?

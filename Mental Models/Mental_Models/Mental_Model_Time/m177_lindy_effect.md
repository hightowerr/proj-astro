### Description

The Lindy Effect is a heuristic for durability under uncertainty: for nonperishable things, time is a filter that destroys what is fragile. If an idea, tool, book, or practice has survived for a long time, its continued survival is more plausible than that of something newly introduced—because it has already endured a wide range of stressors.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When the object is perishable or designed to expire (biological, seasonal, rapidly obsoleting fads).
- When survival so far is protected by artificial support that could disappear suddenly.
- When novelty provides a genuine structural advantage and the downside is bounded.

Durability, time-tested, neomania, antifragility, subtraction, robustness, technological forecasting.

### Thinking Steps

1. **Check if it’s Lindy-eligible**
   Is the thing nonperishable (ideas, books, protocols, technologies) rather than inherently decaying?
2. **Measure “time survived”**
   How long has it persisted in real use (not just in theory)?
3. **Ask what stressors it already survived**
   What regimes, competitors, and failure conditions has it endured?
4. **Prefer the old when costs of failure are high**
   Use Lindy as a tie-breaker when fragility is expensive (security, safety, mission-critical choices).
5. **Combine with optionality**
   If you adopt the new, do so in a barbell way: small exposure, easy rollback, bounded downside.
6. **Watch for false Lindy**
   Identify cases where longevity is propped up by subsidies, lock-in, or monopoly rather than fitness.

---

### Coaching Questions

- Is this something that can “age” (nonperishable) or something that must decay?
- What would have killed it already if it were fragile?
- If this fails, what’s the downside—can you cap it?
- Where are you being pulled by neomania rather than evidence from time?

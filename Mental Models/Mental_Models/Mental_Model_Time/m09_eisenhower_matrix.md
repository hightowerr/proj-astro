# Eisenhower Matrix

## Description
The Eisenhower Matrix is a time management and priority-setting tool named after Dwight D. Eisenhower that categorizes tasks based on two criteria: urgency and importance. The matrix divides tasks into four quadrants: (1) Urgent and Important (Do First) - crises, deadlines, pressing problems that require immediate attention, (2) Important but Not Urgent (Schedule) - prevention, planning, relationship building, development that contributes to your long-term mission, (3) Urgent but Not Important (Delegate) - interruptions, some calls, some emails, routine meetings that demand immediate attention but don't contribute significantly to your goals, and (4) Neither Urgent nor Important (Eliminate) - time wasters, trivial activities, busy work that neither contribute to your goals nor require immediate attention. The matrix helps prioritize tasks based on these criteria rather than on how quickly they demand attention.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
This model should be used with caution when working in crisis situations where all tasks are urgent and important. Avoid relying on it exclusively if you're in a creative field where spontaneous opportunities might be valuable. Be cautious when using it if you're collaborating with others who don't share your definition of urgency or importance. It may be less effective during periods of major organizational change where priorities shift frequently. Also be careful when using it if you're in a role where you have little control over which tasks are delegated to you.

## Thinking Steps
1. List all the tasks and activities competing for your time
2. Categorize each task as either urgent or not urgent
3. Categorize each task as either important or not important
4. Place tasks into one of the four quadrants based on these criteria
5. Focus your attention on Quadrant 1 (Urgent and Important) tasks first
6. Schedule time for Quadrant 2 (Important but Not Urgent) tasks to prevent them from becoming urgent
7. Delegate Quadrant 3 (Urgent but Not Important) tasks when possible
8. Eliminate or minimize Quadrant 4 (Neither Urgent nor Important) tasks
9. Regularly review and update the matrix as priorities change
10. Strive to spend most of your time on Quadrant 2 activities

## Coaching Questions
- What tasks do you feel pressured to complete immediately, and are they truly important?
- Which important tasks keep getting postponed because they're not urgent?
- What activities can you delegate to others without sacrificing quality?
- What time-wasting activities consume your schedule unnecessarily?
- How much of your time is spent on urgent vs important activities?
- What important tasks could you schedule to prevent future crises?
- How do you distinguish between tasks that are urgent and tasks that are important?
- What tasks would you eliminate if you had to reduce your workload by 20%?
- How do you protect time for important but non-urgent activities?
- What quadrant do you spend most of your time in, and is that optimal?

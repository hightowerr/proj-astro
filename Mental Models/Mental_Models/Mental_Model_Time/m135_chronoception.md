**Category:** Time

People's perception of time is subjective

**Description:**
Chronoception refers to the *subjective perception of time*—how fast or slow time seems to pass depending on emotional states, attention, or context. It’s the mental model that explains why hours can feel like minutes during flow, or minutes can feel like hours when bored or anxious. Time perception is influenced by focus, novelty, and internal biological rhythms, not just clocks or calendars.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

* When making decisions that rely on *objective time* (e.g., deadlines, scheduling, safety-critical systems).
* When emotional intensity or stress might distort perception of duration (e.g., crisis management, user time-on-task studies).
* When comparing user experiences without normalizing for engagement or emotional state.

---

**Keywords for Situations:**

* Time dilation or contraction
* Flow state
* Boredom and anxiety
* Perceived waiting time
* UX session length perception
* Attention and engagement

---

**Thinking Steps:**

1. **Identify the context:** What’s causing users (or yourself) to feel time pass differently? Boredom, stress, or absorption?
2. **Assess task engagement:** Is the experience producing “flow” or frustration?
3. **Separate objective from subjective time:** What is the real vs. perceived duration?
4. **Design or act accordingly:**
   * To *speed up perceived time*, increase novelty, challenge, or feedback.
   * To *slow down perceived time*, add breaks, reduce cognitive load, or create calmer pacing.
5. **Validate with feedback or metrics:** Compare perceived vs. actual time to test interventions.

---

**Coaching Questions:**

* When was the last time time “flew by” for you—what conditions made that happen?
* How do emotions like stress or excitement change your sense of time passing?
* In your product or work, where might users feel time dragging or racing?
* How could you redesign the experience to make waiting feel shorter or flow feel longer?
* What assumptions are you making about how people *feel* time vs. how they *measure* it?

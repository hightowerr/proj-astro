**Category:** Time

When people's convictions are challenged, their beliefs get stronger

**Description: Core concept explanation**
The backfire effect is when attempts to correct someone’s mistaken belief make them double-down instead—especially when the belief is identity-linked or the correction feels threatening. Originally popularised by Nyhan & Reifler’s work, later evidence suggests strong backfire is **uncommon**: well-designed fact-checks and respectful framing usually improve accuracy rather than worsen it. Use the model to anticipate resistance and design *how* you correct, not to assume all debunking will backfire. 

* **Evidence note:** Overviews find that debunking generally increases factual accuracy; “backfire” tends to appear under specific conditions (high partisanship, identity threat, hostile tone). 
* **Origins & definition:** Described as people becoming *more* entrenched when confronted with disconfirming evidence. 

---

* **When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

  * When the belief is tightly bound to group identity or status—direct contradiction may harden views. 
  * When your tone or format implies blame, humiliation, or threat (e.g., “gotcha” fact-checks). 
  * When you’re simply repeating the myth without a clear alternative (myth-first headlines can reinforce recall). 
  * When you haven’t stress-tested whether your correction actually improves accuracy for your audience segment—assume heterogeneity. 

* **Keywords for Situations:**
  Identity-laden topics; political claims; brand/reputation crises; vaccine/health misinformation; organisational change comms; sensitive product deprecations; code review disagreements; high-stakes A/B test post-mortems. 

* **Thinking Steps (how to apply effectively):**

  1. **Map the belief & stakes:** What value/identity does the belief serve for this audience? (Don’t correct yet.) 
  2. **Choose a non-threatening frame:** Lead with shared goals/values; signal respect and autonomy. 
  3. **Offer an alternative mental model:** Present the core fact/explanation clearly; avoid myth-first, use “truth sandwich” (truth → myth → truth). 
  4. **Reduce friction:** Keep it short, specific, and concrete; visuals/examples beat abstractions. 
  5. **Prebunk where possible:** Forewarn about common misleads before they’re encountered. 
  6. **Test for heterogeneous effects:** Pilot messages with segments; measure accuracy shift, not just clicks. 
  7. **Follow-up & reinforce:** Repeat the correct model over time in trusted channels; invite questions. 

* **Coaching Questions:**

  * Which identity, value, or status might this belief protect for my audience? How can I acknowledge it up front? 
  * What’s my **positive** replacement explanation? Can I state it in one sentence without repeating the myth? 
  * How can I give people autonomy (choice, “you decide”) while presenting clear evidence? 
  * What tone or wording risks threat/embarrassment here—and how do I soften it? 
  * How will I measure belief change across segments to detect any pockets of backfire?

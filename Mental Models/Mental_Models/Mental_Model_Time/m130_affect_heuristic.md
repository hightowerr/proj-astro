**Category:** Time

Mental Model: Affect Heuristic

**Description:**

The Affect Heuristic describes how people rely on emotional impressions—rather than rational analysis—when making judgments or decisions. Instead of assessing risks and benefits independently, individuals base choices on how they feel about an object, person, or situation. A positive feeling lowers perceived risk and inflates perceived benefit; a negative feeling does the reverse. In short: we substitute “How do I feel about this?” for “What do I think about this?”

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When making high-stakes or complex decisions requiring objective trade-offs.
- When emotions are being intentionally manipulated (marketing, political framing, media bias).
- When mood or context (e.g., weather, fatigue, social setting) may distort risk–benefit assessment.
- When confirmation bias reinforces positive or negative affect without evidence.

**Keywords for Situations**:
- Decision-making under uncertainty
- Risk perception and consumer choice
- Emotional reasoning in policy, finance, or relationships
- Brand loyalty and trust formation
- Cognitive bias in forecasting or evaluation

**Thinking Steps:**
1. Pause Emotional Substitution — Recognise the impulse to decide by “feeling” instead of “thinking.”
2. Separate Affect from Evidence — Explicitly list risks and benefits as independent variables.
3. Quantify Objectively — Use data, probabilities, or expected value instead of gut feeling.
4. Check Environmental Influences — Note transient factors (mood, lighting, tone, fatigue).
5. Seek Counter-Affect — Deliberately consider arguments or evidence that provoke the opposite emotion.

**Coaching Questions:**
- What emotions arise when I think about this option?
- Would my judgment change if I were in a different mood or environment?
- Am I liking or disliking the idea, or the person/message delivering it?
- Have I clearly separated how I feel from what I know?
- What data or evidence contradicts my initial emotional response?

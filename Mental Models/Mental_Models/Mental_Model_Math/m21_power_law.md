# The Power Law

**Description:**
The Power Law is a mathematical relationship between two quantities where one quantity varies as a power of another. In practical terms, it describes situations where a small number of factors account for a large proportion of the effect (like the 80/20 rule). Power laws appear in many natural and social phenomena, indicating that there are few large things and many small things, with the relative frequency changing as a power of the relative size. This principle helps explain why most effects come from a few causes rather than being evenly distributed.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When expecting normal distributions in systems that actually follow power law distributions
- When underestimating the impact of rare, extreme events
- When applying linear thinking to non-linear systems governed by power laws
- When not preparing for the possibility of extreme outliers in decision making
- When using traditional statistical methods inappropriate for power-law distributed data

**Keywords for Situations:**
Risk management, wealth distribution, market crashes, social networks, network effects, scaling, extreme events, cognitive biases, probability, non-linear systems.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify Distribution Type:** Determine whether a phenomenon follows a power law or normal distribution.
2. **Prepare for Extremes:** Account for the possibility of rare, high-impact events.
3. **Look for Skewed Relationships:** Notice when small causes can have disproportionately large effects.
4. **Understand Scaling:** Recognize how systems behave differently at different scales.
5. **Plan for Tails:** Prepare for extreme events that occur more frequently than normal distributions would predict.
6. **Check for Connectivity:** Understand how network effects can create power law distributions.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "Is this situation following a power law or normal distribution pattern?"
* "Am I prepared for rare events that could have disproportionately large impacts?"
* "What small factors could potentially create large effects in this system?"
* "How might extreme scenarios affect my plans or models?"
* "Am I underestimating the impact of interconnectedness in this system?"
* "What would happen if this system scaled significantly larger or smaller?"

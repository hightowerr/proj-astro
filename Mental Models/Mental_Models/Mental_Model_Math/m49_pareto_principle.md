## Description: Core concept explanation
In 1906, the Italian polymath Vilfredo Pareto was researching wealth distribution in Italy and noticed that 20 percent of the population owned 80 percent of the land and wealth. He also observed the same distribution in the pea plants he grew, where 20 percent of plants produced 80 percent of the peas. In the 1940s, quality control consultant Joseph M. Juran noted that 80 percent of manufacturing defects were the result of 20 percent of production issues. Juran applied Pareto's name to the principle he defined as: in systems, 80 percent of outputs are typically the result of 20 percent of inputs. The other 20 percent of outputs are the result of the remaining 80 percent of inputs. We can apply the Pareto principle to numerous areas: 20 percent of researchers produce 80 percent of published research; 20 percent of words in a language are used 80 percent of the time; 20 percent of the population use 80 percent of healthcare resources; 20 percent of a company's customers create 80 percent of its profits.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail
- When assuming all items on a list are equally important: The principle challenges this assumption, but in some cases, a more balanced approach is needed.
- When the goal is to ensure equal access or fair distribution: The Pareto principle optimizes for efficiency, not equity.
- When all the time you put into a project won't be equally productive: Some situations require consistent effort across all areas.
- When not all employees will be responsible for the same amount of a company's profits: In some contexts, team collaboration makes individual contributions harder to separate.

## Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process
1. **Identify the inputs and outputs:** Determine what causes (inputs) are producing what results (outputs) in your system.
2. **Quantify the distribution:** Measure how much of the output is being generated by what percentage of the input.
3. **Locate the vital few:** Identify which specific 20% of inputs are generating 80% of the desired output.
4. **Analyze the relationship:** Understand why these particular inputs are so much more effective than others.
5. **Focus resources:** Direct more time, money, and effort toward the high-impact inputs and away from low-impact ones.
6. **Monitor changes:** Recognize that the specific 20% may shift over time and reassess periodically.

## Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts
- What 20% of our inputs are generating 80% of our desired outputs?
- Are we assuming all our inputs are equally important when they may not be?
- How can we increase the effectiveness of our most productive inputs?
- What inputs are we spending time on that have minimal impact?
- How might we apply more of our resources to the most effective inputs?
- In what areas of our life or business do we see the 80/20 rule playing out?
- How can we identify the vital few inputs in a new situation?

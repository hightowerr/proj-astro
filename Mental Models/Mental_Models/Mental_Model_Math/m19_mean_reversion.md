# Mean Reversion

**Description:**
Mean Reversion is a statistical concept stating that events that are not average will tend to be followed by events that are closer to the average. In other words, extreme outcomes tend to move back toward the mean over time. This principle applies in many fields including finance, sports, and performance evaluation. However, mean reversion only occurs in systems where there is a random component and is most pronounced at extremes. It's important to distinguish between random fluctuations and fundamental changes in underlying conditions.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When expecting short-term performance to reverse without understanding underlying causes
- When making investment decisions based solely on performance reversion without fundamental analysis
- When assuming that all extreme situations will revert to normal without considering structural changes
- When using mean reversion as the only investment strategy without considering other factors
- When failing to distinguish between random fluctuations and fundamental changes

**Keywords for Situations:**
Investing, performance evaluation, statistical analysis, forecasting, finance, sports, economics, cognitive biases, regression to the mean, probability.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify Base Rate:** Determine the long-term average or mean for the variable in question.
2. **Assess Randomness:** Evaluate whether the system has random components that would drive mean reversion.
3. **Distinguish Permanent Changes:** Determine if recent extreme values reflect temporary fluctuations or fundamental shifts.
4. **Consider Time Frame:** Recognize that mean reversion may take a long time to manifest.
5. **Look for Structural Factors:** Identify if there are structural barriers to reversion.
6. **Combine with Analysis:** Use mean reversion insights alongside other analytical methods.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "What is the long-term average for this metric and how far out of line is the current value?"
* "Is this extreme value due to temporary factors or permanent structural changes?"
* "What time frame should I expect for reversion to occur?"
* "Are there structural reasons why mean reversion might not occur?"
* "How can I combine mean reversion insights with other analytical methods?"
* "Am I over-relying on mean reversion without considering other factors?"

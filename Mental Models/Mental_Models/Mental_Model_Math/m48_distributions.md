## Description: Core concept explanation
Distributions are different ways a group of something—like people's heights, grades on a test, or flavors of ice cream sold—spreads out or bunches up. They tell you how often each item shows up and help contextualize what to expect given a certain data set. The normal distribution is most familiar, where the majority of values cluster around a midpoint, with fewer values the farther from the midpoint. When plotted, normally distributed data forms a symmetrical bell curve. Many common measurements like height, IQ, blood pressure, and exam results are normally distributed. However, many things follow power law distributions where values cluster at low or high points. Wealth follows a typical power law distribution, with exponentially more people having $1,000 in assets than $1 billion. Normal distributions usually characterize the price of common household goods.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail
- When you assume the world fits a normal distribution: Reality rarely fits into a neat normal distribution, causing you to miss important nuance and variation.
- When dealing with power law distributions but using normal distribution thinking: You'll underestimate the probability and impact of extreme events.
- When working with small sample sizes: Distribution patterns may not yet be apparent in limited data.
- When data quality is poor and doesn't represent the actual distribution: Raw data is almost never as well behaved as we would like.

## Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process
1. **Identify the type of distribution:** Is the data normally distributed (clustering around a midpoint) or following a power law (clustering at low or high points)?
2. **Understand the characteristics:** Determine if the data is discrete or continuous, symmetric or asymmetric, with upper and lower limits, and the likelihood of extreme values.
3. **Recognize the implications:** Understand that normal distributions make extreme values unlikely, while power law distributions allow for more extreme events.
4. **Consider the context:** Think about whether the distribution is appropriate for your data set and what it means for future predictions.
5. **Look for long-tail values:** Be aware that even unlikely events in normal distributions are not impossible and can have outsize impact.
6. **Test against reality:** Remember that you never really know if you have the right distribution for your data, as future data points may change it.

## Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts
- Are we assuming the data follows a normal distribution when it might follow a power law distribution?
- How might extreme events (long-tail values) impact our planning and decision-making?
- What happens if we're dealing with a power law distribution but using normal distribution thinking?
- Are we prepared for the possibility that rare events might have a significant impact?
- How can we tell if our current distribution model is still appropriate for the data?
- What would be the impact of a significant deviation from our expected distribution?
- How might we be underestimating or overestimating the probability of extreme events?

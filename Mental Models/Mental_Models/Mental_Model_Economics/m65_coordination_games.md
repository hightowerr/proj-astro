# m65_coordination_games.md

## Description
Coordination games are situations where players get the best possible payoff by all doing the same thing. In these games, if everyone chooses the same strategy, everyone benefits. If one player chooses a different strategy, they get a diminished payoff while others may benefit or suffer depending on the specific setup. Coordination games occur when outcomes depend on players' expectations of others' behavior. In real-world terms, this applies to many social and economic situations where the optimal action depends on others' choices. The key challenge is when there are multiple possible equilibria, some better than others, but all requiring unanimous agreement.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When individual interests are better served by non-coordination
- In competitive situations where you benefit from others' coordination
- When diversity of approaches is more valuable than uniformity
- When the coordination goal is suboptimal or harmful
- When coordination creates vulnerability to external shocks
- When the coordination mechanism is fragile or unreliable
- When local optimization is more important than global coordination

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify if success requires multiple parties to act in synchronization
2. Assess whether there are multiple possible coordinated outcomes
3. Determine which coordinated outcome would be best overall
4. Evaluate the incentives for each party to coordinate toward the best outcome
5. Identify barriers that prevent coordination toward optimal outcomes
6. Consider mechanisms to align incentives for better coordination
7. Assess the costs of non-coordination versus coordination
8. Evaluate what it would take to shift from suboptimal to optimal coordination
9. Consider transition paths from current coordination to better coordination
10. Plan for maintaining coordination once achieved

## Coaching Questions
- Do we all need to do the same thing to achieve the best outcome?
- Are there multiple ways we could coordinate our actions?
- Which coordinated outcome would be best for everyone involved?
- What prevents us from coordinating for the better outcome?
- How do our expectations of others' actions affect our own choices?
- What mechanisms could help us coordinate better?
- What would happen if only some people coordinated while others didn't?
- How can we shift from suboptimal to optimal coordination?
- Are there early adopters who could initiate a shift toward better coordination?

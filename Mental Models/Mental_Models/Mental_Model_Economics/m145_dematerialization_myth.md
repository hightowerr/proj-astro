## Mental Model = Dematerialization Myth

**Category = Economics / Systems Thinking**
**Description:**
The dematerialization myth is the belief that shifting toward services/information and improving efficiency will naturally reduce total resource use. In practice, falling resource intensity can coincide with rising total consumption because growth and rebound expand the scale of activity.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When totals are capped (emissions caps, strict resource budgets).
- When you have strong evidence of absolute decoupling in the relevant boundary (rare; verify).

**Keywords for Situations:**
Green growth; decoupling; digitalization; “paperless office”; energy intensity; sustainability policy.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. Measure totals, not just intensity (absolute energy/material throughput).
2. Identify expansion mechanisms enabled by “dematerialization” (more output, more demand).
3. Account for indirect infrastructure and supply-chain requirements.
4. Test whether absolute totals fall under realistic growth assumptions.
5. If the goal is absolute reduction, add binding constraints (caps/prices/budgets).

**Coaching Questions:**
- Are we seeing lower intensity or lower total consumption?
- What physical infrastructure supports the “immaterial” service?
- If the service becomes cheaper/easier, what expands?

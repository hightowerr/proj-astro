### Description

Optionality is the ability to take advantage of favorable outcomes without being forced to bear symmetric costs when outcomes are unfavorable. In the pure form, it is “the right but not the obligation.” Optionality is powerful because it reduces the need to forecast: instead of being correct, you structure exposure so that being wrong is survivable and being right is meaningful.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When “optionality” is just a story and the downside is actually unbounded or hidden.
- When you pay so much for optionality that the net exposure becomes negative.
- When you use optionality as an excuse to never commit, even after uncertainty is resolved.

---

### Keywords for Situations

Asymmetry, rights vs obligations, contracts, exit clauses, small bets, trial and error, convexity, “free options.”

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Write the payoff in plain language**
   What do you gain if things go well, and what do you lose if they go poorly?
2. **Bound the downside**
   Make the maximum loss explicit (time, money, reputation). If it can spiral, it isn’t optionality.
3. **Delay commitment**
   Replace early irreversible decisions with staged decisions: keep the ability to walk away.
4. **Increase the number of small trials**
   Prefer many small, bounded experiments over one big forecast-driven bet.
5. **Avoid negative optionality**
   Watch for positions where you have obligations but limited upside (often disguised as “stable”).
6. **Convert knowledge into better option selection**
   Use what you learn to prune bad options and double down only where upside is real.
7. **Audit who holds the option**
   Ensure you are not providing a “free option” to someone else at your expense (transfer of fragility).

---

### Coaching Questions

- What part of this decision is an obligation, and what part is a right?
- What is the maximum loss, and can it compound?
- How can you make this reversible or staged?
- Are you buying optionality—or selling it to someone else for free?

## Mental Model = Backfire (Rebound > 100%)

**Category = Economics / Systems Thinking**
**Description:**
Backfire is the extreme case of rebound where efficiency improvements lead to *more than full take-back*: total resource/energy consumption increases above the pre-efficiency baseline. It is the “worst case” of Jevons-style dynamics: per-unit savings enable scale expansion large enough to increase aggregate use.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When demand is capped, rationed, or strictly priced such that total use can’t rise.
- When the relevant service is already saturated (no meaningful expansion path).
- When you only have device-level data and no system-level baseline.

**Keywords for Situations:**
Energy efficiency standards; cheaper mobility; industrial productivity; induced demand; “efficiency makes it worse”.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. Define baseline total consumption for the relevant boundary (sector/region).
2. Identify the effective price drop in the service/output due to efficiency.
3. Estimate demand expansion (usage quantity, adoption, new applications).
4. Include indirect/economy-wide pathways (re-spending, growth, trade).
5. Compare post-change total consumption to baseline to test “> 100%”.

**Coaching Questions:**
- What’s the system boundary where “total consumption” should be evaluated?
- What are the realistic expansion paths enabled by this efficiency gain?
- Which elasticities (price, substitution) matter most here?
- What policy or physical constraints could prevent backfire?

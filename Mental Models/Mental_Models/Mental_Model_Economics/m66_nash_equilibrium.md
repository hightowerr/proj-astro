# m66_nash_equilibrium.md

## Description
Nash equilibrium occurs when each player in a game is making the best possible decision for themselves given the decisions made by all other players. In this state, no individual player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy while other players keep their strategies unchanged. This creates a stable state where everyone is doing the best they can given others' choices. Named after mathematician John Nash, the concept is widely applicable beyond game theory to economics, political science, and social phenomena where multiple parties make interdependent decisions.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When the current equilibrium is suboptimal or harmful to collective welfare
- In situations where individual rationality leads to collective irrationality
- When the equilibrium is based on faulty assumptions or incomplete information
- When the system needs to change or adapt to new conditions
- When the equilibrium is maintained through coercion or manipulation
- When the game has multiple equilibria and you want to shift to a better one
- When the Nash equilibrium represents a local but not global optimum

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify all the players in the decision-making situation
2. Determine each player's possible strategies and payoffs
3. Assess how each player's outcomes depend on others' choices
4. Identify if everyone is currently making their optimal choice given others' strategies
5. Evaluate whether any player has an incentive to unilaterally change their behavior
6. Consider if the current equilibrium is optimal for all or just individually rational
7. Analyze what would need to change for players to adopt different strategies
8. Assess the stability and fragility of the current equilibrium
9. Evaluate potential for multiple equilibria and which might be better

## Coaching Questions
- What decision is each player making given everyone else's current choices?
- Could anyone improve their outcome by changing their strategy alone?
- Is this the best possible collective outcome or just individually rational?
- What would need to happen to shift to a different equilibrium?
- How stable is the current arrangement to small changes?
- Are there incentives that could encourage different behavior?
- What would be the consequences if one player changed their approach?
- How did we reach this equilibrium and is it the only possible one?
- Are there external factors that could disrupt this equilibrium?

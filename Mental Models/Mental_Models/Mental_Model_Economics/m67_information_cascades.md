# m67_information_cascades.md

## Description
Information cascades occur when people make decisions based on the actions of others rather than their own information, knowledge, or signals. When individuals observe others making particular choices, they infer that those people have valuable information and follow suit, even if their own private information suggests a different course of action. As more people follow the same path, subsequent individuals have increasingly strong reasons to follow, regardless of their own private information. This process can lead to a cascade where large groups of people all make the same decision based on limited initial information.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When your private information is more accurate than the public signals
- In situations requiring independent thinking and analysis
- When the initial decision-makers may have had faulty information
- When the stakes are high and independent analysis is crucial
- When diversity of opinion is essential for optimal outcomes
- When early decisions were made under different conditions
- When you have specialized knowledge that others lack
- When the crowd effect could amplify error rather than truth

## Keywords for Situations
- Investment and financial market trends
- Social media trends and viral content
- Voting behavior
- Restaurant choice and popularity
- Technology adoption (network effects)
- Academic research and citation patterns
- Corporate decision-making
- Consumer purchasing behavior
- Job selection and career choices
- Educational institution selection
- Medical diagnosis patterns
- Crisis response and panic buying

## Thinking Steps
1. Assess the quality of your private information versus public signals
2. Determine whether others' choices reflect superior information or just following others
3. Evaluate the independence and quality of early decision makers in the cascade
4. Identify what private information you possess that others might not
5. Consider whether following the crowd aligns with your objectives
6. Analyze the conditions under which the cascade started
7. Assess whether the underlying situation has changed since early decisions
8. Evaluate the potential consequences of following versus bucking the trend
9. Consider whether you're part of creating a cascade that could mislead others

## Coaching Questions
- What private information do I have that differs from public signals?
- How did the initial decision-makers in this trend gain their information?
- What if my private information is better than the public's assumption?
- How has the underlying reality changed since this trend began?
- What would happen if I made a decision based on my private information?
- Am I following others because they have better information or just because they're following others?
- What are the potential consequences of bucking this trend?
- How might my decision influence others in this cascade?
- Is this information cascade based on actual information or just conformity?

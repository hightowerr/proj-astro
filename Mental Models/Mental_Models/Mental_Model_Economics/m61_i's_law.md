# m61_i's_law.md

## Description
I's Law is the inverse of Gresham's Law. In the absence of legal tender laws, good money drives out the bad. This occurs when people prefer to receive payments in the form of an older and therefore seemingly more reliable currency from abroad. Even if this practice is illegal, if legal tender laws are sufficiently difficult to enforce, the imported currency predominates. This principle highlights how quality and reliability naturally win out when people have genuine choice in what they accept as value.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When legal tender laws are strictly enforced and violations carry severe penalties
- In highly regulated financial systems where currency substitution is prohibited
- When transaction costs of using alternative currencies outweigh benefits
- In situations where the "good money" is not readily accessible or available
- When enforcement mechanisms make currency substitution impractical
- When the "good money" has high volatility or other risks

## Keywords for Situations
- Currency substitution in unstable economies

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify if there are multiple forms of "money" or "value" available to choose from
2. Assess which options represent "good money" (higher quality, reliability, or intrinsic value)
3. Determine if there are legal or practical restrictions on choice
4. Evaluate the enforcement mechanisms for any restrictions
5. Consider the costs and benefits of choosing higher-quality options
6. Assess whether people will naturally gravitate toward better options when they can
7. Determine the long-term sustainability of the "good money" preference
8. Consider what happens when restrictions are lifted or enforcement weakens

## Coaching Questions
- What are the different forms of "value" available in this situation?
- Which options represent higher quality or reliability ("good money")?
- Are there legal or practical barriers preventing people from choosing quality?
- How effective are the enforcement mechanisms for these barriers?
- What would happen if people could freely choose the best option?
- Are there hidden costs to using the higher-quality alternative?
- What prevents the "good money" from naturally prevailing in this situation?
- How might market conditions change to allow quality to win out?
- Would people prefer the higher-quality option if they could access it freely?

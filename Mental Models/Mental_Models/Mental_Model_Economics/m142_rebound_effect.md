## Mental Model = Rebound Effect

**Category = Economics / Systems Thinking**
**Description:**
The rebound effect is the portion of expected resource/energy savings from efficiency that is “taken back” by increased consumption. It includes direct rebound (more use of the same service), indirect rebound (re-spending savings on other goods/services that require resources), and economy-wide rebound (system-level expansion, price effects, and structural change).

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When you can’t define the counterfactual baseline clearly.
- When you’re measuring a narrow direct effect but making economy-wide claims.
- When binding caps mean increased demand cannot translate into increased total use.

**Keywords for Situations:**
Energy efficiency; appliance standards; fuel economy; induced demand; conservation programs; “paperless office”.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. Define the “service” (lighting, mobility, heating) and the efficiency change.
2. Specify the baseline: what savings were “theoretically possible” and over what boundary.
3. Estimate direct rebound: how usage quantity changes with lower operating cost.
4. Estimate indirect rebound: where do monetary/time savings go?
5. Consider economy-wide effects: production expansion, input-price effects, trade.
6. Report rebound by type (direct/indirect/total) and boundary assumptions.

**Coaching Questions:**
- “Rebound of what, exactly, is a percentage?”
- What boundary are we analyzing: device, household, sector, or whole economy?
- Where do saved money/time get reallocated?
- What happens to prices and production when efficiency rises at scale?

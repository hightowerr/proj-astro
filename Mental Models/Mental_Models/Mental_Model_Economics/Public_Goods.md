# Mental Model: Public Goods

## Description
Public goods are economic goods that are both non-excludable and non-rivalrous in consumption. Non-excludable means that it's impossible or very costly to prevent anyone from using the good, regardless of whether they've paid for it. Non-rivalrous means that one person's use of the good doesn't reduce its availability to others. Classic examples include national defense, public radio, street lighting, and clean air. The challenge with public goods is that people have incentives to free-ride (benefit from the good without paying), which can lead to under-provision of these goods by the market.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Use with caution when:
- The good is actually excludable or rivalrous in nature
- Private markets can effectively provide the good without free-rider issues
- Government provision might lead to inefficiencies or reduced innovation
- The classification as a public good is being used to justify unnecessary government intervention
- The good has characteristics that can be modified to make it excludable or rivalrous
- The costs of collective provision outweigh the benefits

## Keywords for Situations
- When dealing with infrastructure projects
- Analyzing environmental issues or natural resource management
- Evaluating government vs. private provision of services
- Understanding market failures and economic policy
- Addressing collective action problems
- When facing free-rider problems in organizations or communities
- Analyzing digital goods and information products
- Considering public health and safety initiatives

## Thinking Steps
1. Determine if the good in question is truly non-excludable and non-rivalrous
2. Identify the extent of free-rider potential in the current provision model
3. Consider whether the good might transition from public to private based on technology changes
4. Analyze potential mechanisms to address free-rider issues (taxation, subsidies, regulations)
5. Evaluate the efficiency of collective vs. individual provision
6. Assess potential governance solutions to manage the public good
7. Consider possible private alternatives or public-private partnerships
8. Examine how the good is funded and whether the funding mechanism is sustainable

## Coaching Questions
- Is this good truly non-excludable and non-rivalrous, or do the characteristics change under certain conditions?
- What are the potential free-rider issues in the current arrangement?
- How might we address the free-rider problem while preserving the public benefit?
- Are there ways to make this good partially excludable to improve provision?
- What would happen if private markets tried to provide this good alone?
- How should we fund the provision of this good to ensure sustainability?
- What governance structures would work best to manage this public good?
- Are there examples of similar goods being provided effectively in other contexts?

**Description**: Core concept explanation
Asymmetric Information describes a market situation where one party in a transaction possesses superior or private information compared to the other party, who has incomplete knowledge about the situation or the "type" of person they are dealing with.[1, 1] This imbalance has profound implications for strategic interaction and can lead to market failure, characterized by inefficient pricing, unfair economic outcomes, and even fraudulent behavior. The model manifests in two primary forms: Adverse Selection (when private information exists before the transaction, leading to selection of undesirable participants, e.g., high-risk borrowers seeking loans) and Moral Hazard (when private information allows one party to change their behavior and take undue risks after a deal is struck, e.g., excessive risk-taking by insured individuals or managers.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):** Situations where the model may mislead or fail
 * Over-Predicting Market Collapse: While the model predicts market failure (e.g., the used car market collapsing due to "lemons"), real-world markets often deploy mitigating factors (such as reputation, screening, or warranties) that prevent total failure, meaning the theoretical failure is minuscule in practice.
 * Assuming Static Information: The degree of information asymmetry is dynamic; for example, a vehicle owner gains an increasing informational advantage about their car as it ages, changing the balance of power over time.
 * Ignoring Policy Solutions: Government intervention, such as mandatory disclosure rules enforced by regulatory bodies (e.g., the SEC), can reduce information asymmetry and mitigate market failure.
 * Impracticality of Perfect Knowledge: The model highlights the inefficiency of asymmetry, but the theoretical solution—for all agents to become experts in all fields—is impractical due to high opportunity costs and resulting lower overall aggregate output.

**Keywords for Situations**: Common contexts or triggers where it applies
	Asymmetric Information, Superior Information, Incomplete Information, Adverse Selection, Moral Hazard, Signaling, Screening, Lemon Problem, Warranties, Hidden Flaws, Insurance Markets, Labor Markets, Credit Markets.

**Thinking Steps**: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively
 1. Identify the Information Disparity: Define precisely which party (the agent, seller, driver, or firm) possesses superior or private information that the other party lacks.[1, 1]
 2. Assess the Timing: Determine if the information asymmetry exists before a deal is finalized (Adverse Selection) or if it involves a change in behavior/effort after the agreement (Moral Hazard).
 3. Analyze the Payoff/Risk Imbalance: Quantify how the information advantage shifts risk or allows the informed party to capture a greater, perhaps unfair, share of the value (e.g., preventing a car manufacturer from being able to charge a high enough price for a reliable, improved car.
 4. Formulate Mitigation Strategy: If uninformed, deploy Screening mechanisms (e.g., aptitude tests, medical exams) to reveal the other party's hidden information. If informed, use Signaling (e.g., high credentials, brand identity, warranties) to credibly communicate genuine high quality.

**Coaching Questions:** Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model
 * If I am the uninformed party, what screening mechanisms (e.g., additional testing, requests for historical records, credit checks) can I reasonably impose to gain insight into the other party's true risk or quality?.
 * If I am the informed party (e.g., a good firm seeking investment), what signals can I deploy (e.g., specialized debt financing, strong collateral, high-quality professional degrees) to convince the market of my quality and avoid being treated like an average 'lemon'?.
 * Am I currently avoiding giving information that is known to be available? If so, is the other party defaulting to the "no news is bad news" principle and assuming the worst about my position?.
 * How does this specific strategic interaction change if the initial distribution of property rights (or liability) is suddenly clarified or made ambiguous?.

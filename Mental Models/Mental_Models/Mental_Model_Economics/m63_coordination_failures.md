# m63_coordination_failures.md

## Description
Coordination failures occur in game theory when players get the best possible payoff by all doing the same thing. If one player chooses a different strategy, they get a diminished payoff while the other player usually gets an increased payoff. A coordination game is one where all players are carrying out a strategy from which they have no individual incentive to deviate (Nash equilibrium). However, a game can have multiple Nash equilibria with different payoffs. In real-world terms, this means there are multiple different choices everyone could make, some better than others, but all only working if they are unanimous. If everyone is already making the same suboptimal choice, it can be difficult to change anything.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When individual incentives are better served by non-coordination
- In competitive situations where you benefit from others' coordination
- When the costs of coordination exceed the benefits
- When there's no clear optimal coordinated outcome
- When diversity of approaches is more valuable than uniformity
- In rapidly changing environments where coordination becomes a liability

## Keywords for Situations
- Traffic patterns and driving rules
- Language standardization
- Technology adoption (QWERTY keyboards, VHS vs. Betamax)
- Market conventions
- Social norms and behaviors
- International cooperation
- Team collaboration
- Currency adoption
- Infrastructure standards
- Organizational processes
- Emergency response coordination
- Economic policy alignment

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify if success requires multiple parties to act in sync
2. Assess whether there are multiple possible coordinated outcomes
3. Determine which coordinated outcome would be best overall
4. Evaluate the incentives for each party to coordinate
5. Identify barriers that prevent coordination
6. Consider how to align incentives for better coordination
7. Assess the costs of non-coordination
8. Evaluate mechanisms to facilitate coordination
9. Determine if the current coordination is optimal or suboptimal
10. Consider transition paths from suboptimal to optimal coordination

## Coaching Questions
- What would happen if everyone coordinated their actions differently?
- Are there alternative coordinated approaches that might be better?
- What prevents people from coordinating for the better outcome?
- How would incentives need to change to encourage better coordination?
- What are the costs of everyone continuing the current suboptimal approach?
- Are there early adopters who could start a shift toward better coordination?
- What would it take to get everyone to switch to the better coordinated approach?
- How can we lower the barriers to coordination?
- What happens if only some people coordinate while others don't?

**Category:** Economics  

**Description: Core concept explanation**  
First-principles valuation is the practice of decomposing a company into its most fundamental, causal units of value creation — the physics, contracts, and incentives that determine revenue, cost, and capital flows.  
Instead of relying on peer multiples or management guidance, you rebuild the business from the ground up: what must be true about inputs, processes, and outputs for current and future cash flows to exist?  
This method replaces spreadsheet heuristics with physically- and economically-anchored reasoning.

---

### 🧱 When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- **Time-critical decisions:** During earnings calls or live trading where heuristics beat slow derivation.  
- **Purely narrative sectors:** Early-stage biotech, crypto, or speculative IP with unobservable fundamentals.  
- **Heavily regulated or capped markets:** Where price and volume are externally fixed — optimisation, not derivation, is the task.  
- **Mature coverage maintenance:** When updating an existing model quarterly, use deltas not full rebuilds.  
- **Data poverty:** If unit data (capacity, conversion rates, cost curves) are missing, you’ll build elegant fiction.

### 🧩 Thinking Steps: Applying First Principles to Valuation  

1. **Define the Economic Engine (What?)**  
   - Ask: *What problem does this firm solve, for whom, and how does it monetise the solution?*  
   - Express value creation in measurable units (e.g., compute tokens, seat-months, tonne-km).  
   - **Checkpoint:** Can you state the company’s core “energy source” (data, attention, electrons, molecules)?  

2. **Map the Value Chain (How?)**  
   - Trace **inputs → activities → outputs → customers**.  
   - Link each stage to cost or revenue drivers and to constraints (supply, regulation, demand elasticity).  
   - Example: *Nvidia → wafers → GPUs → model training hours → enterprise inference revenue.*  
   - **Checkpoint:** Do you have quantified levers for each arrow (price, volume, efficiency)?  

3. **Decompose to Fundamentals (Why?)**  
   - Break metrics into invariant truths:  
     - Physics (kWh per compute hour, tonnes per shipment).  
     - Economics (marginal cost curves, network effects).  
     - Incentives (contract terms, switching costs).  
   - Quantify relationships using observable data, not accounting aggregates.  
   - **Checkpoint:** Each “truth” must be independently verifiable and causally linked to financial outcomes.  

4. **Reconstruct Financial Dynamics (Solution)**  
   - Build revenue and margin forecasts from the bottom up:  
     - `Revenue = unit_price × volume` (bounded by capacity & demand).  
     - `Cost = resource_input × unit_cost` (bounded by technology curve).  
   - Integrate these causal drivers into DCF or Monte Carlo simulations.  
   - **Checkpoint:** Each cell in your model traces back to a physical or contractual constraint.  

5. **Stress-Test & Invert (Devil’s Advocate)**  
   - Flip assumptions: What if input costs double, demand saturates, or incentives misalign?  
   - Run *premortems* to expose second-order effects (policy, substitution, supply shocks).  
   - **Checkpoint:** Document “what would have to be true” for bull and bear scenarios; link to sensitivity table.  

6. **Optimise & Benchmark**  
   - Compare derived unit metrics (e.g., £ per user per month, $ per kWh of compute) across peers.  
   - Validate outputs against industry cost curves or Damodaran/IBES aggregates.  
   - If your unit economics violate known limits, revisit step 3 — the error is structural, not cosmetic.

### 💬 Coaching Questions for Equity Analysts  
- What is the **fundamental bottleneck** that limits this firm’s growth?  
- Which **inputs** dominate variable cost, and are they subject to physics or contract?  
- How does **energy, data, or time** flow through the business model?  
- Which driver (price, volume, efficiency) contributes most to incremental value?  
- What invariant truths about demand, cost, or capital intensity would **falsify** my base case?  
- How can I express this thesis in one **quantifiable statement** (e.g., “Every +10 bps in yield cuts 3 % of FCF”)?  

---

### 🧭 Summary  
First-principles valuation transforms equity research from *pattern recognition* to *causal reasoning*.  
It grounds conviction in verifiable constraints — energy use, throughput, contract design — rather than historical multiples.  
Used well, it reveals where value is physically or economically created, transferred, or destroyed.  
> “Don’t model the spreadsheet — model the reality the spreadsheet tries to describe.”

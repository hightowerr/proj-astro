### Description

Goodhart's Law states that "any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed upon it for control purposes." This principle, named after economist Charles Goodhart, explains how relationships that appear stable in historical data break down when they are used as targets for policy control. The law is closely related to the Lucas Critique, which argues that economic relationships estimated from historical data will change when policy regimes change, because people's behavior adapts to the new environment. When policymakers target specific indicators or use statistical relationships for control purposes, individuals and institutions change their behavior in response to the policy pressure, which undermines the original relationship that made the indicator useful in the first place.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- Avoid using Goodhart's Law when analyzing relationships in controlled experimental settings where behavioral adaptation is not a factor.
- Don't apply it to fundamental physical laws or constants that don't depend on human behavior.
- Be cautious when using it to dismiss all statistical relationships as inherently unreliable - some relationships may be more robust than others.
- This law is most applicable to social and economic systems where human behavior can adapt to measurement and control attempts.

### Thinking Steps

1. **Identify the statistical relationship**: What correlation or regularity has been observed in the data?
2. **Assess the proposed use**: Is this relationship being used for policy control or as a target?
3. **Consider behavioral responses**: How might people change their behavior when they know they're being measured?
4. **Evaluate robustness**: What would happen to the relationship if pressure were applied to it?
5. **Look for alternatives**: Are there more fundamental indicators that might be less susceptible to gaming?
6. **Design safeguards**: How can the measurement system be designed to minimize gaming incentives?
7. **Monitor for breakdown**: What early warning signs would indicate the relationship is breaking down?
8. **Plan for adaptation**: How will you respond if the relationship does collapse under pressure?

### Coaching Questions

- What statistical relationship are you planning to use for control purposes?
- How might people change their behavior when they know they're being measured on this metric?
- What happened to similar relationships when they were previously used for policy control?
- Could focusing on this indicator cause people to neglect other important aspects of the system?
- What would be the early warning signs that this relationship is breaking down?
- Are there multiple indicators you could use instead of relying on just one?
- How could you design the measurement system to reduce gaming incentives?
- What is the fundamental objective you're trying to achieve, versus the proxy measure you're using?
- Have you observed this relationship breaking down in other contexts when pressure was applied?
- What would happen if everyone knew exactly how this indicator was being used for policy?

**Category**: Economics

**Description: Core concept explanation**  
Cost of capital is the minimum return a company must earn to compensate providers of capital—both lenders and shareholders—for the risk they bear. It combines the after-tax cost of debt and the cost of equity into the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). WACC acts as the hurdle rate for investment decisions: projects that exceed it create value; those that fall short destroy value. The model guides capital allocation, valuation, and performance assessment by ensuring returns exceed the economic cost of funding.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail**  
- Projects with different risk profiles than the firm’s core business  
- Situations involving major leverage changes or non-standard financing  
- Reliance on CAPM assumptions that do not hold in practice  
- Assuming debt beta is zero while using a non–risk-free cost of debt  
- Conglomerates using one WACC across unrelated divisions  
- Long-duration projects where future market conditions diverge from current inputs  
- Environments with volatile or uncertain tax rates  
- Private-company valuations where capital structure and market values are hard to estimate

**Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively**

1. **Assess Project Risk Alignment**  
   Determine whether the project matches the firm’s existing business risk. If not, plan to adjust the discount rate accordingly.

2. **Calculate Cost of Debt**  
   Use the marginal borrowing rate based on current yields or recent borrowing. Apply the tax shield using a realistic, stable tax rate.

3. **Estimate Cost of Equity**  
   Apply CAPM:  
   *Cost of Equity = Risk-free rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium.*  
   Use industry comparables for beta if the firm’s own beta is distorted.

4. **Determine Capital Structure Weights**  
   Use market values of debt and equity to compute weights. For private firms, use comparable company structures.

5. **Compute WACC**  
   Combine components using:  
   *WACC = E/(D+E) × Cost of Equity + D/(D+E) × After-tax Cost of Debt.*  
   Verify consistency in all risk assumptions, especially around debt beta.

6. **Apply Decision Rules**  
   Compare IRR vs. WACC or compute NPV using WACC. Accept only positive-NPV or IRR > WACC projects.

7. **Perform Sensitivity Analysis**  
   Test outcomes under changes in key inputs: beta, capital structure, tax rates, and risk-free rates.

8. **Risk-Adjust When Necessary**  
   Use divisional WACCs, industry betas, or add risk premiums for projects with nonstandard risk characteristics.

**Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model**

- Does this project carry risk that differs from our core operations?  
- Which inputs in our WACC calculation are most uncertain or assumption-heavy?  
- Will this investment materially change our leverage or financing mix?  
- Are we applying one WACC across many future years for a project likely to face changing market conditions?  
- Would alternative valuation approaches (e.g., scenario analysis, real options) give a clearer view of risk?  
- What is the spread between expected ROIC and WACC, and is it large enough to absorb uncertainty?  
- Are we unintentionally selecting a discount rate to support a preferred conclusion?  
- Are our debt and equity assumptions internally consistent across divisions or project types?

## Description: Core concept explanation
Market power is a firm's ability to profitably set prices above marginal cost, restrict output, or influence market conditions without losing customers. In competitive markets, prices track marginal cost; in concentrated or differentiated markets, firms can sustain markups due to entry barriers, switching frictions, information advantages, or regulation.

Two empirical measurement systems dominate:

**Lerner Index:**  
\(L = (p - MC) / p\), capturing the price–cost margin. It requires marginal-cost estimation and assumes efficiency unless corrected with efficiency-adjusted variants. Production-function-based markups (output elasticity ÷ input revenue share) offer an alternative when cost data are scarce.

**Panzar–Rosse H-Statistic:**  
Based on revenue elasticities with respect to input prices. Interpretation hinges on equilibrium assumptions, unscaled specifications, and dynamic panel estimation. Static, scaled models systematically bias competition estimates.

Market power also reflects structural features—barriers to entry, switching costs, differentiation, network effects, regulation—and dynamic forces such as potential entry, supply-side substitution, buyer concentration, and information asymmetry. Behavioural evidence (persistent margins, discrimination, rigidity, strategic pricing) complements structural measures.

Welfare depends on whether rents reflect efficiency or extraction, with implications for consumer surplus, labour share, innovation, and financial stability.

---

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- Mis-specified Panzar–Rosse models (scaled revenue, static panels, disequilibrium markets).  
- Lerner Index use when marginal cost is poorly estimated or firms exhibit inefficiency (“quiet life”).  
- Comparing margins across markets with different product mixes or cost structures.  
- Inferring power directly from concentration; HHI is outcome-driven and often weakly correlated with markups.  
- Ignoring regulatory context (entry, capital rules, licensing).  
- Treating temporary price rigidity as evidence of market power; power requires persistent price–cost gaps.

## Thinking Steps
1. **Define the market**  
   Clarify product boundaries, geographic scope, and substitutability; consider adjacent categories and potential entrants.

2. **Assess structural drivers**  
   Evaluate concentration, switching frictions, network effects, regulation, information asymmetry, buyer power, and contestability.

3. **Select the measurement framework**  
   - Lerner Index when detailed cost data exist.  
   - Panzar–Rosse when input price data exist (unscaled, dynamic).  
   - Production-approach markups when output/input data and a reliable production function are available.

4. **Check behavioural signals**  
   Look for persistent margins, rigidity, discrimination, bargaining asymmetries, or strategic pricing.

5. **Test equilibrium assumptions**  
   Avoid methods requiring long-run equilibrium when markets show adjustment dynamics; prefer dynamic panel methods where appropriate.

6. **Cross-validate indicators**  
   Compare structural measures, behavioural evidence, and markup estimates; interpret differences in context.

7. **Evaluate welfare effects**  
   Identify beneficiaries and losers; distinguish efficiency-driven rents from extraction; examine dynamic effects on innovation and stability.

## Coaching Questions
1. What is the precise product and geographic market being analysed?  
2. Are prices persistently above marginal cost, or are margins transient?  
3. Could observed margins reflect inefficiency rather than power?  
4. Is the market plausibly in long-run equilibrium?  
5. Which measurement approach matches available data?  
6. What barriers or frictions protect incumbents?  
7. How does regulation shift bargaining power or rents?  
8. Who gains and who loses from current pricing?  
9. Does behaviour indicate the deliberate exercise of power?  
10. Are cross-market comparisons valid given differences in cost, quality, or mix?

## **Description**
The Perfect Substitutes model explains situations where a buyer sees two products, services, or skills as completely interchangeable. If two options deliver the same functional outcome, the buyer becomes indifferent between them and selects the cheaper one. This creates the sharpest form of price competition: any price difference leads to an immediate shift in demand toward the lower-priced alternative. In theory, this produces straight-line indifference curves and “corner solutions” where consumers buy only one option, not a mix.

The model is useful as a stress test for strategy: if your offering is close to a perfect substitute, pricing power collapses and differentiation becomes essential.

---

## **When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**
The model is an extreme theoretical case. Real markets rarely behave with perfect interchangeability. Use it carefully when:

- **Preferences exist.** Brand loyalty or small qualitative differences push goods into “imperfect substitute” territory.
- **Switching costs matter.** Time, effort, training, or financial friction can lock a buyer into their current choice.
- **Digital markets hide differences.** Tech products may look like commodities, but value often sits in data loops, tooling, or ecosystem lock-in.
- **Financial products have hidden frictions.** Products that appear equivalent (e.g., index funds) still differ through fees, execution costs, or tax consequences.

If any of these are present, the model overstates how easily customers will switch.

## **Thinking Steps**
1. **Define the core need.** Be explicit about what function the buyer is trying to satisfy.
2. **Test for functional parity.** Compare your option to alternatives. If the functional outcome is identical, treat them as near-substitutes.
3. **Search for friction or differentiation.** Identify switching costs, brand effects, relationship depth, or unique features that prevent full interchangeability.
4. **Set the strategy:**
   - If you are in a near-PS market: prioritise cost leadership and operational efficiency.
   - If you are in an imperfect-substitute market: strengthen and communicate differentiation.
5. **Apply the “cheapest one wins” rule (as a buyer).** When two options are truly equivalent, cost differences dominate rational choice, especially when they compound over time.

---

## **Coaching Questions**
- How many customers would switch if a competitor undercut my price by a trivial amount?
- What specific non-functional reasons make a customer choose my product over the cheapest equivalent?
- If I treat my skills as labour in a market, what makes me non-interchangeable compared with others?
- When I choose a product, am I paying extra to avoid switching costs? Is that premium justified relative to the price difference?

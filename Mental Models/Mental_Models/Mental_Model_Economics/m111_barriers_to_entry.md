**Description**: Core concept explanation
Barriers to Entry are the structural, financial, legal, or strategic advantages that incumbent firms possess, making it difficult or costly for new competitors to enter a market profitably. These hurdles protect existing firms, limiting competition and the threat of new entrants. When barriers to entry are high, the threat posed by new rivals is reduced, allowing existing companies to maintain high profit margins. Conversely, low entry barriers lead to market crowding, increased competition, and lower industry profitability for everyone involved. Major sources of these barriers include high capital requirements, economies of scale, product differentiation (customer loyalty/brand identity), switching costs for buyers, and government policies.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Situations where the model may mislead or fail
 * High Exit Barriers: The most attractive scenario is high entry barriers combined with low exit barriers. When high entry barriers are paired with high exit barriers, the industry becomes unstable, as struggling firms cannot leave, leading to intense rivalry and fluctuating profit margins.
 * Focusing on Traditional Metrics: Traditional competitive efforts like product differentiation, innovation, and high capital requirements may not always result in sustained abnormal economic profits once profitability is risk- and industry-adjusted.
 * Ignoring Disruptive Technology: Technological advances can rapidly eliminate structural barriers like economies of scale, making computing power, for instance, available to new, smaller players, which quickly undermines the advantages of entrenched incumbents.
 * Inelastic Demand: Attempting to overcome entry barriers with cheap pricing strategies may fail if customer demand for the product is inelastic (price-insensitive), as seen with certain branded goods where customers are loyal regardless of a discount.

**Keywords for Situations**: Common contexts or triggers where it applies
	Industry Rivalry, Threat of New Entrants, Economies of Scale, Capital Requirements, Product Differentiation, Switching Costs, Government Regulation, Monopoly, Oligopoly, Competitive Strategy, Sustained Profitability

**Thinking Steps**: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively
 1. Identify Incumbent Advantages: Analyze the industry to catalogue the specific advantages enjoyed by existing firms (e.g., brand loyalty, control over distribution channels, proprietary technology, or scale advantages).
 2. Measure Barrier Height: Assess the magnitude of the economic or regulatory cost required for a new entrant to match the incumbent's advantage (e.g., the volume needed to achieve minimum efficient scale or the capital needed for plant facilities).
 3. Determine Competitive Impact: Conclude that the higher the barriers, the lower the overall threat of new entrants, suggesting a more profitable and stable industry structure.
 4. Formulate Strategic Response (If Incumbent): To maintain advantage, continually seek to raise barriers, perhaps by acquiring complementary assets, investing in continuous product differentiation, or lobbying for favorable regulations.
 5. Formulate Entry Strategy (If New Entrant): If facing high barriers, craft strategies that bypass the barriers rather than meeting them head-on (e.g., using a minimum viable product, leasing assets instead of buying, targeting niche markets, or utilizing viral marketing to cut costs).

**Coaching Questions**: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model
 * What is the single most costly obstacle (financial, technical, or regulatory) a new company must overcome to operate at the same scale as the established leaders?
 * If we were to cut costs by 20%, would that be enough to entice a wave of new competition? (i.e., How high are our current profit margins protected by these barriers?)
 * Are our barriers to entry structural (e.g., economies of scale) or strategic (e.g., expected retaliation from us)? Which are easier for a savvy competitor to bypass?
 * If we plan to enter a new market, can we reduce our risk by leasing equipment or focusing on an underserved niche rather than incurring massive, irreversible capital requirements?

# m60_keynesian_beauty_contest.md

## Description
The Keynesian beauty contest is a concept where investors purchase an asset intending to resell it later at a higher price, but they assess the price based on ever more elaborate estimations of other people's estimations of its value. Economist John Maynard Keynes used the metaphor of a beauty contest to explain how stock prices are set - investors don't price stocks according to their intrinsic value but according to investors' perceptions of other investors' perceptions of their value. This requires thinking of the consequences of consequences (second-order thinking) rather than first-order thinking.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When you have access to objective, fundamental data that should drive decision-making
- In situations where intrinsic value matters more than perception
- When you're operating in markets that are well-regulated and transparent
- In long-term investment decisions where fundamentals ultimately prevail
- When the cost of being wrong on perception-based trades is higher than the potential gains

## Keywords for Situations
- Stock market speculation
- Cryptocurrency trading
- Real estate bubbles
- Collectibles and art markets
- Social media popularity contests
- Political campaigns and polling
- Market timing strategies
- Momentum investing
- Bubble markets
- Popularity-driven decisions

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify whether the asset or decision involves others' perceptions of its value
2. Assess how much of the price/value is based on expected future changes in other people's views
3. Determine if there's an underlying intrinsic value independent of perception
4. Consider what would happen if others changed their minds about the asset
5. Evaluate your positioning if the market consensus shifts
6. Calculate how much of your decision relies on outguessing other people's expectations
7. Determine if the risk-reward ratio justifies the speculative nature of the decision

## Coaching Questions
- What percentage of this asset's value comes from others' perceptions versus intrinsic fundamentals?
- If everyone else changed their minds about this, what would happen to its value?
- What would cause other people to change their minds about this asset?
- How much of my decision is based on guessing what others will think?
- What's my exit strategy if perceptions shift against me?
- Do I have an edge in predicting others' expectations, or am I speculating?
- How much of my portfolio is allocated to perception-driven investments versus fundamental value?
- What are the fundamental drivers of value here, separate from market sentiment?

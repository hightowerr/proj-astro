# Cost Analysis Framework

## Description
The cost analysis framework recognizes that customers incur costs in every stage of the Customer Value Chain, not just monetary price. These costs include monetary costs (the item's price, loan fees, shipping charges, etc.), time costs (time to delivery, time spent purchasing), and effort costs (measured through elementary information processes - cognitive steps that account for how consumers evaluate options, decide to pay, and so on). Understanding all three types of costs helps businesses identify where they might be creating friction for customers and where decouplers might find opportunities. The framework emphasizes that customers pay with three "currencies": their money, their time, and their effort.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
This framework may be too granular for B2B markets where the purchasing process involves multiple stakeholders with different cost considerations. Use caution when costs are difficult to quantify, especially effort costs that vary greatly between different customers. Avoid oversimplifying complex purchasing decisions where emotional or relationship factors may be more important than the three costs. Also be careful not to focus exclusively on cost reduction at the expense of value creation, as customers may be willing to pay more for increased value.

## Thinking Steps
1. Map out all the activities in your customer's value chain
2. Identify monetary costs customers incur at each stage (prices, fees, etc.)
3. Calculate time costs customers incur at each stage (time to complete, wait times, etc.)
4. Determine effort costs at each stage by identifying elementary information processes (typing search terms, creating accounts, inputting payment info, etc.)
5. Compare your cost structure to competitors to identify where you may be creating more friction
6. Assess which costs matter most to different customer segments
7. Identify opportunities to reduce costs in ways that don't compromise value
8. Consider which costs customers might be willing to pay more to reduce
9. Evaluate how technology could reduce costs in your value chain
10. Monitor how changes in costs affect customer behavior and satisfaction over time

## Coaching Questions
- What monetary costs do customers incur when engaging with your business that they don't see as valuable?
- At which stages of your customer value chain do customers spend the most time?
- What effort-intensive activities could be simplified or automated in your customer experience?
- How do your costs compare to competitors across all three categories?
- Which type of cost matters most to your customers: monetary, time, or effort?
- Where in your process do customers experience the most friction or difficulty?
- What would happen if you eliminated one type of cost for customers?
- How do costs influence your customers' decision to stay with or leave your service?
- What technology could help reduce costs for your customers?
- How do customers in different segments value their time versus their money differently?

**Category:** Economics

---

**Description: Core concept explanation**  
Public goods are goods that are **non-rivalrous** (one person’s use doesn’t reduce availability for others) and **non-excludable** (people cannot be prevented from accessing them).  
Because markets cannot price or allocate these goods effectively, they tend to suffer from **free-riding**, which leads to **under-investment** and collective loss.  
Examples include national defence, clean air, scientific research, open-source software, and street lighting.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**  
- Misclassifying goods that are excludable or rivalrous (many public services do *not* qualify).  
- Applying the model to **common-pool resources** (these follow the “Tragedy of the Commons” instead).  
- Assuming “public” means “government-provided”; private groups can create public goods too.  
- Ignoring technological shifts (e.g., digital paywalls can convert non-excludable goods into private goods).

**Thinking Steps:**  
1. **Identify the type of good**  
   Determine whether it is rivalrous or excludable.  
2. **Assess incentives**  
   Check if individuals can benefit without contributing.  
3. **Compare private vs societal value**  
   Public goods usually generate large positive externalities.  
4. **Evaluate provision mechanisms**  
   Consider taxation, voluntary contributions, club models, foundations, or tech-based enforcement.  
5. **Analyse sustainability**  
   Can the production or maintenance model persist?  
6. **Check boundary shifts**  
   Investigate whether tech or policy has changed rivalry or excludability.

---

**Coaching Questions:**  
- Is the good actually non-rivalrous and non-excludable?  
- Who gains and who pays?  
- What incentives shape contribution vs free-riding?  
- What happens if everyone declines to contribute?  
- Can regulation, taxation, or design solve the free-rider issue?  
- Can technology redefine the good’s category?  
- Is there an institutional model elsewhere that solves similar provision problems?

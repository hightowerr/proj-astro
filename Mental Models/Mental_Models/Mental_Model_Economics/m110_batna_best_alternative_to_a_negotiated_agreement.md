**Description**:
The Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement (BATNA) is the course of action a party will pursue if the current negotiation reaches an impasse and fails to produce an acceptable agreement,. Coined by negotiation researchers Roger Fisher and William Ury, the BATNA is the standard against which any proposed agreement must be measured: an agreement is only worthwhile if it produces a better result than the BATNA. Identifying a strong BATNA is the primary source of negotiating power and defines the negotiator's reservation value—the worst possible offer they are willing to accept before walking away.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Situations where the model may mislead or fail
 * Overestimating Your BATNA: Inflated perceptions of one's own fallback plan can lead to unrealistic expectations and an unwillingness to compromise, resulting in failed negotiations over deals that were actually favorable.
 * Phantom BATNAs (Uncertainty): It is difficult to assess uncertain alternatives (sometimes called "phantom BATNAs") accurately. Miscalculating an uncertain BATNA can lead to subpar decisions and may cause a negotiator to turn down a decent offer that never materializes.
 * Losing a BATNA: When a promising BATNA unexpectedly falls through (e.g., a job offer is revoked), negotiators often fail to rationally adjust their expectations to the new, weaker bargaining position, still seeking terms they are no longer entitled to.
 * Neglecting the Other Party's BATNA: Failing to consider the other party's alternatives limits the ability to craft an agreement within the Zone of Possible Agreement (ZOPA), or what Nash called the fair division of surplus.[1]

**Thinking Steps**: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively
 1. Develop Alternatives: Systematically generate a list of all conceivable actions or paths you might take if the current negotiation fails.
 2. Convert to Practical Options: Improve the most promising ideas and transform them into concrete, practical, and executable options.
 3. Select the Best Alternative: Provisionally select the single option that represents the most attractive outcome in the event of an impasse—this is your BATNA.
 4. Determine Reservation Value: Quantify the monetary or non-monetary value of your BATNA. This sets your "walk-away" point (your reservation value). You should not accept any offer that is worse than this value.
 5. Research the Other Party's BATNA: Estimate the other party's likely walk-away option (an Estimated Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement, or EATNA) to anticipate their reservation value and understand their leverage.[1] This helps locate the Zone of Possible Agreement (ZOPA),.

**Coaching Questions**: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model
 * If I walk away from this table right now, what is the single best outcome I can realistically expect to achieve elsewhere?
 * What is the lowest acceptable offer I can take before pursuing my BATNA becomes the financially superior choice (my reservation value)?
 * How confident am I that my BATNA is real and guaranteed, and what risks could make it fall through?
 * If I were the other party, what is their weakest realistic alternative? How does knowing their best alternative (and worst acceptable outcome) shape the deal I should propose? [1]
 * Am I willing to commit the resources now (time, effort, capital) to strengthen my BATNA, knowing that a stronger alternative increases my bargaining power?

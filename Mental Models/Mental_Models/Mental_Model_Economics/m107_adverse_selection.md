**Category:** Economics  

---

**Description:**  
Adverse selection is a type of market failure that occurs when one party in a transaction possesses more information about a relevant attribute (such as risk or quality) than the other. This asymmetry causes individuals with higher risks or lower-quality goods to participate more actively in the market, driving out lower-risk participants or higher-quality goods. Classic examples include health insurance (where sicker individuals are more likely to purchase coverage) and used car markets (where sellers know more about vehicle quality than buyers). The result can be inefficiency, inflated prices, or even total market collapse — famously captured by George Akerlof’s “Market for Lemons” theory.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**  
- When both parties have near-symmetric information (e.g., regulated disclosures, transparent digital marketplaces).  
- When screening or signaling mechanisms (like credit checks, warranties, or medical exams) already mitigate informational gaps.  
- When over-correcting for adverse selection could unfairly exclude legitimate low-risk or high-quality participants.  
- In settings where moral hazard (risky behaviour post-contract) is the dominant issue rather than pre-contract information asymmetry.

**Thinking Steps:**  
1. **Identify Information Asymmetry:**  
   Determine what one party knows that the other does not (e.g., health status, product quality, credit risk).  

2. **Map Incentives:**  
   Ask how private knowledge changes each side’s behaviour — who gains or loses by participating or opting out.  

3. **Forecast Market Consequences:**  
   Consider whether higher-risk participants dominate the pool, pushing out safer or higher-quality ones.  

4. **Evaluate Mitigations:**  
   Look for mechanisms like mandatory participation, risk-based pricing, or disclosure standards that can restore balance.  

5. **Assess Broader Impact:**  
   Decide if government or institutional intervention (e.g., social insurance, regulation) is warranted to prevent collapse.  

---

**Coaching Questions:**  
- What critical information might one side of this exchange be hiding or unable to reveal?  
- How could that imbalance distort participation or pricing?  
- What mechanisms (tests, guarantees, transparency tools) could reduce the information gap?  
- If high-quality participants are leaving the market, how might that trend accelerate or self-reinforce?  
- Where might policy or system design step in to make participation fairer and more sustainable?

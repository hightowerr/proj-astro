Description: Core concept explanation
Arbitrage is the practice of simultaneously buying an asset in one market and selling an identical or highly similar asset in another market at a higher price, thus generating a profit by exploiting a temporary price discrepancy. In academic finance, pure arbitrage is defined as a transaction that yields a guaranteed risk-free profit with no capital required. In the real world, however, most opportunities involve a small degree of risk and capital investment (often called risk arbitrage). Arbitrageurs play a crucial economic role by identifying and quickly exploiting these market inefficiencies, forcing the prices of the identical assets to converge and thereby enhancing overall market efficiency.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Situations where the model may mislead or fail
 * Transaction Costs: Avoid assuming the profit is guaranteed before accounting for all transaction costs (broker fees, taxes, exchange fees). Since arbitrage profits are often minuscule, these costs can easily eliminate the expected gain, rendering the activity unproductive.
 * Execution Risk: In reality, the instantaneous nature of arbitrage means the window of opportunity is extremely small. The sheer speed required introduces execution risk: the possibility that competition or illiquidity will cause the price to move before the trader can successfully complete both the buy and sell sides of the transaction.
 * Model Failure: Statistical arbitrage relies on complex mathematical models to identify subtle mispricings. This introduces model risk, where flawed assumptions or unforeseen market shifts (like extreme volatility) can cause prices to diverge further from the model's prediction, leading to significant losses instead of profits.
 * Regulatory Arbitrage: The practice of exploiting differences in financial regulations between countries (regulatory arbitrage) has become increasingly rare due to the globalization of financial markets.[1]
**Keywords for Situations**: Common contexts or triggers where it applies

**Thinking Steps**: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively
 1. Identify the Fungible Asset: Confirm that the asset or security being examined is truly identical, or at least exposed to functionally identical risks, across different markets or forms.[1]
 2. Locate the Discrepancy: Pinpoint the specific markets (e.g., geographical, future vs. spot, two different exchanges) where the asset is trading at different prices simultaneously.
 3. Calculate the Net Spread: Determine the gross profit from the price difference, and rigorously deduct all related costs, including trading fees, taxes, and estimated execution costs.
 4. Assess Execution Feasibility: Evaluate the speed and certainty of execution. A strong arbitrage opportunity must be exploitable quickly before other participants close the gap. Factor in the possibility that your action could eliminate the profit before the trade is complete.
 5. Execute the Dual Trade: Simultaneously buy the asset in the cheaper market and sell it in the more expensive market to lock in the net spread and restore equilibrium to the market prices.

**Coaching Questions**: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model
 * Is this supposed "risk-free" opportunity truly devoid of risk, or am I overlooking capital constraints or the potential for prices to diverge further in extreme circumstances?
 * Have I accounted for the cost of my own actions? Is my attempt to exploit this price difference likely to wipe out the profit margin due to the crowding effect of other competing arbitrageurs?
 * If this is a non-financial application (e.g., information arbitrage), what is the specific barrier (lack of information, high transaction costs) maintaining the inefficiency I am exploiting?
 * How confident am I in the identity of the two assets? Could small, seemingly insignificant differences lead to a major unexpected loss if market dynamics change?
 * What is my reservation price (the price that leaves zero profit after costs)? Is the observed spread large enough to absorb unavoidable real-world friction and still deliver a sufficient return?

# Razor and Blades Model

## Description
The razor-and-blades model is a business strategy where companies build a user base by selling cheaply (or even giving away) the durable component of their product, and make money by selling the disposable component at a high margin. This model requires that the initial product and its refills or accessories be technically and legally compatible only with the company's own supplies. Companies subsidize the durable component to quickly snare customers, then make up for losses by capturing high margins on the replenishable module. This model has been widely adopted across industries including razors and blades, printers and ink cartridges, game consoles and games, coffeemakers and pods, and more.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
Avoid this model when the market is highly price-sensitive and customers won't pay premium prices for refills or accessories. Use caution in markets with strong intellectual property protection challenges where competitors can easily create compatible refills. This model should be avoided when customers are likely to switch to alternative solutions that don't require repeated purchases of specific components. Also avoid this approach when the company lacks control over the supply and compatibility of replacement components. Be cautious if the market might develop competing open standards that would break the lock-in effect.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify which component of your offering could be the durable base unit
2. Determine what replenishable component could generate ongoing revenue
3. Assess the technical and legal barriers needed to ensure compatibility with only your refills
4. Calculate whether the long-term margins on refills justify subsidizing the base unit
5. Evaluate customer lifetime value under this model versus alternative pricing strategies
6. Consider how competitors might work around your compatibility restrictions
7. Plan for potential legal challenges to your restrictions on third-party refills
8. Develop strategies to maintain customer loyalty throughout the relationship
9. Assess the sustainability of high margins on refills over the long term
10. Monitor for emerging alternatives that could disrupt the razor-and-blades approach

## Coaching Questions
- What is the durable component in your solution that could be sold at low cost?
- What consumable or refill component could generate high margins?
- How would you ensure customers can only use your refills with your base product?
- Do customers in your market accept the razor-and-blades pricing approach?
- How much would customers pay for the base unit versus the refills over time?
- What would happen if competitors started making compatible refills?
- How would you respond if legal decisions weakened your control over compatibility?
- Are customers in your market sensitive to the total cost of ownership?
- What alternative solutions might customers choose to avoid razor-and-blades pricing?
- How would you adjust if customers began to resist this pricing model?

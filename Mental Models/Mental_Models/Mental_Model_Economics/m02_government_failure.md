**Category:** Economics

**Description:** 
Government Failure is a mental model for recognizing when state intervention in the economy creates inefficiencies that misallocate scarce resources and produce outcomes *worse* than the unregulated market would have achieved. Unlike market failure—which justifies intervention—government failure warns that intervention itself can become the primary source of waste, distortion, and welfare loss. It encompasses four critical drivers: information deficiencies (policymakers lack market knowledge), perverse incentives (political/bureaucratic self-interest over public good), implementation failures (regulatory capture, bureaucratic bloat), and rent-seeking (resource extraction via political manipulation). The core principle: good intentions do not guarantee good outcomes, and every policy must be weighed against the risk of generating new, larger inefficiencies.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- Do not use this model to justify blanket anti-government ideology; it is an analytical tool, not a political position
- Avoid when underlying market failures are catastrophic (e.g., climate externalities, systemic financial risk) without rigorously comparing *magnitudes* of failure
- Exercise caution when data on the counterfactual market outcome is unavailable—speculation can mislead
- Do not apply to non-economic domains (social justice, cultural policy) without substantial adaptation
- Beware confirmation bias: the model can become a weapon for special interests opposing regulation that threatens them
- Use caution during crises requiring rapid response—thorough analysis may be infeasible, but post-hoc evaluation remains critical

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify Intervention:** Define the specific government policy, regulation, or program
2. **Clarify Market Failure:** Articulate the exact market imperfection this intervention targets
3. **Map Information Gaps:** Identify what critical knowledge policymakers likely lack (dispersed market data, future actor responses)
4. **Analyze Incentive Structures:** Model how politicians, bureaucrats, and target groups will *actually* behave (not how they *should*)
5. **Project Unintended Consequences:** Brainstorm second-order effects, feedback loops, and behavioral adaptations
6. **Quantify Deadweight Loss:** Estimate resource waste and inefficiency *created* by the policy itself
7. **Compare Baselines:** Contrast projected outcomes against both the imperfect market *and* the theoretical optimal intervention
8. **Design Safeguards:** Build in sunset clauses, measurement metrics, and exit strategies before supporting implementation
9. **Monitor Actively:** Track real-world outcomes against projections; be prepared to terminate or reform

**Coaching Questions:**
- What precise market failure are we addressing, and how severe is it?
- Who benefits from this policy beyond the stated beneficiaries?
- What information do we *not* have that market actors do?
- How might regulated industries capture this agency or rule-making process?
- What behavior does this incentivize that we haven't explicitly intended?
- If we were a profit-maximizing actor faced with these rules, how would we respond?
- How will we measure whether this creates more inefficiency than it solves?
- Under what specific conditions would we *terminate* this policy?
- Can we test this on a small scale before full implementation?
- What does the *worst-case* version of this intervention look like?


# Opportunity Cost

**Description**: The value of the next-best alternative that you give up when you make a choice. It is a core concept in economics and a powerful tool to fight Narrow Framing in decision-making. It forces you to see that every choice has an implicit cost in terms of what you could have done instead with the same resources (money, time, effort). People often fail to consider opportunity costs spontaneously, leading them to make choices without seeing the full picture.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Considering every single opportunity cost for every minor decision would be exhausting and lead to analysis paralysis. The model is most useful for decisions involving a significant allocation of limited resources (such as time or money). The "next-best" alternative can also be subjective and hard to define perfectly, so the goal is to consider strong alternatives, not every possible one.

**Keywords for Situations**: Budgeting, resource allocation, time management, career choices, major purchases, strategic planning, personal finance.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  When evaluating an option, don't just compare it to nothing. Explicitly ask, **"What am I giving up by making this choice?"**
2.  For financial decisions, frame the choice clearly to make the opportunity cost visible. For example, instead of "Should I buy this video for $14.99?", ask "Should I buy this video, or should I keep the $14.99 for other purchases?" As research shows, this simple reframing dramatically changes choices.
3.  For time-based decisions, ask, "What is the next-best use of this afternoon/week/year?"
4.  Use the concept to compare multiple options directly by bundling the cost difference. For example, "Would you rather have the $1,000 stereo, or the $700 stereo and $300 worth of music?"
5.  Listen for opportunity costs in the language of others, like Dwight D. Eisenhower's speech: "The cost of one modern heavy bomber is this: a modern brick school in more than 30 cities..."

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "What is the opportunity cost of this decision? What could we do with that same amount of time and money if we didn't do this?"
*   "Let's rephrase the choice. Is it 'buy the new equipment' or 'keep the $50,000 for other things, like marketing or hiring'?"
*   "I know we like option A, but what's the next-best alternative we'd be giving up to pursue it?"
*   "Thinking about your time, if you say 'yes' to this project, what are you implicitly saying 'no' to?"

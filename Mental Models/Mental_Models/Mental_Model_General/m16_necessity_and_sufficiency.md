# Necessity and Sufficiency

## Description
Necessity and sufficiency distinguish between what is required for an outcome versus what is enough to guarantee it. A necessary condition must be present for the desired event or effect to occur, but having it alone doesn't guarantee the outcome. A sufficient condition, when present, guarantees the outcome will occur. The gap between what is necessary and what is sufficient is often filled with luck, chance, or other factors beyond direct control. Many things that are necessary to succeed are not sufficient to guarantee success.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When assuming that having necessary conditions means you have sufficient conditions to guarantee an outcome
- When not recognizing that additional factors beyond necessary conditions are needed for success
- When making decisions based on incomplete understanding of what is required versus what is sufficient for an outcome

## Keywords for Situations

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the desired outcome or event.
2. Determine what conditions are necessary for the outcome to occur.
3. Identify what conditions would be sufficient to guarantee the outcome.
4. Recognize the gap between necessary and sufficient conditions.
5. Consider what additional factors beyond necessary conditions are needed.
6. Assess the likelihood of achieving sufficient conditions versus just necessary ones.
7. Plan for contingencies when only necessary conditions are met.

## Coaching Questions
- What conditions must exist for your desired outcome (necessary conditions)?
- What would be enough to guarantee your desired outcome (sufficient conditions)?
- Are you confusing what is necessary with what is sufficient for success?
- What additional factors beyond the necessary ones are needed to succeed?
- What is the gap between what you have and what you need to guarantee results?
- How much of your expected outcome depends on factors beyond your control?
- Are you assuming that meeting necessary conditions will guarantee success?
- What could cause failure even if all necessary conditions are met?
- Are you overestimating the probability of success based on necessary conditions?
- How might you better account for the difference between necessary and sufficient conditions in your planning?

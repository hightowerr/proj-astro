# Causation vs. Correlation

## Description
Confusion between these two terms often leads to inaccurate assumptions about how the world works. We notice two things happening at the same time (correlation) and mistakenly conclude that one causes the other (causation). We then often act upon that erroneous conclusion, making decisions that can have immense influence across our lives. The correlation coefficient between two measures, which varies between -1 and 1, is a measure of the relative weight of the factors they share. For example, two phenomena with few shared factors, such as bottled-water consumption versus suicide rate, should have a correlation coefficient of close to 0. By contrast, measures that are solely dependent on the same factor will have a correlation coefficient of 1.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When assuming that correlated events have a causal relationship
- When making important decisions based solely on correlational data
- When the correlation coefficient is weak and may not indicate meaningful relationship
- When not considering that both variables might be caused by a third factor
- When not considering the possibility of reverse causation

## Keywords for Situations
- Statistical analysis
- Decision making with data
- Policy development
- Research interpretation
- Media consumption
- Investment decisions
- Medical research

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the two correlated phenomena you're examining.
2. Determine the correlation coefficient and strength of the relationship.
3. Consider whether A causes B, B causes A, or both are influenced by a third factor.
4. Look for evidence of temporal sequence - which came first?
5. Examine whether the correlation is consistent across different contexts.
6. Evaluate if there's a plausible mechanism for causation.
7. Consider alternative explanations for the observed correlation.

## Coaching Questions
- Are you assuming that correlation implies causation in this situation?
- Could a third factor be causing both of the variables you're observing?
- Have you considered that the causation might run in the opposite direction?
- How strong is the correlation between these two phenomena?
- What evidence would be needed to prove a causal relationship?
- Are you confusing what causes the event with what simply correlates with it?
- What other factors might explain the relationship you're seeing?
- How might regression to the mean be affecting your interpretation?
- Have you looked for evidence that supports causation beyond just correlation?
- What would be the consequences if you're wrong about the causal relationship?

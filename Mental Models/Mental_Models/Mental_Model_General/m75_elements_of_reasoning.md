
# The Elements of Reasoning

**Description**: A framework for analyzing thought by breaking it down into its eight essential and universal component parts. The premise is that all reasoning, whether good or bad, contains these elements. By systematically identifying and questioning each part, one can deconstruct any line of thought to better understand it and identify potential weaknesses.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This is primarily a tool for deconstruction and analysis, not for generative brainstorming. It is most useful for understanding a line of reasoning that already exists (in an article, a speech, or one's own mind), rather than creating something new from a blank slate. Overly mechanical application can stifle creative or associative thinking.

**Keywords for Situations**: Argument analysis, critical thinking, deconstructing an article or speech, understanding another's reasoning, improving one's own reasoning, structured thinking.

**Thinking Steps**: To analyze a piece of reasoning, identify and question each of the eight elements:
1.  **Purpose**: What is the goal or agenda of this reasoning? (e.g., *"What are we trying to accomplish here?"*)
2.  **Question at Issue**: What is the central question being addressed or problem being solved? (e.g., *"What is the main question in my mind?"*)
3.  **Information**: What data, facts, and experiences is the reasoning based on? (e.g., *"On what information are you basing that comment?"*)
4.  **Inferences/Conclusions**: What conclusions are being drawn from the information? (e.g., *"How did you reach that conclusion?"*)
5.  **Concepts**: What are the main ideas, theories, or principles being used? (e.g., *"What is the main idea you are using in your reasoning?"*)
6.  **Assumptions**: What is being taken for granted or presupposed? (e.g., *"What exactly are you taking for granted here?"*)
7.  **Implications/Consequences**: Where is this line of thought headed? What follows from it? (e.g., *"If we do this, what is likely to happen as a result?"*)
8.  **Point of View**: From what perspective or frame of reference is this reasoning being presented? (e.g., *"Is there another point of view we should consider?"*)

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Let's break down this argument using the Elements of Reasoning. What is the author's main **purpose**?"
*   "What are the key **assumptions** they are making that they haven't stated explicitly?"
*   "Based on the **information** they provided, are their **conclusions** logical?"
*   "What are the long-term **implications** if we accept their conclusion and act on it?"
*   "Is there another **point of view** we should consider before we agree with this?"

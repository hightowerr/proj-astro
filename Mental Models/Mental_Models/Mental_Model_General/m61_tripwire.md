
# Tripwire

**Description**: A pre-defined, specific signal that snaps you out of "autopilot" and forces you to make a conscious decision or re-evaluate a situation. It helps to combat overconfidence and organizational momentum by creating a moment for deliberate choice, rather than allowing you to drift into failure. The classic example is Van Halen's contract rider demanding a bowl of M&Ms with no brown ones; seeing a brown M&M was a tripwire that triggered a full safety check of the stage production, indicating the contract hadn't been read carefully.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: A tripwire must be clear, specific, and respected. A vague tripwire (e.g., "we'll check in when things feel wrong") is useless. If the culture of a team or organization ignores tripwires, they lose all power. They are less useful for simple, one-off decisions and most useful for projects, habits, or commitments that unfold over time.

**Keywords for Situations**: Project management, habit formation, strategic planning, risk management, long-term goals, monitoring progress, budget control.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  When making a decision that will unfold over time, acknowledge the risk of overconfidence and of going on "autopilot."
2.  Identify a future moment or a specific metric that should trigger a re-evaluation.
3.  Set a clear, unambiguous tripwire. This could be:
    *   A **date** on a calendar ("If we haven't launched by June 1, we will reconsider our strategy").
    *   A **metric** ("If customer retention drops below 80% for two consecutive weeks, we will hold an emergency meeting").
    *   A **budget threshold** or partition ("We will only fund the first phase of the project; to get more funding, the team must prove viability").
4.  Commit to honoring the tripwire when it is triggered. The tripwire doesn't dictate the outcome, but it forces a conscious choice at a critical moment.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "We're committing to this path, but how will we know if we're drifting into failure? Can we set a tripwire?"
*   "What's a specific, measurable signal that would make us pause and reconsider this decision?"
*   "Instead of giving this project a huge budget, can we give it a small initial budget that, when exhausted, acts as a tripwire forcing us to decide whether to continue?"
*   "What date can we put on the calendar right now that will force us to ask, 'Is this still the right path?'"

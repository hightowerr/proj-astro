
# Hypothesis-Driven Analysis

**Description**: An approach to problem-solving where one forms a clear, testable hypothesis—a potential answer to the problem—early in the process. This hypothesis then acts as a guide for the analysis, making the work more focused and efficient than aimless data gathering. It allows a team to maintain a "one-day answer": a coherent, albeit preliminary, solution path at any point in the project. It is the core of the scientific method applied to business and policy problems.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: A strong initial hypothesis can create a significant risk of **confirmation bias**, where the team only seeks data that supports their initial guess while ignoring contradictory evidence. It is critical to maintain objectivity and be willing—even eager—to disprove the hypothesis. This approach is also less useful in purely exploratory or ethnographic research where the goal is to understand a landscape without a specific problem in mind.

**Keywords for Situations**: Consulting projects, scientific method, strategic analysis, diagnostics, efficient problem solving, data analysis.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  After defining and disaggregating a problem (Steps 1 & 2), formulate a plausible initial hypothesis about the solution. (e.g., "We should install solar PV now because the payback is attractive and future cost declines won't offset immediate savings.")
2.  Use the logic tree to identify the arguments and supporting facts needed to prove or disprove the hypothesis.
3.  Prioritize the analysis that will most quickly and efficiently test the core of the hypothesis (the "critical path").
4.  Gather and analyze the data with an objective mindset. Actively look for facts that would disprove the hypothesis.
5.  Refine, pivot, or discard the hypothesis as the analysis provides new insights. The initial hypothesis is a starting point, not a destination.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "What is our initial hypothesis here? Based on what we know today, what do we think the answer is?"
*   "What would have to be true for this hypothesis to be correct? Let's list the conditions we need to test."
*   "What is the single most important piece of analysis we need to do to test the core of this hypothesis?"
*   "To avoid confirmation bias, let's spend the next hour only looking for facts that would disprove our hypothesis. What would show us we are wrong?"

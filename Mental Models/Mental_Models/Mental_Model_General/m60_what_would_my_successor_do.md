**Description**: A powerful question for attaining emotional distance from a decision, especially one where you are paralyzed by internal politics, historical legacy, or short-term emotion. By asking what an outsider brought in to replace you would do, you shift your perspective from "What do *I* feel?" to "What is the *right thing to do*?" This was famously used by Intel's President Andy Grove to make the difficult but crucial decision to exit the memory chip business, breaking years of fruitless debate.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This tool is most effective for leaders or individuals with a long history in a role who may be overly constrained by past decisions, sunk costs, or emotional attachments. It is less relevant for newcomers or for decisions that don't carry heavy emotional or historical baggage. The "successor" is assumed to be rational and unencumbered, which may not always be a valid assumption.

**Keywords for Situations**: Overcoming inertia, breaking a deadlock, strategic pivots, leadership decisions, emotional conflict, legacy systems or projects, sunk cost fallacy.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Recognize that you are stuck or agonizing over a decision, likely due to emotional conflict, political infighting, or historical baggage.
2.  Pose the question directly to yourself or your team: **"If we got kicked out and the board brought in a new CEO, what do you think they would do?"** (Or the personal equivalent: "If a smart, objective person took over my life, what would they do in this situation?").
3.  Answer the question honestly. The answer is often immediately obvious because the hypothetical "outsider" is not encumbered by the same emotional attachments or historical loyalties.
4.  Follow up with Grove's second question: **"Why shouldn't you and I walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves?"** This provides the impetus to act on the clear-eyed insight you've just gained.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "We seem to be stuck on this. Let's try a thought experiment: If we were all fired today and a new team came in tomorrow, what's the first thing they would do about this problem?"
*   "Let's remove our personal history from this decision. What would a smart outsider, looking at this for the first time, recommend?"
*   "What would your successor do? ... Okay, so why aren't we doing that?"
*   "If you were advising a stranger in your exact position, what would you tell them is the obvious choice?"

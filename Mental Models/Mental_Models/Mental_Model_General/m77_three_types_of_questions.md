
# Three Types of Questions

**Description**: A system for classifying questions to understand the type of reasoning required to answer them. This model avoids the simplistic and often misleading division of all statements into mere "fact or opinion." The three types are: 
1. **One-System Questions (Questions of Procedure)**: Have a single, correct answer that can be found through a known method or procedure (e.g., science, mathematics).
2. **No-System Questions (Questions of Preference)**: Are answered based on subjective, personal preference. There is no "correct" answer, only what an individual likes or wants.
3. **Conflicting-System Questions (Questions of Judgment)**: Require reasoning and can have better or worse answers, but do not have one single verifiable "correct" one. These are debatable questions where evidence and reasoning are evaluated within multiple competing viewpoints (e.g., ethics, philosophy, complex social issues).

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This is a classification tool. Its main purpose is to clarify the nature of a discussion or problem. It is less of a direct problem-solving technique and more of a way to ensure the "rules of the game" are clear before you start. Some complex questions may have elements of all three types, making a clean classification difficult.

**Keywords for Situations**: Debates, discussions, clarifying arguments, problem definition, distinguishing fact from opinion, ethical dilemmas, policy analysis.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  When faced with a question, first determine which of the three types it is.
2.  Ask: "Are there relevant facts we need to consider?" If the answer is no, it is a **No-System** question of preference.
3.  If there are facts, ask: "Do the facts alone settle the question?" If yes, it is a **One-System** question of procedure.
4.  If the facts can be interpreted in different reasonable ways, or if multiple value systems apply, it is a **Conflicting-System** question of judgment.
5.  Adjust your approach accordingly: For One-System, apply the correct procedure. For No-System, state your preference. For Conflicting-System, engage in reasoned dialogue, weigh evidence, and consider multiple perspectives to arrive at the best possible judgment.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "What type of question are we dealing with here? Is there one right answer, is it a matter of opinion, or is it something we need to judge based on reasoning?"
*   "Can we settle this question with facts alone, or are there multiple valid ways to look at it?"
*   "Are we treating a question of judgment (like 'what is the best way to solve this problem?') as if it's a simple question of fact?"
*   "Before we answer, let's agree on what kind of question this is. Are we looking for a verifiable fact, sharing our personal preferences, or trying to form the best possible judgment on a complex issue?"

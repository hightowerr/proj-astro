
# Multitracking

**Description**: The practice of considering several options simultaneously. Rather than analyzing one option at a time (single-tracking), you keep multiple distinct alternatives on the table and compare them. This process helps to combat narrow framing and confirmation bias. It prevents you from getting locked into a single path too early, deepens your understanding of the situation by allowing for comparison, and makes you more likely to have a good backup plan if your initial favorite fails.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Multitracking can lead to choice overload or shallow analysis if you try to track too many options at once. The sweet spot is often two or three distinct alternatives. The process can also feel inefficient because some of the work you do on the options you ultimately discard may feel "wasted." It is less useful for very simple, low-stakes decisions.

**Keywords for Situations**: Evaluating candidates, design choices, strategic planning, brainstorming solutions, product development, vendor selection, A/B testing.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  When faced with a choice, ensure you have more than one viable option on the table before moving into deep analysis.
2.  If you only have one option, use techniques like the **Vanishing Options Test** or considering **Opportunity Cost** to generate more.
3.  Explore the options in parallel. For example, if choosing a design, create mockups for multiple designs. If hiring, interview several candidates. If choosing a vendor, run a small-scale "horse race" with a few of them, as HopeLab did.
4.  Use the comparison between the options to learn more about the problem and what you truly want. Notice what makes each option distinct and valuable.
5.  Be prepared to combine the best features of several options into a new, superior hybrid option.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "We're spending a lot of time analyzing this one option. What's another path we could explore at the same time?"
*   "Instead of picking a winner now, can we run a 'horse race'? Let's develop two or three of these ideas a bit further and then compare them."
*   "What are we learning by comparing these options side-by-side? What are the key tradeoffs?"
*   "This feels like a choice between A and B. Is there a C we're missing? Or could we combine the best parts of A and B into a new solution?"

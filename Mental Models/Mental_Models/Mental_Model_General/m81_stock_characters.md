# m81_stock_characters.md

## Description
Stock characters are archetypal characters or character types that are instantly recognizable to audiences based on common experiences and cultural knowledge. They are based on generalizations about types of people and serve as familiar, easily understood elements in stories. These characters often have predictable behaviors and roles, making them useful for advancing plot or establishing setting quickly. While they can provide comfort and familiarity to audiences, overuse can lead to predictability and lack of depth. They function as narrative shortcuts that help audiences quickly understand roles and relationships.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When creating complex, multi-dimensional characters is the goal
- In literary works requiring depth and originality
- When the focus is on character development and growth
- When trying to avoid stereotypes and generalizations
- When the story depends on surprise or unpredictability
- When creating content for diverse audiences with varied experiences
- When challenging social norms or expectations is important

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify which character roles are essential to your story or message
2. Determine if a stock character would serve your purpose quickly and clearly
3. Assess whether the audience will recognize the character type immediately
4. Consider how to add unique elements that distinguish your version
5. Evaluate whether the stock character reinforces or challenges stereotypes
6. Determine if the character needs development beyond the basic type
7. Assess how the stock character serves the overall narrative purpose
8. Consider if subverting stock character expectations might enhance your work

## Coaching Questions
- What character type does my audience need to recognize immediately?
- Which stock character archetype fits this role in my story?
- How can I use this familiar type while still adding something unique?
- What stereotypes might this character type reinforce?
- Does this character need development beyond the basic stock type?
- How might different audiences interpret this stock character differently?
- Could subverting expectations of this character type improve my work?
- What unique elements can I add to distinguish this character?
- How does this stock character serve my overall narrative purpose?

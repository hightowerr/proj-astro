# m79_archetypes.md

## Description
Archetypes are recurring patterns, symbols, or themes that appear across cultures and throughout history. The psychologist Carl Jung believed that archetypes emerge from the collective unconscious of the human race, which explains why they are almost universal. In literature, archetypes manifest as recurring character types, situations, or symbols. They appear in our dreams, religion, and art. While writers may use archetypes in new and creative ways, the underlying symbolism remains powerful because it resonates deeply in the human psyche. Each archetype has distinctive qualities, features, values, and motivations.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When you want to create completely original and unexpected content
- In situations requiring innovation and breaking from traditional patterns
- When the audience expects fresh, non-stereotypical approaches
- When archetypal patterns might lead to predictable outcomes
- When the goal is to challenge or subvert traditional expectations
- When archetypal associations might create unwanted biases

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify which archetypal patterns might apply to your situation
2. Assess the cultural and psychological resonances of different archetypes
3. Consider how archetypal elements might influence audience perception
4. Examine whether the archetype fits the unique aspects of your situation
5. Determine which archetypal elements to emphasize or modify
6. Consider how to use archetypes without making characters or concepts predictable
7. Evaluate the potential impact of archetypal associations on your audience
8. Plan how to add unique elements that distinguish your approach from pure archetypes

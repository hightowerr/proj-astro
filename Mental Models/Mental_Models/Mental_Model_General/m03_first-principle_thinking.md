Category: General

**Description**: Core concept explanation
First-principles thinking means deconstructing a problem to its irreducible truths (physics, maths, contracts, incentives), then rebuilding a solution from scratch—challenging every assumption and ignoring precedent unless it survives verification. It shifts you from “we do it this way because we always have” to “what must be true for this to work,” producing testable, comparable units before you commit capital, code, or policy.  

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):** Situations where the model may mislead or fail  
1. Incremental optimisation: When a system works and only needs small tweaks; analogy and benchmarks are faster.
2. Severe time pressure: Fire-fighting and on-call incidents favour proven playbooks over deep derivations.
3. Sparse or unmeasurable data: You risk elegant fiction if “truths” can’t be verified.
4. Highly regulated / legacy-coupled domains: Clean-sheet designs that ignore migration and compliance costs.
5. Taste/aesthetics decisions: Normative goals where “truths” don’t capture preference.
6. Analysis paralysis cultures: Use bounded timeboxes (“appetite”) to prevent infinite decomposition.

**Keywords for Situations:** Common contexts or triggers where it applies  
    Innovation • Greenfield design • Cost drivers • Unit economics • Constraints • Invariants • Value-chain mapping • Capacity planning • Pricing logic • Second-order effects • Premortems • ROI under constraints

**Thinking Steps**: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively  

1. Identify the problem/goal (What?)  
	- State the concrete outcome; rank pains/gains by urgent / important / both.
	- Checkpoint: Is the top problem measurable and evidenced (not anecdotal)? If not, loop.
2. Clarify the system (How?) 
	- Map inputs → activities → outputs, with units, owners, scale, timing, blind spots, and stakeholder views.
	- Checkpoint: Does every arrow have a quantified driver and a responsible owner?
3. Deconstruct to fundamentals (Why?)  
    - Break into invariant truths (physics, maths, contracts, incentives); ask “Why?” until you can’t go deeper.
    - Quantify primitives (kWh, FLOPs, £/GB-month, minutes of dwell, error rates).
	- Checkpoint: Are the fundamentals verifiable and numerically linked to the problem?
4. Reconstruct solutions from the ground up (Solution)  - 
	- Generate options that change one invariant at a time; model cost/benefit, feasibility, and constraints (capital, compliance, talent, time).
	- Select the option with the highest ROI under real constraints.
	- Checkpoint: Does the option move the driver you quantified? What’s the smallest shippable slice?
    
5. Stress-test (Devil’s advocate)  
	- Flip assumptions, probe second-order effects, incentives, ethics, and compliance; run premortems and “what would have to be true?”.
	- Checkpoint: Are top risks priced in with contingencies or kill-switches? If not, revisit earlier steps.
	- 
6. Optimise and integrate  
	- Iterate with real data; compare against precedent to avoid rediscovering solved problems; document decisions and invariants for future audits.
**Coaching Questions:** Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model  
	- **Find**: What single measurable problem matters most this cycle, and what data proves it?
	- **Clarify**: Can I draw the input→activity→output chain with units and named owners for each arrow?
	- **Deconstruct**: Which statements are inviolable truths versus habits or folklore? What is the real invariant?
	- **Reconstruct**: Which option changes one invariant the most per £/week of appetite? What constraint bites first?
	- **Stress-test**: If my key assumption flips tomorrow, what breaks—and what’s my pre-planned response?
	- **Optimise**: What is the smallest experiment that would most quickly falsify this approach?


# SMART Problem Definition

**Description**: A mnemonic checklist for creating a clear and effective problem statement. A good problem statement should be **S**pecific, **M**easurable, **A**ction-oriented, **R**elevant, and **T**imely. The book "Bulletproof Problem Solving" notes that while SMART is a useful guide, it should be augmented with two other critical factors: being **outcome-focused** (not just focused on activities) and ensuring the problem is **solved at the highest level possible** for the organization, not just optimized for a small part.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: SMART is a checklist for refining a problem statement, not a substitute for the deep thinking required to frame the problem correctly in the first place. A statement can be technically SMART but still address the wrong issue. It is a tool for sharpening a problem statement, not for generating the initial, creative framing of the problem.

**Keywords for Situations**: Goal setting, project management, performance management, problem definition, strategic planning, project charters.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Draft an initial problem statement.
2.  Review the statement against each of the SMART criteria:
    *   **Specific**: Is the problem statement precise and unambiguous? Does it state exactly what needs to be solved?
    *   **Measurable**: Can we quantify success? What metrics will we use to know the problem is solved?
    *   **Action-oriented**: Does solving the problem lead to a clear action or decision?
    *   **Relevant**: Is this problem important and relevant to the overall goals of the organization or individual?
    *   **Timely**: Is there a clear timeframe or deadline for solving the problem?
3.  Go beyond the basic mnemonic and ask the two additional questions:
    *   Is the statement focused on the ultimate desired **outcome**? (e.g., not "build a website," but "increase online sales by 20%").
    *   Are we solving this at the **highest, most impactful level** possible?
4.  Refine the problem statement based on this complete review.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Let's test our problem statement against the SMART criteria. Is it specific enough?"
*   "How will we measure success here? What does 'done' look like in numbers?"
*   "What action will we take once this problem is solved? Is the statement oriented towards that action?"
*   "Is this the right problem for us to be working on right now? How is it relevant to our main goals?"
*   "What's our deadline for this? Is the problem statement time-bound?"
*   "Are we focused on an activity, or the actual outcome we want to achieve?"

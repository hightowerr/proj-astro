# Falsifiability

## Description
Falsifiability is the idea that a theory is part of empirical science if and only if it conflicts with possible experiences and is therefore in principle falsifiable by experience. In other words, if you can't prove something wrong, you can't really prove it right either. This means that if a theory is to be considered scientific, it must have an element of risk to it—namely, it has to be able to be proven wrong under stated conditions. Falsification is the opposite of verification: you must try to show that the theory is incorrect and, if you fail to do so, you actually strengthen it.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
- When considering theories that are not intended to be testable against reality
- When dealing with historical deterministic theories that cannot be tested experimentally
- When a theory has become immune to falsifying evidence because any new evidence is interpreted through the lens of the theory
- When the theory is not making specific, testable predictions

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify the theory or belief you're examining.
2. Ask: "What evidence would demonstrate that this theory is incorrect?"
3. Determine if the theory makes specific, testable predictions.
4. Look for potential ways to test the theory against reality.
5. Consider whether the theory can be falsified in principle, even if not in practice.
6. Examine if evidence that contradicts the theory is being interpreted through the lens of the theory itself.
7. Assess whether the theory has the characteristics of testable scientific knowledge.

## Coaching Questions
- What would have to happen for you to conclude this theory is wrong?
- Is this theory testable against observable reality?
- Does this theory make specific predictions that could be proven false?
- Are you interpreting contradictory evidence through the lens of your existing beliefs?
- How might you be protecting this theory from being falsified?
- What would be the consequences if this theory was incorrect?
- Can you think of any evidence that would definitively disprove this theory?
- How do you know if your belief system is becoming immune to contradictory evidence?
- What have you done to actively try to disprove your beliefs?
- Are you approaching this theory with the mindset that it could be wrong?


# Socratic Questioning

**Description**: Socratic Questioning is a disciplined, systematic, and deep form of questioning used to explore complex ideas, get to the truth of things, uncover assumptions, and analyze concepts. It is distinguished from normal questioning by its systematicity and its focus on foundational concepts, principles, and problems. The goal is to help individuals gain a greater command of their own thinking and develop a powerful inner voice of reason by probing the structure of their own thought.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Socratic questioning is not a quick method for transferring facts; it is a method of exploration. It can be slow and should be used with patience. It is less effective in situations requiring immediate, simple answers rather than deep exploration. The spirit of genuine inquiry is crucial; using the method to trap, belittle, or humiliate someone is a misuse of the art.

**Keywords for Situations**: Critical thinking, deep learning, exploring beliefs, coaching, self-reflection, teaching, problem-solving, analyzing arguments, clarifying concepts.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Identify a foundational concept, principle, or problem to explore (e.g., What is justice? What is friendship?).
2.  Ask an initial question to open the discussion and elicit a viewpoint (e.g., "What do you think justice is?").
3.  Listen carefully to the response, not to rebut, but to understand.
4.  Ask a follow-up question that probes the assumptions, concepts, evidence, or implications of the response. Use the other person's answer as the basis for your subsequent question.
5.  Continue this disciplined process, systematically breaking down the initial statement using questions that target the elements of reasoning (e.g., purpose, assumptions, viewpoint) and assess the quality of reasoning (e.g., clarity, depth, breadth).
6.  Periodically summarize what has been figured out and what remains unresolved to keep the dialogue focused and productive.

**Coaching Questions**: (This model *is* a set of coaching questions, so this section describes how to apply it to oneself or others).
*   To apply to yourself: "What is the foundational concept I need to understand here? What are the assumptions underlying my current belief? Let me follow the implications of my thinking. If I believe X, what else must be true?"
*   To coach another: "That's an interesting point. Could you elaborate further? What do you mean by [key term]? What are you assuming when you say that? What is an alternative viewpoint?"

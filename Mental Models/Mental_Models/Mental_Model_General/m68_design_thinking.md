
# Design Thinking

**Description**: A user-centered, iterative approach to creative problem-solving, often used for product or service design but applicable to a wide range of problems where the user experience is critical. "Bulletproof Problem Solving" presents it as a complementary process to the seven-step analytical framework. Its typical stages are: **Empathize**, **Define**, **Ideate**, **Build**, and **Test**. The core idea is to make decisions that evolve based on evidence from users' reactions to prototypes, rather than relying solely on historical data or internal instincts.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Design thinking is not a replacement for analytical rigor. While excellent for exploring new and creative solutions, it should be paired with analytical problem-solving to ensure the solutions are not just desirable to users but also viable and feasible for the organization. It is most critical when the "user experience" is a central part of the problem and less so for problems that are purely technical or financial.

**Keywords for Situations**: User experience (UX), product design, service design, innovation, creative problem solving, customer needs, prototyping, human-centered design.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Empathize**: Spend significant time understanding the user and their "pain points" through direct observation, interviews, and research. The goal is to gain an empathetic understanding of the people you're designing for.
2.  **Define**: Use the insights from the empathy stage to construct a clear, user-centered problem statement. This corresponds to Step 1 (Define the Problem) of the BPS cycle, but with a strong user focus.
3.  **Ideate**: Brainstorm a wide range of potential solutions to the problem. This step is about quantity over quality initially and corresponds to the hypothesis generation part of Step 2 (Disaggregate).
4.  **Build (Prototype)**: Create low-fidelity, inexpensive, and scaled-down versions of the potential solutions that users can interact with.
5.  **Test**: Put the prototypes in front of users, observe their reactions, and gather feedback to learn what works and what doesn't.
6.  **Iterate**: Use the feedback from testing to refine the solution, deepen your understanding of the user, and even redefine the original problem statement. The process is a cycle, not a linear path.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Have we spent enough time empathizing with our users? Do we truly understand their pain points?"
*   "Instead of analyzing this further in a spreadsheet, could we build a quick prototype to test our idea with actual users?"
*   "What's the simplest, cheapest prototype we can build to get feedback on this core concept?"
*   "What did we learn from the user tests? How should that feedback change our thinking or our solution?"
*   "How can we integrate the user-centered insights from design thinking with the analytical rigor of our problem-solving process?"

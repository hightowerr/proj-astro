
# The Universal Intellectual Standards

**Description**: A set of standards used to assess the quality of reasoning about a problem, issue, or situation. These are the criteria that educated and reasonable persons use to judge thinking. Applying these standards through questioning—either to one's own thinking or the thinking of others—helps to raise the quality of reasoning. The primary standards include Clarity, Accuracy, Precision, Relevance, Depth, Breadth, Logic, Significance, and Fairness.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: The standards are almost universally applicable to any reasoning process. However, the level of rigor for each standard depends on the context. A casual conversation does not require the same level of precision as a scientific paper. Applying them too rigidly during an initial creative brainstorming session might stifle idea generation. The key is to apply them appropriately for the task at hand.

**Keywords for Situations**: Evaluating arguments, critical thinking, editing a paper, providing feedback, improving reasoning, decision making, self-reflection.

**Thinking Steps**: When evaluating a line of thought (your own or someone else's), systematically apply these standards by asking targeted questions:
1.  **Clarity**: Is the thinking easy to understand? Can it be elaborated on? (*"Could you give me an example?"*)
2.  **Accuracy**: Is the information presented true and correct? (*"How could we check on that to see if it is true?"*)
3.  **Precision**: Is the information specific and detailed enough for the context? (*"Could you give me more details?"*)
4.  **Relevance**: Does the information or argument bear directly on the question at hand? (*"How does that relate to the problem?"*)
5.  **Depth**: Does the thinking grapple with the complexities of the issue, or is it superficial? (*"What factors make this a difficult problem?"*)
6.  **Breadth**: Does the thinking consider other relevant points of view? (*"Do we need to look at this from another perspective?"*)
7.  **Logic**: Does it all make sense together? Do the conclusions follow from the evidence? (*"Does what you say follow from the evidence?"*)
8.  **Significance**: Is the information or issue the most important one to be considering? (*"Which of these facts are most important?"*)
9.  **Fairness**: Is the thinking considering the views of others sympathetically, especially those it disagrees with? (*"Am I sympathetically representing the viewpoints of others?"*)

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "I understand your main point, but can we make it more **precise**? What are the specific details?"
*   "That's an interesting idea, but is it **relevant** to the core question we're trying to answer right now?"
*   "I think we're only looking at this from one angle. To add **breadth**, what would someone who disagrees with us say?"
*   "Let's double-check the **accuracy** of that claim. How can we verify that this information is true?"
*   "This is a complex issue. Are we addressing the complexities with enough **depth**, or are we staying on the surface?"

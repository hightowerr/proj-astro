
# "AND" not "OR" thinking

**Description**: A mindset that pushes you to find ways to achieve multiple objectives simultaneously, rather than assuming you must choose between them. It reframes a choice from "Should I do this OR that?" to "Is there a way I can do this AND that?" It is a powerful tool for breaking out of a Narrow Frame by rejecting false dichotomies and seeking integrative solutions.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: Sometimes, options are truly mutually exclusive (e.g., you can't be in two places at once), and trying to force an "AND" solution can lead to an unworkable, over-complicated compromise. It is also not an excuse to avoid making a tough choice when resources are genuinely too scarce to pursue multiple paths. The goal is to challenge assumed constraints, not ignore real ones.

**Keywords for Situations**: Negotiation, resource allocation, career planning, relationship compromises, strategic planning, product features, work-life balance.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Identify a decision that is framed as an "OR" choice (e.g., "Should we focus on quality OR speed?", "Should I take the safe job OR the risky one?").
2.  Explicitly ask the question: **"How can we do both?"** or **"Is there a way to get the best of both worlds?"**
3.  Brainstorm creative solutions that integrate elements of both options. For example, Steve Cole at HopeLab didn't choose one design firm; he ran a "horse race" with five, getting the benefits of multiple perspectives AND moving the project forward.
4.  Consider sequencing. Can you do one option now and the other one later?
5.  Look for ways to modify the options to make them compatible, as Joseph Priestley did when he negotiated his job offer to get both financial security AND scholarly independence.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "We're debating between this OR that. What would it look like if we tried to do both?"
*   "Is this really an either/or choice, or is that just how we've framed it?"
*   "How could we get 80% of the benefit from both options, instead of 100% from just one?"
*   "What would have to be true for us to be able to do this AND that? Let's explore that possibility."

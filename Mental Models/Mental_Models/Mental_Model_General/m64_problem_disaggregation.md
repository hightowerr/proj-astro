
# Problem Disaggregation

**Description**: The process of taking a large, complex problem apart into smaller, more manageable pieces. This is described as the most important step in the Bulletproof Problem Solving framework because any problem of real consequence is too complicated to solve without breaking it down first. Disaggregation reveals the structure of the problem, uncovers potential root causes or drivers, and allows a team to prioritize which parts of the problem to focus on. The primary tool for disaggregation is the logic tree.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: The way you choose to "cleave" or break down the problem is a critical judgment call. An illogical or unhelpful disaggregation can obscure the real issue or lead to dead ends. It is often necessary to test different ways of breaking down the problem to find the one that yields the most insight. For very simple, linear problems, formal disaggregation may be overkill.

**Keywords for Situations**: Complex problems, strategic analysis, root cause analysis, project planning, systems thinking, diagnostics.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Start with a clear problem definition (Step 1 of the BPS cycle).
2.  Use a logic tree as the canvas for your disaggregation.
3.  Make the first "cleave" by breaking the problem into its highest-level components. This is a crucial creative step. For example, disaggregating the "burden of asthma" not just by overall incidence, but by `incidence` AND `severity` revealed the true crux of the issue in the Western Sydney case.
4.  Continue breaking down the components into smaller, more detailed sub-components until you have isolated discrete, analyzable elements.
5.  Ensure the breakdown is as MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive) as possible to ensure analytical rigor.
6.  Use this detailed breakdown to form hypotheses about where the core of the problem lies, which will guide the prioritization and analysis steps.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "This problem feels too big to solve as is. How can we disaggregate it into smaller pieces?"
*   "What are the fundamental components of this issue? Let's try to map them out."
*   "Is there another way to break this down? What if we cleaved it by geography instead of by product line? Which view is more insightful?"
*   "Have we broken this down far enough to identify the specific drivers we can analyze with data?"

**Category**: Meaning

**Description:**
*Anchoring Bias* is the tendency to rely too heavily on the first piece of information encountered (the “anchor”) when making decisions or estimates. Once an anchor is set — whether relevant or arbitrary — it influences subsequent judgments. People adjust insufficiently from that initial reference point, even when presented with new evidence. Anchors can be numerical (prices, probabilities) or conceptual (first impressions, expectations).

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

* When making decisions under uncertainty or negotiation where early numbers distort perception.
* When the first information received is arbitrary, outdated, or strategically framed.
* When dealing with performance reviews, forecasts, or valuations where past benchmarks bias expectations.
* When priming effects or framing language could manipulate choices (e.g., “Was Gandhi older than 140 when he died?”).

---

**Keywords for Situations:**

* Negotiation and pricing
* Forecasting and budgeting
* Hiring and performance evaluation
* Consumer behaviour and marketing
* Strategic framing and persuasion

---

**Thinking Steps:**

1. **Identify the Anchor** — Detect the first number, label, or assumption shaping your thought.
2. **Pause and Reset** — Mentally detach from the anchor; explicitly note its arbitrary nature.
3. **Reframe the Range** — Generate your own independent estimate or baseline before exposure.
4. **Compare Across Contexts** — Look for multiple reference points to dilute the initial anchor’s weight.
5. **Quantify Adjustment** — Deliberately move farther from the anchor than feels comfortable.
6. **Review Evidence** — Ask what data you would consider valid *if the anchor didn’t exist.*

---

**Coaching Questions:**

* What’s the first number, idea, or assumption that entered this conversation?
* How might it be shaping your expectations right now?
* If you hadn’t heard that figure, what would your independent estimate be?
* How far do you need to adjust to reach a neutral perspective?
* Who benefits most if that anchor stays in place?

  - A system performs well in calm conditions but fails catastrophically under stress
  - Success depends on suppressing volatility, errors, or randomness

  - Confusing antifragility with “resilience” (staying the same) or with reckless risk-taking
  - Maximizing short-term efficiency and removing buffers/redundancy
  - Over-optimizing for predictability and stability (pseudostabilization)
  - Ignoring path dependence and irreversibility (ruin)

### Description

Antifragility is the property of systems that *improve* when exposed to volatility, randomness, and stressors. It is different from robustness/resilience, which resists shocks and stays the same. Antifragility shows up in domains where the unknown dominates: evolution, innovation, learning, and many complex social and economic systems.

In practice, antifragility is about *asymmetric exposure*: when variability can help you more than it can hurt you (and ideally where the maximum harm is bounded).

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When you use “antifragile” as a vibe label instead of specifying the actual payoff asymmetry.
- When the system is safety-critical and experimentation without strict downside caps is unacceptable.
- When you chase volatility for its own sake (confusing positive convexity with thrill-seeking).

---

### Keywords for Situations

Volatility, stressors, redundancy, optionality, convexity/concavity, experimentation, trial-and-error, fat tails, ruin, path dependence.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Name the system and the stressor**
   What entity are you analyzing (body, org, product, portfolio, habit), and what variability shocks it (demand, failures, load, time, competition)?
2. **Test for payoff asymmetry**
   Under more volatility, do you gain more than you lose—or vice versa? Is the downside bounded or can it compound into ruin?
3. **Locate path dependence and irreversibility**
   What breaks “ratchet-style” and doesn’t self-repair? Identify the conditions that create permanent damage.
4. **Subtract fragility first**
   Remove exposures to large downside: leverage, tight coupling, single points of failure, hidden risks, and interventions with iatrogenic effects.
5. **Add convexity (optionality)**
   Prefer positions with limited downside and large potential upside: small bets, multiple trials, and the ability to say “no” later.
6. **Use stressors as information**
   Create safe-to-fail variations (small stresses) that reveal weaknesses early and train adaptation.
7. **Keep redundancy where the tail risk lives**
   Maintain buffers in the parts of the system that fail catastrophically (not everywhere uniformly).
8. **Re-test after changes**
   After each intervention, re-check whether the system became more convex (antifragile) or more concave (fragile).

---

### Coaching Questions

- Where is the downside unbounded or irreversible?
- What would happen if volatility doubled?
- Which “stabilizing” actions might be suppressing useful stressors and fragilizing the system?
- What is one fragility you can remove this week without needing a perfect narrative?
- What small, bounded experiment would create the most learning with the least ruin risk?

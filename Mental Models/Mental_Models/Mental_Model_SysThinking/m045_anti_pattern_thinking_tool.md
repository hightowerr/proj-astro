**Category:** Systems Thinking

## **Description: Core concept explanation**
An Anti-Pattern is a recurring response to a common problem that appears useful at first but leads to ineffective or counter-productive outcomes when repeated. It is the inverse of a design pattern: instead of a proven solution, it captures a tempting shortcut that creates long-term complexity, fragility, or rework.

Anti-patterns matter because they clarify:
- why flawed solutions look attractive in the moment,  
- how they introduce hidden systemic costs, and  
- which better patterns should replace them.  

Classic illustration: **The Golden Hammer** — assuming one familiar tool solves every problem.

Core idea: *short-term relief creates long-term structural cost, forming a recurring failure loop.*

---

## **When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**
- When defaulting to familiar methods without assessing context suitability.  
- When short-term convenience is chosen over long-term sustainability or maintainability.  
- When systems must scale or remain resilient over time.  
- When the same “quick fix” reappears as a repeated problem with new symptoms.  
- When processes or tools are followed dogmatically rather than intentionally (e.g., Cargo Cult practices).

Note: Anti-patterns are context-sensitive — a workaround that is pragmatic in one environment may degrade another.

## **Thinking Steps**
1. **Recognise the recurring response** — Identify solutions that repeatedly surface for this type of problem.  
2. **Evaluate the initial attraction** — Clarify why the approach feels natural or easy.  
3. **Project future consequences** — Consider system behaviour if the approach is scaled or repeated over time.  
4. **Check proven alternatives** — Identify stable patterns or principles that better meet the underlying need.  
5. **Name the anti-pattern** — Create shared language so the team can recognise and communicate it early.  
6. **Refactor or redesign** — Replace the flawed structure with the corresponding better pattern.  
7. **Capture the learning** — Document the insight so that similar future work avoids the same trap.

---

## **Coaching Questions**
- What makes this approach feel comfortable or obvious right now?  
- Have we seen this pattern fail in similar scenarios?  
- What hidden cost or complexity might emerge over time if we repeat this?  
- What would a neutral outsider identify as the real underlying issue?  
- Are we choosing this because it’s effective, or because it’s familiar?  
- Which pattern or principle provides a higher-quality long-term solution?  
- What leading indicators or metrics would signal that this behaviour is becoming an anti-pattern?

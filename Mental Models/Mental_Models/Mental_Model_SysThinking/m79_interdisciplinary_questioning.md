
# Interdisciplinary Questioning

**Description**: A method for analyzing a complex issue by explicitly breaking it down into its constituent domains of thought. For any complex question, one should consider which disciplines or perspectives are relevant (e.g., economic, biological, sociological, cultural, political, ethical, historical) and formulate questions within each domain. This ensures a more comprehensive and less simplistic analysis, preventing one from missing critical dimensions of the problem.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: This model is designed for "wicked problems" or highly complex issues that cross multiple domains. For simpler problems that fall squarely within one domain (e.g., a purely technical bug in code), it would be unnecessary. The approach requires a willingness to engage with fields outside one's own expertise, which can be challenging and requires intellectual humility.

**Keywords for Situations**: Wicked problems, policy analysis, systems thinking, complex social issues, strategic planning, holistic analysis, multidisciplinary collaboration.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  Start with the complex question you are addressing (e.g., "How should we address climate change?").
2.  Brainstorm all the potential domains of thought that are inherent in the question. For example, a question about climate change involves scientific, economic, political, ethical, and sociological domains.
3.  For each relevant domain, formulate specific questions that force you to consider the complexities from that perspective. For example:
    *   *Ethical domain*: "What is our responsibility to future generations?"
    *   *Economic domain*: "What are the costs of inaction versus the costs of intervention?"
    *   *Political domain*: "What are the obstacles to international cooperation?"
4.  Use the answers from each domain to build a multi-faceted, integrated understanding of the issue.
5.  Synthesize the insights from the different domains to arrive at a more robust and well-rounded conclusion or solution, acknowledging the trade-offs between different domain-specific goals.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "We've looked at this from an economic perspective. What are the ethical and political dimensions we might be missing?"
*   "What are all the relevant domains of thought inherent in this complex question? Let's list them out."
*   "Are we sure we are dealing with all the relevant domains? Have we left any important perspectives out, like the historical or cultural context?"
*   "To answer this properly, we need to ask questions from a historical, a sociological, and a psychological point of view. What would those questions be?"

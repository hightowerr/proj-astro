## Description: Core concept explanation
Chaotic systems are sensitive to initial conditions, which gives rise to a phenomenon known as the butterfly effect. This sensitivity was discovered by MIT meteorologist Edward Lorenz who found that tiny discrepancies in starting variables could result in dramatically different outcomes in weather-prediction models. Chaotic systems are deterministic (following rules) but still impossible to predict long-term because modeling outcomes requires perfect understanding of starting conditions. Any inaccuracies result in incorrect predictions, and as we progress into the future, the impact of such deviations is magnified further, making predictions exponentially less accurate. The butterfly effect contradicts many of our assumptions about the world since we tend to assume systems are deterministic and tiny differences shouldn't matter too much, but this is false for chaotic systems.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail
- When seeking precise long-term forecasts: In chaotic systems, we cannot make perfect predictions even if we know the governing rules, because we cannot know the precise initial conditions.
- In linear systems: The butterfly effect applies specifically to chaotic, non-linear systems that are sensitive to initial conditions.
- When the goal is to make detailed predictions: Instead of prediction, chaotic systems require probability-based thinking and preparation for various outcomes.
- When looking for a single cause: Within chaotic systems, no moment is any more significant than any other; every moment changes everything that happens after.

## Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process
1. **Identify if the system is chaotic:** Does the system exhibit sensitivity to initial conditions where tiny differences lead to vastly different outcomes?
2. **Recognize the limits of prediction:** Accept that detailed long-term predictions are impossible in chaotic systems due to sensitivity to initial conditions.
3. **Focus on probability-based thinking:** Instead of trying to predict exact outcomes, consider the likelihood of different scenarios.
4. **Understand system rules vs. predictability:** Even if you know the rules governing a system's behavior, you cannot predict its future behavior precisely.
5. **Prepare for various outcomes:** Design systems that can adapt to multiple possible futures rather than optimizing for a single predicted outcome.
6. **Accept uncertainty:** Work within the reality that chaotic systems will surprise you, even when they follow deterministic rules.

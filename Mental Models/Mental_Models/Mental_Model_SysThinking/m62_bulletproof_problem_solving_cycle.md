
# Bulletproof Problem Solving Cycle

**Description**: A seven-step, iterative framework for creative and systematic problem solving. It provides a structured process to define a problem, disaggregate it into manageable parts, prioritize analysis, conduct the analysis while guarding against bias, and synthesize the findings into a compelling story for action. The process is not strictly linear but a cycle that can be repeated to deepen understanding and refine solutions.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: The full seven-step process may be too rigorous for very simple, low-stakes problems where a quick, intuitive answer suffices. Its power lies in tackling complex, uncertain challenges. The framework's effectiveness depends on completing all the steps; skipping steps, such as proper problem definition or disaggregation, is a common pitfall that leads to poor outcomes.

**Keywords for Situations**: Complex problems, strategic planning, business challenges, policy analysis, personal life decisions, project management, root cause analysis.

**Thinking Steps**:
1.  **Define the Problem**: Create a precise, outcome-focused problem statement. Use a worksheet to clarify the context, boundaries, criteria for success, and required level of accuracy.
2.  **Disaggregate the Problem**: Break the problem down into its component parts using a logic tree. This helps to see the structure of the problem and identify potential solution paths.
3.  **Prioritize Issues**: Use the logic tree to identify the most critical elements—the parts of the problem that will have the most impact if solved. Explicitly decide what *not* to work on to focus effort.
4.  **Develop a Workplan**: Create a plan that outlines the required analyses, assigns responsibilities to team members, and sets timelines for completion.
5.  **Conduct Analysis**: Gather the necessary facts and data to test the hypotheses developed during disaggregation. Actively guard against cognitive biases (e.g., confirmation bias, anchoring).
6.  **Synthesize Findings**: Go beyond just listing results. Weave the analysis together to highlight the core insights and the "so what" of the findings.
7.  **Communicate a Storyline**: Develop a compelling narrative, often using the pyramid principle, that clearly articulates the problem, the analysis, and the recommended solution in a way that persuades the audience to take action.

**Coaching Questions**:
*   "Have we clearly defined the problem we're trying to solve? What does success look like for the decision-maker?"
*   "How can we break this problem down into smaller, more manageable pieces? Let's try to draw the logic tree."
*   "Which part of this problem, if we solved it, would have the biggest impact? Where should we focus our limited time and resources?"
*   "What is our workplan? Who is doing what, and by when, to test our hypotheses?"
*   "What is the simplest analysis we can do to test this? What biases might be affecting our thinking here?"
*   "What is the single most important insight from our analysis? What is the story we are trying to tell?"
*   "How can we communicate our findings in a way that is compelling and leads to action?"

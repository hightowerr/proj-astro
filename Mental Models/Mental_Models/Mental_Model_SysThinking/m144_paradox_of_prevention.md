### Description

The paradox of prevention is when preventing a targeted set of diseases/harms reduces costs in that category but increases total long-run system costs because people live longer and eventually incur other (often higher) costs. The system doesn’t “cash out” the savings; it reallocates them across time and categories.

---

### When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)

- When prevention truly eliminates downstream costs (rare; verify).
- When you only care about wellbeing, not budgets (don’t over-index on “saves money” claims).

---

### Keywords for Situations

- Public health; longevity; healthcare budgets; safety; downstream costs; demographic shifts.

---

### Thinking Steps

1. **Define the targeted harm being prevented and its short-run costs.**
2. **Model lifespan/demographic changes induced by prevention.**
3. **Identify downstream cost categories likely to increase (elder care, chronic disease).**
4. **Evaluate outcomes on a long time horizon and system-wide budget.**
5. **Separate “good outcomes” (health) from “budget outcomes” (spend).**

---

### Coaching Questions

- What costs disappear, and what costs shift later in life?
- Are we optimizing a line item or total system cost?
- What time horizon makes the “savings” claim true or false?

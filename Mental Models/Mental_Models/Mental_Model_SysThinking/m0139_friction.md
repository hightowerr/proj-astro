**Description: Core concept explanation**
Friction is any force or process that bleeds energy from a system, requiring continual input just to maintain motion; remove unnecessary friction and efficiency rises, add targeted friction and behavior can be shaped. Think of pushing a hockey puck through grass (high friction) versus ice (low friction): the same effort travels far further when friction is reduced. In organizations, “friction” shows up as extra steps, time, confusion, or cognitive switching that saps throughput; systematically identifying and trimming it compounds gains over time. Conversely, a pinch of deliberate friction (e.g., asking for a receipt on returns) can deter abuse without nuking trust. 

Friction also operates at market scale. “Market friction” is how hard it is for customers to switch; low-friction arenas (coffee, fashion) punish missteps fast and trigger apologies, while high-friction arenas (banks, telecoms) see fewer defections and fewer cultural apologies. 

In retail, outcomes hinge on the friction–reward balance: experiences win by either lowering purchase friction or raising customer reward (or both). Mapping scenarios with a simple index (Friction – Reward = FR) reveals where to intervene first. 

Platforms succeed by stripping friction from connections and interactions between producers and users (trust, transparency, onboarding), whereas pipelines often embed opaque, high-friction handoffs. Shifting from pipeline to platform reframes your job as facilitating low-friction value exchange. 

**Category:** Systems Thinking

---

**- When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail**

* When “zero friction” harms safety, privacy, or quality (e.g., removing verification in critical processes). Some frictions are protective guardrails. 
* When added friction backfires by degrading trust or reputation (e.g., making refunds onerous); small, well-placed speed bumps beat roadblocks. 
* When automation magnifies hidden defects—removing human “friction” in oversight without robust testing can multiply errors at scale (“paradox of automation”). 
* When you ignore context: market switching costs (external friction) can dominate customer behavior more than your internal UX polish. Misread this and you’ll ship the wrong fix. 
* When you equate speed with value and miss reward. The FR lens insists on tuning both sides of the equation; low friction with low reward still under-delivers.

**- Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively**

1. **Define the journey & outcome.** Map the end-to-end flow (user, employee, data) and the specific behavior or metric you want to shift. Note where energy/time is lost. (Use process maps and real-world observation, not assumptions.) 
2. **Inventory frictions.** List concrete barriers: time, distance, steps, forms, unclear copy, context switches, layout, delivery, trust signals missing, etc. Include external market frictions (switching costs). 
3. **Score FR (Friction–Reward).** For key scenarios/segments, estimate friction vs. perceived reward; target scenarios with high FR (bad) or where small changes could flip FR negative (good). Build a simple FR matrix for you and competitors. 
4. **Decide: remove or add.**
   * **Remove** wasteful steps (SOPs, clearer layouts, one-click or prefill, batching to reduce cognitive switching, local/mobile access). 
   * **Add** micro-friction as a nudge where misuse is costly (e.g., extra confirmation for high-risk actions, minimal proof for refunds). Right-size the bump. 
5. **Platform lens (if applicable).** If you mediate interactions, lower multi-sided frictions: trustworthy ratings, safe payments, simple producer onboarding, transparent policies. Shift from pipeline push to facilitation. 
6. **Pilot safely.** Where removing friction also removes checks, counterbalance with automation + monitoring + sampling to avoid the automation paradox. Test before scaling. 
7. **Measure & iterate.** Track dropout, time-to-complete, NPS/CSAT, conversion, complaints, and operational errors. Use service recoveries/apologies data to spot hidden frictions—especially in low-friction markets where churn is swift. 

---

**- Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model**

* Where do users or staff spend time waiting, re-entering data, or switching context—and what one change would cut that in half? 
* If you had to deliver the same outcome with **one** tap/click/step, what must change in copy, layout, or policy? What risks would that introduce? 
* Which single policy adds “good friction” that prevents 80% of costly errors or abuse—and is it right-sized? 
* In your market, is churn governed more by **experience friction** or **switching friction**? How does that change your apology/service-recovery strategy? 
* For your top three customer need-states, what are the FR scores today, and what specific moves would flip at least one to negative (reward > friction)?

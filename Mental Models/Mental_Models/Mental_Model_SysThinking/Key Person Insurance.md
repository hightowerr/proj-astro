**Description: Core concept explanation**  
Key Person Insurance is a risk management strategy where a business insures against the financial loss resulting from the death, critical illness, or long-term incapacity of a key individual whose skills, knowledge, or leadership are crucial to the business’s profitability and continuity. It treats human capital as a critical asset, similar to physical assets, and uses insurance mechanisms (e.g., life assurance, income protection, critical illness cover) to provide financial stability during disruption. This model recognizes that certain individuals disproportionately influence organizational resilience and that their absence can trigger cascading financial, operational, and strategic consequences.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**  
- When the individual’s contribution is not quantifiable or does not significantly impact profits.  
- In businesses with deep benches of talent or where roles are easily replaceable.  
- If the cost of premiums outweighs the potential financial risk.  
- When the key person is also a major shareholder, as tax and ownership complications may arise.  
- In cases where the business lacks clear succession planning or dependency on a single person is being reinforced rather than mitigated.

---

**Keywords for Situations:**  
Business continuity planning, dependency on key individuals, financial risk mitigation, shareholder agreements, partnership protection, loan guarantees, succession planning, talent retention, profit volatility, operational stability.

---

**Thinking Steps:**  
1. **Identify Key People:** Determine whose absence would cause significant financial or operational harm. Consider founders, top salespeople, lead engineers, or primary decision-makers.  
2. **Assess Financial Impact:** Quantify potential losses using methods like:  
   - *Multiple of salary* (e.g., up to 2x annual salary)  
   - *Proportion of profits* (e.g., key person’s contribution to gross profit)  
   - *Cost of replacement* (recruitment fees, training, interim cover)  
3. **Select Appropriate Cover:**  
   - *Term assurance* for temporary needs  
   - *Whole-of-life policies* for long-term importance  
   - *Income protection or critical illness cover* for medium-term risks  
4. **Structure the Policy:** Ensure it is written on a *“life of another”* basis, with the company as the proposer, owner, and beneficiary.  
5. **Review Legal and Tax Implications:** Confirm that premiums are tax-deductible and benefits are structured to meet HMRC criteria (e.g., short-term cover for loss of profits).  
6. **Integrate with Broader Business Protections:** Align with share protection, partnership agreements, or loan cover to create a comprehensive safety net.

---

**Coaching Questions:**  
- Who in your business would be hardest to replace, and what would be the immediate financial impact of their absence?  
- How do you currently quantify the value of key people beyond their salary?  
- What existing business agreements (e.g., articles of association, partnership deeds) should inform your insurance structure?  
- Have you considered how bank financing or client contracts might be affected by the loss of a key person?  
- How would insurance proceeds be used—to recruit, cover lost profits, or pay off debts?

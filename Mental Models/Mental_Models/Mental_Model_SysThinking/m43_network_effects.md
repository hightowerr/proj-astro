## Description: Core concept explanation
Network effects occur when the utility of a product or service increases as more people use it. More users means more value for all users. An obvious example is the telephone: if you own one but none of your friends do, it's not much use, but with each additional friend who gets one, the utility increases. Network effects may also be indirect, as when a group of people using something for one purpose generates value for a group using it for a different purpose. For instance, more drivers joining a ride-sharing app means more utility for riders. Network effects set off a reinforcing feedback loop wherein the added value attracts new users, who in turn create more users. The single most important factor behind the rise of many of the most significant technologies over the past two centuries is network effects.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail
- When network effects become negative: More users can sometimes reduce value through congestion, overload, or changing the fundamental nature of the offering.
- For products with significant inherent quality differences: Superior products may still win even without network effects.
- When the market is still forming: Early markets may not yet have reached the critical mass needed for network effects to take hold.
- For products with high switching costs: Users may stay even when better alternatives exist due to the costs of switching.

## Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process
1. **Determine if network effects apply:** Does the value of the product or service increase as more people use it?
2. **Identify direct vs. indirect effects:** Are the network effects direct (more users make it better for all users) or indirect (more users attract suppliers or complementary services)?
3. **Assess the critical mass:** How many users are needed before the network effects start to take hold and create a reinforcing loop?
4. **Consider the competitive advantage:** Once network effects take hold, how difficult will it be for competitors to overcome this advantage?
5. **Plan for early adoption:** Since getting network effects started can be difficult, what strategies will attract the early users needed?
6. **Watch for negative effects:** As the user base grows, what might cause the value to decrease rather than increase?

## Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts
- Does the value of our product or service increase as more people use it?
- Are we seeing direct network effects or indirect network effects?
- How many users do we need to reach critical mass for the network effects to take hold?
- What is our strategy for attracting early users before network effects kick in?
- Are users reluctant to switch because of network effects or other factors?
- How might negative network effects set in as we grow?
- What are competitors doing regarding network effects in this market?

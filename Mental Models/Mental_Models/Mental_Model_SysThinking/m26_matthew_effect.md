# Matthew Effect

**Description:**
The Matthew Effect describes how accumulated advantage helps the advantaged advance further and the disadvantaged fall further behind. Named from a verse in the Gospel of Matthew, it captures how initial advantages compound over time to create larger disparities, even when the original advantages were small. This principle explains why early success often creates conditions for continued success, and why small initial differences can lead to large outcomes over time.

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**
- When underestimating the impact of small initial advantages or disadvantages
- When expecting equal opportunities to produce equal outcomes without intervention
- When not recognizing how small initial differences can compound over time
- When failing to address systemic advantages that perpetuate inequality
- When assuming that merit alone determines long-term outcomes

**Keywords for Situations:**
Educational achievement, career advancement, wealth accumulation, social mobility, economic inequality, network effects, cumulative advantage, success, opportunity.

**Thinking Steps:**
1. **Identify Initial Advantages:** Recognize how small initial differences can compound over time.
2. **Look for Cumulative Mechanisms:** Understand how advantages build upon themselves.
3. **Consider Equalization:** Evaluate what interventions might help level playing fields.
4. **Focus on Early Stages:** Recognize the importance of early investment and support.
5. **Address Systemic Issues:** Consider how systems can be changed to reduce Matthew Effect.
6. **Build on Strengths:** Leverage existing advantages to create more advantage.

**Coaching Questions:**
* "How might small initial advantages compound over time in this situation?"
* "What can I do to create positive feedback loops for success?"
* "How can I address systemic advantages that compound over time?"
* "What initial investments could have disproportionate long-term returns?"
* "Am I recognizing and leveraging existing advantages effectively?"
* "How might I help level the playing field for others?"

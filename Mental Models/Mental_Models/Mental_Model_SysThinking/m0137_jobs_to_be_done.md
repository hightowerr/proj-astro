**Category:** Systems Thinking

**Description: Core concept explanation**  
Jobs To Be Done (JTBD) is a way to understand customer motivation as a **struggle to make progress**. People “hire” products or services to help them move from their current situation toward a better life; the product is only a means, not the goal. A JTBD is _done_ when the solution overcomes the struggle and delivers that progress. Rather than fixating on features or demographics, JTBD investigates the circumstances, desired progress, and switching behavior across a whole system of solutions that might compete to do the job.  

JTBD statements often follow a concise “Job Story” structure: **When… (situation), I want to… (motivation), so I can… (expected result)**—useful for aligning teams on the real outcome users seek.  

Four “progress-making forces” shape adoption: **push** of current problems, **pull** of a promising solution, **anxieties** about switching, and **inertia/habits** that favor the status quo. Designing experiences that amplify push/pull while reducing anxiety and inertia helps customers successfully “hire” your solution.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail**

- **Confusing tasks with jobs.** If you describe actions or product functions (the _how/what_), you’re likely modeling a solution, not the underlying _why_. Keep the focus on progress, not features.
    
- **Over-segmenting job “types.”** Fragmenting into too many job categories (e.g., functional vs. emotional vs. social) can create overlaps and contradictions; treat all jobs as fundamentally emotional drives for progress.
    
- **Relying on demographics or opinions.** Demographic profiles and stated preferences can mislead; JTBD works best with _revealed_ behavior (switching, compensating workarounds) in real contexts.
    
- **Ignoring system dynamics.** Jobs and solutions live in a system (users, choosers, payers, influencers). Optimizing a part (a feature) without considering the whole (workflows, stakeholders) can backfire.
    
- **Skipping direct interviews.** Analytics alone can’t uncover struggles, anxieties, or desired outcomes; you need qualitative interviews about what people actually did and considered.
    

---

**Keywords for Situations: Common contexts or triggers where it applies**

- Switching from one solution to another; _compensatory behaviors_ or DIY workarounds (e.g., hacks, spreadsheets).
- Friction in onboarding or adoption; anxiety about trying a new tool or process.
- Conflicting “requirements” across stakeholders (users vs. buyers vs. execs).
- Customer research, discovery, and value proposition design; “job openings” created by new life circumstances.
- Prioritizing roadmaps by impact on **push**, **pull**, **anxiety**, **inertia**.
    

---

**Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively**

1. **Spot the struggle.** Collect concrete episodes where people struggled and sought change; listen for switching, cobbled solutions, or repeated complaints. Prioritize real events over hypotheticals.
2. **Map the circumstances.** Capture context (who/when/where), constraints, and triggers that created the “job opening.” Avoid demographic shortcuts.
3. **Elicit desired progress.** Ask how life would be better if the problem were solved—emotionally and practically. Draft Job Stories: _When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [outcome]._
4. **Analyze the four forces.** For the considered options (past, present, alternatives), articulate the **push**, **pull**, **anxiety**, and **inertia**. Design to strengthen push/pull and reduce anxieties/habits (e.g., trials, refunds, guidance).
5. **Widen the competitive set.** Include any substitute that could deliver the same progress (including doing nothing, policies, or services). Identify what customers will **stop doing** if they hire you.
6. **Model the system.** Identify users, buyers, approvers, and beneficiaries; ensure the solution delivers progress across the stakeholder system, not just to end-users.
7. **Prototype around moments.** Target the key moments of struggle with experience changes (onboarding, cues, safeguards) that make progress obvious and risk small.
8. **Validate with evidence.** Seek _revealed preferences_: switching behavior, reduced compensations, faster time-to-value. Iterate Job Stories and force maps accordingly.
    

---

**Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model**

- **Struggle:** “Tell me about the last time this was hard—what did you try, and what broke down?”
    
- **Circumstances:** “What was happening in your life/work that made _now_ the moment to look for a change?”
    
- **Desired progress:** “If this worked perfectly, how would your life feel or look different next week/month?”
    
- **Four forces:** “What pulled you toward our solution? What worried you? What habits did you have to overcome?”
    
- **Competition/system:** “What would you stop doing if you used this? Who else needs to see progress for this to stick?”
    
- **Job Story check:** “Can we rephrase this as: _When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [result]_—and is it solution-agnostic?”

# Five Whys

**Description: Core concept explanation**
The Five Whys is a simple, disciplined root-cause analysis method: start with a clear problem statement, then ask “Why?” repeatedly (about five times) to move past symptoms and uncover the underlying cause(s). Popularized within the Toyota Production System, it’s often paired with tools like fishbone (Ishikawa) diagrams and Pareto analysis to structure causes, prioritize, and prevent recurrence. The core power is its insistence on digging beyond the proximate cause to the true, fixable root cause. 

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution):**

* When problems are complex, socio-technical, or multi-causal and require quantitative analysis or multiple lines of evidence—5 Whys can oversimplify without supporting data. 
* When participants lack psychological safety or honesty—answers will stall at superficial causes. 
* When “five” is treated as a rigid rule—stop when evidence runs out or causes converge, not at a number. 
* When used without validation—each “why” should be checked against observations, tests, or data to avoid chasing anecdotes. 

**Keywords for Situations:**

* Incident/post-mortem reviews; outages; quality defects; safety events; missed OKRs; launch failures; recurring customer complaints; handoff failures; process bottlenecks; equipment downtime. 

**Thinking Steps: Step-by-step reasoning process to apply the model effectively**

1. **Frame the problem crisply.** Write a succinct, observable problem statement (what/where/when/impact). 
2. **Gather the right people.** Include those closest to the work plus a facilitator; foster candor and a blameless tone. 
3. **Map possible causes (optional but helpful).** Use a fishbone to cluster candidate causes (people, process, tools, environment, etc.) before drilling down. 
4. **Ask “Why?” iteratively.** Starting from the stated problem, ask “Why did this happen?” Capture the first, honest answer; then ask “Why is that true?” Repeat until you reach a cause you can act on and verify. Don’t force exactly five; go deeper or stop sooner as appropriate. 
5. **Validate each link.** Corroborate with data, observation (gemba), or experiments; distinguish proximate from root causes. 
6. **Prioritize with impact.** If multiple roots appear, use voting/Pareto to identify the highest-leverage cause(s). 
7. **Define countermeasures.** Convert roots into specific, owner-assigned actions (e.g., procedure changes, training, design fixes), avoiding mere symptom patches. 
8. **Follow through and learn.** Monitor outcomes, capture lessons learned, and adjust processes to prevent recurrence. 

**Coaching Questions: Reflective prompts to help someone use or practice this model**

* Is our problem statement concrete and observable (not a diagnosis in disguise)? 
* What evidence supports each “why” answer—and what would disconfirm it? 
* Are we stopping at a convenient proximate cause (e.g., “human error”) instead of a systemic one? 
* Which stakeholders closest to the work have we not yet heard from? 
* If multiple root causes surfaced, which one, if fixed, would prevent most recurrence (80/20)? 
* What lasting change (policy, design, automation, training, feedback loop) will eliminate the cause—not just treat the symptom?

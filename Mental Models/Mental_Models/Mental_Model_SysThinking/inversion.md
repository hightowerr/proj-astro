---
model: Inversion
category: Thinking Framework
detectors: Problems that seem unsolvable from a forward perspective, situations where you want to avoid failure, decisions with significant consequences
triggers: When facing complex problems, making important decisions, planning for success, risk assessment
failure_modes: Applying inversion only superficially without deep analysis, failing to consider all potential failure paths, stopping at the first answer instead of exploring multiple angles
aliases: Invert, Always Invert, Think Backwards, Negative Visualization, Avoiding Problems
core_mechanics: Reversing the typical problem-solving approach to identify what would cause failure, then avoiding those elements
---
**Description**: Inversion is a powerful thinking tool that involves approaching problems from the opposite direction. Instead of asking "How can I succeed?" you ask "How can I fail?" and then systematically avoid the failure pathways. This approach was famously advocated by Charlie Munger, who credited the great algebraist Jacobi with the phrase "Invert, always invert." The mental model leverages the fact that many hard problems are best solved when addressed backward, focusing on eliminating what doesn't work rather than optimizing what might work.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)**: When dealing with simple, straightforward problems that have clear solutions; when time constraints don't allow for thorough backward analysis; when the problem space is too narrow to benefit from perspective shifts; when working with people who are resistant to unconventional thinking approaches.

---

**Keywords for Situations**: Risk management, strategic planning, decision making, quality control, process improvement, investment analysis, career planning, relationship building, business strategy, product development, personal goal setting, troubleshooting, root cause analysis.

---

**Thinking Steps**: 
1. Clearly define the desired outcome or goal you're working toward
2. Explicitly state the opposite of your goal (what would constitute failure)
3. Identify all the ways you could fail to achieve your goal
4. List the specific actions, behaviors, or conditions that would lead to failure
5. Rank these failure modes by likelihood and impact
6. Develop strategies to avoid each identified failure pathway
7. Create systems and checks to prevent the failure conditions from occurring
8. Regularly review and update your inversion analysis as circumstances change
9. Consider second and third-order consequences of potential failure modes
10. Use the insights gained to strengthen your primary approach to achieving the goal

---

**Coaching Questions**: 
- What would be the surest way to fail at achieving this goal?
- If you were trying to sabotage your own success, what would you do?
- What are the most common ways people fail in this particular situation?
- What blind spots might be preventing you from seeing potential pitfalls?
- What would happen if you focused all your energy on avoiding the worst outcomes?
- What do you need to stop doing to improve your chances of success?
- How might your current approach backfire or create unintended consequences?
- What would someone who disagrees with your approach say about potential risks?
- What are the systemic factors that could undermine your efforts?
- If you imagine yourself six months from now having failed, what would you identify as the key reasons?

# Customer Value Chain

## Description
The Customer Value Chain (CVC) is composed of the discrete steps a typical customer follows in order to select, buy, and consume a product or service. It represents the series of activities that customers perform in order to fulfill their needs and wants, including searching for, evaluating, purchasing, using, and disposing of products. CVCs vary according to the specifics of a business, industry, or product. For example, the key stages in a CVC for purchasing a flat-screen TV involve going to a retailer, evaluating the options available, choosing one, purchasing it, and then using the TV at home. The CVC is analogous to Michael Porter's value chain but reflects the customer's vantage point rather than the company's.

## When to Avoid (or Use with Caution)
The CVC framework should be used with caution when dealing with highly emotional or impulse purchases that don't follow a linear sequence of activities. It may be less applicable in B2B contexts where multiple stakeholders have different value chains within the same organization. Also avoid overcomplicating the CVC with too many granular activities that obscure the main value creation points. Use caution when applying CVC to markets where the customer behavior is still evolving or not well understood.

## Thinking Steps
1. Identify your target customer segment and their specific needs and wants
2. Map out all the activities customers typically perform when acquiring your product or service
3. Break down each major activity into smaller sub-activities for complete understanding
4. Determine which activities create value for the customer, which capture value for your business, and which erode value for the customer
5. Assess the costs (monetary, time, and effort) customers incur at each stage of the CVC
6. Identify where customers experience the most friction or pain points in the process
7. Look for opportunities to eliminate value-eroding activities or reduce their impact
8. Consider how technology or process innovations could improve the customer experience at each stage
9. Analyze competitors' approaches to each CVC stage to identify differentiation opportunities
10. Continuously monitor and update the CVC as customer behaviors and market conditions evolve

## Coaching Questions
- What are all the steps your customers take from recognizing a need to disposing of your product or service?
- At which stage of the value chain do customers experience the most frustration or difficulty?
- How do customers in different segments experience the value chain differently?
- What activities in your current CVC create the most value for customers versus cost for them?
- How might your customers' value chain activities change in the future due to technology or behavior shifts?
- Where do customers currently go to other companies for activities that could be part of your CVC?
- What would happen if you eliminated one of the activities in your customer's value chain?
- How do customers currently compare your value chain experience to your competitors'?
- Which activities in your CVC could be performed more efficiently or effectively by others?
- How might you reconfigure the customer value chain to create more value for customers?

**Category:** Systems Thinking

**Description: Core concept explanation**  
A Post-Implementation Review (PIR) is a structured evaluation conducted after a project, policy, or system has been in operation long enough to yield stable outcomes. The model compares intended objectives with actual results, assesses benefit realization, analyses variances, surfaces systemic patterns, and captures lessons to improve future initiatives. It treats implementation as one loop in a broader learning cycle: outcomes → reflection → adaptation → improved execution.

---

**When to Avoid (or Use with Caution): Situations where the model may mislead or fail**
- Conducting the review before the solution reaches steady-state performance  
- Lacking baseline data or clear success criteria  
- Operating in blame-oriented environments where honest feedback is unsafe  
- When the review would consume scarce resources needed for ongoing operations  
- When rapid environmental shifts make comparison to the original plan low-value  
- When findings lack executive sponsorship or follow-through mechanisms  

---

**Keywords for Situations**
- Project go-live or completion  
- System or software deployment  
- Policy or regulatory rollout  
- Change management initiatives  
- Post-merger integration  
- Cost-benefit verification  
- Benefit realization tracking  
- Operational efficiency assessments  
- Governance and risk evaluation  
- Stakeholder satisfaction checks  
- Process standardization  
- Compliance effectiveness  

---

**Thinking Steps**
1. **Define Scope and Timeline**  
   Establish what is being reviewed, the review period, and required stakeholders.

2. **Retrieve Original Objectives and Success Criteria**  
   Gather business case, requirements, baselines, assumptions, and planned outcomes.

3. **Collect Comprehensive Data**  
   Assemble quantitative metrics (cost, schedule, quality) and qualitative insights (interviews, observations, user feedback).

4. **Assess Performance Against Goals**  
   Compare actual vs planned outcomes across cost, schedule, scope, quality, benefits, and risk management.

5. **Analyze Systemic Patterns**  
   Identify recurring causes, broken assumptions, process gaps, communication failures, or external influences.

6. **Identify Lessons and Replicable Practices**  
   Separate context-specific findings from reusable lessons that should influence future initiatives.

7. **Develop Actionable Recommendations**  
   Convert insights into prioritized, owned, and time-bound improvements.

8. **Communicate and Operationalize Findings**  
   Share results, embed lessons into processes, and ensure follow-through via tracking and accountability.

---

**Coaching Questions**
- What outcomes were intended, what occurred, and what explains the gap?  
- If we re-ran the project today, what would we change at each phase?  
- Which original assumptions proved incorrect, and what early signals did we miss?  
- Who can provide the most candid feedback, and how do we create safety for that?  
- What capability or process gaps became visible, and which matter most going forward?  
- What three lessons would most improve our next initiative, and why?  
- Who must adopt these lessons for them to influence future behaviour, and how will that transfer occur?  
- Which metrics from this implementation should be monitored as leading indicators?  
- What exceeded expectations, and what conditions enabled that success?  
- What obstacles might block the adoption of recommended improvements?  
- How will we verify in six months that lessons were actually applied?
